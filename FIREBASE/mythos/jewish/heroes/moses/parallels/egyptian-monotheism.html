Akhenaten and Yahwism

Egyptian Monotheism, Moses, and the Question of Aten's Influence on Israelite Religion

Introduction: The Heretic Pharaoh

Akhenaten (Amenhotep IV, r. c. 1353-1336 BCE), the "heretic pharaoh," instituted the ancient world's first known monotheistic reform. He abolished the traditional Egyptian pantheon, closed the temples of Amun-Ra and other gods, and declared the Aten (sun disk) as the sole deity. His radical religious revolution lasted barely a generation—after his death, Egypt violently restored polytheism and erased his name from monuments.

Scholars have long debated: Did Moses, educated in Pharaoh's palace, encounter Akhenaten's monotheism? Did Atenism influence Yahwism? Or are the similarities coincidental? The question touches the origins of biblical monotheism itself.

Akhenaten: The Aten Revolution

Amenhotep IV Becomes Akhenaten

c. 1353 BCE:
Amenhotep IV ascends throne as Pharaoh of Egypt (18th Dynasty, New Kingdom)
Year 5 (c. 1348 BCE):
Declares Aten (sun disk) supreme deity; changes name to Akhenaten ("Effective for Aten"); begins closing temples of Amun-Ra and other gods
Year 8 (c. 1345 BCE):
Moves capital from Thebes to newly built Akhetaten (modern Amarna), "Horizon of Aten"
c. 1345-1336 BCE:
Amarna Period: Exclusive worship of Aten enforced; persecution of Amun priesthood; defacement of Amun's name on monuments; composition of Great Hymn to Aten
c. 1336 BCE:
Akhenaten dies; succeeded by Tutankhaten (later Tutankhamun)
c. 1334-1323 BCE:
Tutankhamun restores traditional polytheism, changes name to Tutankhamun ("Living Image of Amun"), abandons Akhetaten, returns to Thebes
c. 1319 BCE onward:
Horemheb (pharaoh) systematically erases Akhenaten's memory: defaces monuments, destroys Akhetaten, removes Akhenaten from king lists as illegitimate "heretic"

Key Features of Atenism

  • Monotheism (or Monolatry?): Aten declared sole god; other deities forbidden
  • Solar Theology: Aten = visible sun disk (not anthropomorphic god)
  • Creator God: Aten creates and sustains all life through sunlight
  • Universal Deity: Aten gives life to all peoples and lands, not just Egypt
  • Rejection of Mythology: No myths, no afterlife journey, no Osiris judgment—radical break from Egyptian tradition
  • Pharaoh as Sole Intermediary: Only Akhenaten and his family worship Aten directly; people worship through Akhenaten
  • Iconoclasm: Images of other gods defaced; Amun's name chiseled off monuments

The Great Hymn to Aten: Parallels to Psalm 104

The Great Hymn to Aten (c. 1340 BCE)

Inscribed on the tomb wall of Ay (Akhenaten's vizier) at Amarna. Attributed to Akhenaten himself.

Creation and Life-Giving:
"O sole god, like whom there is no other!
Thou didst create the world according to thy desire,
Whilst thou wert alone:
All men, cattle, and wild beasts,
Whatever is on earth, going upon its feet,
And whatsoever is on high, flying with its wings."
Light and Darkness:
"When thou settest in the western horizon,
The land is in darkness, in the manner of death...
When thou risest on the eastern horizon,
Thou fillest every land with thy beauty."
Universal Provider:
"How manifold are thy works!
They are hidden from the face of man,
O sole god, like whom there is no other!
Thou didst create the earth according to thy desire,
Whilst thou wert alone."
Foreign Lands:
"The countries of Syria and Nubia, the land of Egypt,
Thou settest every man in his place,
Thou suppliest their necessities:
Everyone has his food, and his time of life is reckoned.
Their tongues are separate in speech,
And their natures as well;
Their skins are distinguished,
As thou distinguishest the foreign peoples."

Psalm 104 (Date Uncertain, Possibly 10th-6th Century BCE)

Creation and Majesty:
"O LORD my God, you are very great!
You are clothed with splendor and majesty,
covering yourself with light as with a garment,
stretching out the heavens like a tent." (Ps 104:1-2)
Animals and Provision:
"The young lions roar for their prey,
seeking their food from God.
When the sun rises, they steal away
and lie down in their dens.
Man goes out to his work
and to his labor until the evening." (Ps 104:21-23)
Ships and Leviathan:
"Yonder is the sea, great and wide,
which teems with creatures innumerable,
living things both small and great.
There go the ships,
and Leviathan, which you formed to play in it." (Ps 104:25-26)
Universal Dependence on God:
"These all look to you,
to give them their food in due season.
When you give it to them, they gather it up;
when you open your hand, they are filled with good things.
When you hide your face, they are dismayed;
when you take away their breath, they die and return to their dust." (Ps 104:27-29)
Manifold Works:
"O LORD, how manifold are your works!
In wisdom have you made them all;
the earth is full of your creatures." (Ps 104:24)

Scholarly Debate on Psalm 104 and Hymn to Aten

Theory 1: Direct Literary Borrowing

Some scholars (minority view) argue Psalm 104 directly borrows from or adapts the Great Hymn to Aten.

  • Evidence: Specific phrases, imagery, and structure parallel too closely to be coincidental
  • Mechanism: Egyptian texts available in royal archives; Israelite scribes had access
  • Timing: If Psalm 104 dates to monarchic period (10th-6th cent. BCE), Egyptian influence plausible
  • Problems:
    • Dating Psalm 104 uncertain (could be pre- or post-exilic)
    • Hymn to Aten largely lost to history after Akhenaten's erasure; how would Israelites access it?
    • Parallels could reflect shared ancient Near Eastern literary conventions, not direct borrowing

Theory 2: Common Ancient Near Eastern Literary Tradition

Most scholars favor this view: Both texts draw on shared ANE hymnic traditions praising creator deities.

  • Evidence: Other ANE creation hymns (Babylonian, Ugaritic) share similar themes
  • Explanation: Common literary stock—sun/light imagery, animals, provision, etc.
  • Independence: Psalm 104 and Hymn to Aten independently utilize conventions of creation theology
  • Conclusion: Parallels reflect genre (creation hymn), not direct dependence

Theory 3: Indirect Influence via Canaanite/Phoenician Intermediaries

Egyptian solar theology influenced Canaanite literature; Israel borrowed from Canaanite sources, not directly from Egypt.

  • Egyptian ideas filtered through Levantine cultures
  • Israel adapted Canaanite hymns, which themselves reflected Egyptian influence
  • Explains parallels without requiring direct Egyptian contact

Moses and Akhenaten: The Controversial Theory

Sigmund Freud: Moses and Monotheism (1939)

Freud's Radical Thesis:

  • Moses was not a Hebrew but an Egyptian priest or noble in Akhenaten's court
  • After Akhenaten's death and Atenism's suppression, Moses fled Egypt with a group of followers (Habiru/Hebrews?)
  • Moses taught these Semitic slaves Akhenaten's monotheism, reframed as worship of Yahweh
  • Israelite monotheism = continuation of Atenism in new cultural form
  • Moses murdered by his followers in wilderness; later tradition sanitized his death

Freud's "Evidence":

  • Moses's name is Egyptian (from ms = "son," as in Ra-mses, Thut-mose)
  • Chronological proximity: Akhenaten (c. 1353-1336 BCE) and traditional Moses date (c. 1300-1200 BCE) overlap
  • Both promote exclusive monotheism in polytheistic context
  • Circumcision practiced by Egyptians, adopted by Israelites

Scholarly Reception:

  • Rejected by mainstream biblical scholarship: Lacks textual or archaeological support
  • Psychoanalytic speculation: Based on Freud's theories (Oedipus complex, primal patricide) rather than historical evidence
  • Chronological problems: Gap between Akhenaten and traditional Moses date too large for direct influence
  • Theological differences: Atenism and Yahwism differ fundamentally (see below)
  • Interesting thought experiment: Raised questions about Egyptian influence on Israelite monotheism

Key Differences: Atenism vs. Yahwism

1. Nature of Deity

Aten Yahweh
Visible sun disk, material object Invisible, transcendent, no physical form
Identified with natural phenomenon (sun) Creator of sun, distinct from creation
Impersonal life-force Personal God who speaks, covenants, acts in history
No mythology or narrative Rich narrative: creation, covenant, Exodus, etc.

2. Worship and Access

Atenism Yahwism
Only Akhenaten (and family) worship Aten directly All Israelites have covenantal relationship with Yahweh
People worship Akhenaten, who mediates to Aten Moses mediates Law, but all worship Yahweh
Pharaoh = divine son of Aten Moses = human prophet; Israel = God's adopted son (Ex 4:22)
No priesthood (Akhenaten monopolizes worship) Levitical priesthood mediates sacrifices

3. Ethics and Law

Atenism Yahwism
No ethical code or moral law associated with Aten Torah: comprehensive ethical, ritual, social law
Aesthetic/naturalistic focus (beauty of creation) Covenantal/ethical focus (justice, righteousness)
No concept of sin against Aten Sin = covenant violation; atonement system central

4. Afterlife and Eschatology

Atenism Yahwism
Rejected traditional afterlife (no Osiris, no Duat, no Field of Reeds) Sheol (later resurrection hope in Daniel 12:2, etc.)
No judgment after death God judges individuals and nations
Focus on this-worldly existence under Aten's light Covenantal blessings/curses in this life, with eschatological hope

5. Iconography

Atenism Yahwism
Aten depicted as sun disk with rays ending in hands "You shall not make for yourself a carved image" (Ex 20:4) — strict aniconism
Royal family worshiping Aten shown in art No images of God permitted

6. Exclusivity and Tolerance

Atenism Yahwism
Intolerant: Defaced Amun's name, closed temples, persecuted priesthood Intolerant of idolatry within Israel; "You shall have no other gods before me" (Ex 20:3)
Imposed by royal decree, enforced top-down Covenantal relationship; people repeatedly choose/reject Yahweh
Collapsed immediately after Akhenaten's death—no popular support Endured despite conquest, exile, persecution—deep popular/theological roots

Could Moses Have Encountered Atenism?

Chronological Considerations

Traditional Exodus Dating:

  • Early Date (c. 1446 BCE): Based on 1 Kings 6:1 ("480th year after Exodus" = 966 BCE + 480 = 1446 BCE). This places Exodus before Akhenaten—no possibility of influence.
  • Late Date (c. 1270-1250 BCE): Based on Merneptah Stele (1210 BCE mentions "Israel"), archaeological evidence of Canaanite conquest. This places Exodus after Akhenaten (d. 1336 BCE), but only by 60-80 years.

Possibility of Indirect Influence (Late Date):

  • If Moses born c. 1320 BCE, educated in palace c. 1300 BCE, Atenism had been suppressed only 15-35 years
  • Court elders, archives, suppressed texts might have preserved memory
  • Moses could have encountered "underground" monotheistic ideas from Atenist remnants
  • Egyptian education ("wisdom of Egypt," Acts 7:22) might have included Amarna period history

Arguments Against Direct Influence:

  • Akhenaten's memory violently erased—unlikely to be taught or preserved openly
  • Fundamental theological differences (above) suggest independent traditions
  • Biblical narrative attributes monotheism to direct divine revelation (burning bush, Sinai), not cultural borrowing
  • Hebrew monotheism develops gradually from monolatry ("no other gods before me") to strict monotheism (Deutero-Isaiah: "I am the LORD, there is no other")

Alternative Explanations for Shared Themes

  1. Universal Religious Intuition: Monotheistic impulses arise independently in different cultures (Zoroastrianism, Sikhism, etc.)
  2. Solar Theology as ANE Common Stock: Sun as supreme deity appears across cultures (Ra, Shamash, etc.); both Atenism and early Yahwism utilize solar imagery
  3. Common Literary Conventions: Hymns praising creator deities share themes (provision, light/darkness, animals, ships) across ANE
  4. Progressive Revelation (Theological Explanation): God prepared humanity through partial revelations (Atenism as failed experiment?) before full revelation in Torah
  5. Egyptian Context Without Borrowing: Moses's Egyptian education exposed him to sophisticated theology, but Yahweh's revelation was distinctive and independent

Conclusion: Parallels Without Dependence

The parallels between Atenism and Yahwism—monotheism, solar imagery, universal creator, ethical exclusivity—are striking but do not prove direct borrowing. The differences are too profound:

  • Aten is the visible sun; Yahweh is invisible and transcendent
  • Atenism had no law, covenant, or ethical system; Torah is comprehensive
  • Atenism died with Akhenaten; Yahwism endured millennia
  • Atenism was imposed by royal decree; Yahwism was covenantal relationship
  • Aten is impersonal life-force; Yahweh is personal God of history

The Psalm 104 parallels likely reflect shared ANE literary traditions, not direct borrowing from the Great Hymn to Aten (which was largely lost). Freud's Moses-as-Egyptian theory remains speculative, rejected by mainstream scholarship.

What we can affirm: Both Akhenaten and Moses challenged polytheism in deeply polytheistic cultures. Both proclaimed one supreme creator deity. But Akhenaten's revolution was political, aesthetic, and short-lived. Moses's was covenantal, ethical, and enduring. Whether God used Akhenaten's failed experiment to prepare the way for Torah, or whether both arose independently from the human search for ultimate truth, the result is clear: Atenism vanished; Yahwism became the foundation of Judaism, Christianity, and Islam.
