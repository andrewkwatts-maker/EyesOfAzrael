Ise Jingu - Soul of Japan

Ise, Mie Prefecture, Japan

Shinto Imperial Japanese

📜 Sacred Overview

Ise Grand Shrine (Ise Jingu) is the most sacred shrine in Japan and the spiritual heart of Shinto, Japan's indigenous religion. Set within a vast ancient forest of cryptomeria cedars covering over 5,500 hectares, the shrine complex comprises 125 shrines centered on the Inner Shrine (Naiku), dedicated to Amaterasu Omikami, the Sun Goddess and divine ancestor of the Imperial family, and the Outer Shrine (Geku), dedicated to Toyouke Omikami, goddess of agriculture and industry. Founded nearly 2,000 years ago according to tradition, Ise Jingu embodies the essence of Shinto's reverence for nature, purity, and the continuity of Japanese culture across millennia.

"The natural beauty of the shrine's forested setting expresses the Shinto belief that kami (spirits) dwell within nature. Here, the sacred and natural are one." - Shrine teaching

Most remarkably, the shrine buildings are completely rebuilt every 20 years in an unbroken tradition called Shikinen Sengu, ensuring the structures are always new yet eternally ancient. This practice, maintained for over 1,300 years, embodies Shinto concepts of renewal, purity, and the transmission of sacred craftsmanship. The 2013 ceremony was the 62nd rebuilding. The main shrines are simple yet profound wooden structures in the ancient Yuitsu-Shinmei-zukuri style, predating Buddhist influence, featuring unpainted cypress, thatched roofs, and elevated floors - a style found nowhere else.
