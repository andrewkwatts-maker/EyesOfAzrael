The Myth of Pandora

Pandora's Box (originally a pithos or large storage jar) is one of the most famous artifacts in Greek mythology, central to the explanation of why evil exists in the world. According to Hesiod, Zeus created Pandora as a punishment for humanity after Prometheus stole fire from the gods. She was the first mortal woman, crafted by Hephaestus and given gifts by all the gods - hence her name, meaning "all-gifted."

Zeus gave Pandora to Epimetheus, Prometheus's brother, along with a jar that she was forbidden to open. Overcome by curiosity, Pandora opened the jar, releasing all the evils contained within - sickness, death, hardship, and countless other afflictions that would plague humanity forever. Only Hope (Elpis) remained trapped inside.

What Was Released:

  • Diseases: Illness and plague spread across the earth
  • Death: Mortality became the lot of humankind
  • Suffering: Pain and hardship entered human experience
  • Deception: Lies and treachery became possible
  • Countless Evils: Every affliction that troubles humanity
  • Hope Remained: Elpis stayed in the jar, either as comfort or further torment

Interpretations and Meaning

The Mystery of Hope

Scholars have long debated why Hope remained in the jar. Some see it as a blessing - hope was preserved for humanity as their only defense against despair. Others argue it represents a final cruelty: either hope is itself an evil (false hope that prolongs suffering) or its retention means humans are denied even the comfort of foreknowledge about their troubles.

Box vs. Jar

The famous "box" is a mistranslation. The Greek word pithos refers to a large storage jar, not a box. The error originated with Erasmus of Rotterdam in the 16th century, who translated pithos as "pyxis" (box) in his Latin version. Despite being incorrect, "Pandora's Box" has become the standard phrase in English.

Cultural Significance

The myth has become a powerful symbol for unintended consequences, forbidden curiosity, and the origin of suffering. "Opening Pandora's box" is now an idiom for triggering a cascade of unforeseen troubles. The story reflects ancient Greek anxieties about women, knowledge, and the gods' relationship with mortals.
