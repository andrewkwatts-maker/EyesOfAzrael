Ziggurat of Ur - Stairway to Heaven

Tell el-Muqayyar, Dhi Qar Province, Iraq

Sumerian Mesopotamian Moon Worship

📜 Historical Overview

The Ziggurat of Ur (Sumerian: E-temen-nigur, "House whose foundation creates terror") is the best-preserved ziggurat in ancient Mesopotamia and one of the most important monuments from the world's earliest urban civilizations. Built around 2100 BCE by King Ur-Nammu of the Third Dynasty of Ur, this massive stepped temple tower was dedicated to Nanna (Akkadian: Sin), the moon god who was the patron deity of Ur. Standing originally at approximately 30 meters (100 feet) tall, the ziggurat dominated the flat Mesopotamian plain and served as the religious heart of one of the ancient world's greatest cities - the biblical "Ur of the Chaldees," traditional birthplace of Abraham.

"For Nanna, the firstborn son of Enlil, his lord, Ur-Nammu, the mighty man, king of Ur, king of Sumer and Akkad, built his temple and restored this ziggurat." - Foundation inscription of Ur-Nammu

The ziggurat was part of a larger temple complex called the Ekishnugal ("House of the Great Light"), which included temples, storerooms, and the gipar - the residence of the high priestess. The structure underwent multiple restorations, most notably by Nabonidus, the last king of Babylon (556-539 BCE), who was devoted to the moon god. After millennia of abandonment, the site was excavated by Sir Leonard Woolley in the 1920s-30s, revealing not only the ziggurat but the Royal Tombs of Ur with their spectacular grave goods and evidence of human sacrifice. Saddam Hussein partially reconstructed the lower levels in the 1980s, and the ziggurat now sits near a major Iraqi air base.

🏛 Architectural Marvel

📐 Structure & Dimensions

  • Base: 64 x 46 meters (210 x 150 feet)
  • Original height: ~30 meters (100 feet)
  • Three stepped terraces (originally)
  • Surviving height: ~15 meters (50 feet)
  • Solid mud-brick core
  • Baked brick facing (8 feet thick)
  • Bitumen mortar waterproofing

🚰 Triple Stairway

  • Three monumental staircases
  • 100 steps each
  • Central staircase for priests only
  • Side stairs for ritual processions
  • Converge at gatehouse on first terrace
  • Symbolic ascent to heaven
  • Dramatically restored by Woolley

🌆 Temple on Summit

  • High temple (no longer extant)
  • House of Nanna the moon god
  • Blue-glazed brick covering
  • Gold and silver decorations
  • Meeting place of god and humans
  • Sacred marriage rituals
  • Only priests accessed summit

🛠 Construction Techniques

  • 720,000 baked bricks for facing
  • Millions of mud bricks in core
  • Weeper holes for drainage
  • Reed matting between courses
  • Bitumen waterproofing throughout
  • Slight inward lean for stability
  • No internal chambers

🏙 Temple Complex

  • Ekishnugal precinct surrounds
  • Court of Nanna at base
  • Gipar: high priestess residence
  • Ganunmah: storehouse
  • Edublamah: courthouse
  • Massive enclosure walls
  • Economic and religious center

🔮 Ziggurat Purpose

  • Artificial mountain for the god
  • Platform between earth and heaven
  • Visible symbol of city's power
  • Astronomical observations possible
  • Foundation of cosmic order
  • Temple "mooring post" of gods
  • Similar to biblical Tower of Babel

🌚 Religious & Mythological Significance

🌚 Nanna/Sin Moon God

  • Patron deity of Ur
  • Father of the gods in Sumerian pantheon
  • Controls months and seasons
  • Lord of wisdom and secrets
  • Crescent moon his symbol
  • Traveled the night sky in boat
  • Ziggurat his earthly dwelling

  • Lived in gipar residence

  • Tradition lasted millennia

👑 Associated Deities & Figures

🌚

Nanna/Sin

Moon god, patron of Ur. Primary deity of the ziggurat, lord of months and wisdom.

🌟

Ningal

Consort of Nanna, goddess of reeds. "Great Lady" with her own temple in the complex.

🔥

Inanna/Ishtar

Daughter of Nanna, goddess of love and war. Venus as morning and evening star.

🌞

Utu/Shamash

Son of Nanna, sun god and lord of justice. Twin of Inanna.

👑

Ur-Nammu

King who built the ziggurat (c. 2112-2095 BCE). Created first law code.

📖

Enheduanna

High priestess and first named author in history. Composed hymns to Inanna.
