Gobekli Tepe - The World's First Temple

Sanliurfa Province, Southeastern Turkey

Pre-Pottery Neolithic Hunter-Gatherer Anatolian

📜 Historical Overview

Gobekli Tepe (Turkish: "Potbelly Hill") is the oldest known megalithic structure in the world, predating Stonehenge by approximately 6,000 years and the Egyptian pyramids by 7,000 years. Dating to approximately 9600-8000 BCE, this Pre-Pottery Neolithic site was constructed by hunter-gatherers who had not yet developed agriculture, writing, or metal tools. Its discovery in 1994 by German archaeologist Klaus Schmidt revolutionized our understanding of prehistoric civilization, suggesting that organized religion and monumental architecture preceded - and perhaps drove - the development of agriculture and settled society.

"First came the temple, then the city." - Klaus Schmidt, lead archaeologist (1953-2014)

The site consists of multiple circular enclosures containing massive T-shaped limestone pillars, some standing up to 5.5 meters tall and weighing up to 20 tons. These pillars are decorated with intricate bas-reliefs depicting animals, abstract symbols, and humanoid figures. Remarkably, after approximately 1,500 years of use, the entire complex was deliberately buried around 8000 BCE, preserving it until its modern rediscovery. Only about 5% of the site has been excavated, with ground-penetrating radar revealing at least 20 circular structures still buried beneath the hillside.

🏛 Architectural Marvel

🛠 T-Shaped Pillars

  • Up to 5.5 meters (18 feet) tall
  • Weight up to 20 tons each
  • Carved from single limestone blocks
  • Anthropomorphic shape (arms and hands carved)
  • Represent stylized humans or deities
  • Central pillars larger than perimeter stones
  • Carved with animal reliefs

🌮 Circular Enclosures

  • Multiple rings of pillars
  • 10-30 meters in diameter
  • Low stone walls between pillars
  • Two central pillars face each other
  • Semi-subterranean construction
  • Benches along interior walls
  • At least 20 enclosures identified

🐧 Animal Reliefs

  • Foxes, lions, wild boars
  • Snakes, scorpions, spiders
  • Bulls (aurochs), gazelles
  • Vultures, cranes, ducks
  • No domesticated animals depicted
  • Many animals shown in profile
  • Some in aggressive poses

🔀 Abstract Symbols

  • H-shapes and crescents
  • Circles and bucranium (bull skulls)
  • Possible astronomical symbols
  • May represent proto-writing
  • Meaning remains undeciphered
  • Similar symbols at contemporary sites
  • Potential clan or ritual markers

🛠 Construction Mystery

  • Built without metal tools
  • No evidence of wheel use
  • Flint tools for carving
  • Limestone quarried 100-500m away
  • Labor of hundreds required
  • No permanent settlement nearby
  • Suggests organized social structure

🌐 Site Layout

  • Hilltop location with wide views
  • Not a habitation site
  • Purely ceremonial function
  • Multiple phases of building
  • Older structures buried by newer
  • Deliberate, planned backfilling
  • Preservation through burial
