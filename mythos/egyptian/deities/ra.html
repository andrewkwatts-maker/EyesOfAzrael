Ra (Re, Atum-Ra)

The Sun God, Creator, King of the Gods

Ra is the supreme solar deity of ancient Egypt, the self-created god who emerged from the primordial waters of Nun to bring light, life, and order to the cosmos. As the sun traversing the sky in his golden barque, Ra embodies divine kingship, the eternal cycle of death and rebirth, and the ultimate source of all existence.

Mythology & Stories

Ra's mythology spans the entire cosmic cycle from creation through daily rebirth. He is simultaneously the ancient grandfather of the gods and the eternally renewed child born each dawn. His stories reveal the Egyptian understanding of time, power, and the delicate balance between order and chaos.

Key Myths:

  • Creation from Nun: In the beginning was only Nun, the primordial waters of chaos. From these waters arose the benben stone, the first land, and upon it Ra (as Atum-Ra) created himself through an act of will and divine masturbation or spitting. He spoke his own name, bringing himself into existence, then created Shu (air) and Tefnut (moisture), beginning the Ennead lineage. Ra's tears became the first humans.
  • The Daily Solar Journey: Each day, Ra travels across the sky in his golden solar barque called the Mandjet (Day Boat). He is accompanied by Ma'at (order), Thoth (wisdom), and defenders against chaos. At noon he reaches his zenith as Ra-Horakhty. At dusk, he boards the Mesektet (Night Boat) and descends into the Duat (underworld), where he must traverse twelve hours of night, facing demons and the chaos serpent Apep. Each dawn he is reborn as Khepri (the scarab), emerging victorious from darkness.
  • The Secret Name of Ra: Ra possessed a secret true name which held the source of his power. Isis, desiring ultimate magical authority, crafted a serpent from Ra's spittle and earth, which bit him. Only Isis could heal the poison, but demanded his true name as payment. Reluctantly, Ra whispered his secret name into Isis's ear, transferring immense magical power to her. This myth explains Isis's supremacy in magic and shows even the greatest god could be manipulated through knowledge.
  • Ra's Eye and the Destruction of Humanity: When Ra grew old and humanity rebelled against his rule, he sent his Eye - manifesting as the goddess Sekhmet (or Hathor in wrathful form) - to punish them. Sekhmet became drunk on bloodlust and nearly destroyed all humanity. Ra relented and tricked her into drinking beer dyed red with pomegranate juice, which she mistook for blood. Intoxicated, she ceased her slaughter. This myth established the Sekhmet festival where beer was consumed to commemorate humanity's salvation.
  • The Contendings of Horus and Set: Ra presided over the divine tribunal that judged between Horus and Set for rulership of Egypt. Though Ra initially favored Set (who defended his barque nightly from Apep), the other gods and Ra's own daughter Hathor persuaded him to award kingship to Horus, establishing the principle of rightful succession.
Sources: Pyramid Texts (Old Kingdom, ~2400 BCE), Coffin Texts (Middle Kingdom), Book of the Dead (New Kingdom), Papyrus of Ani, Temple inscriptions at Heliopolis (largely destroyed), Karnak Temple Complex, Amduat ("That Which is in the Underworld"), Book of Gates

Worship & Rituals

Sacred Sites

Ra's primary cult center was Heliopolis (Iunu, "City of the Sun") in Lower Egypt, where the benben stone was housed in the Temple of Ra. Other major worship sites included Karnak Temple (as Amun-Ra), Luxor Temple, Abu Simbel (where Ramesses II built temples aligned so sunlight illuminates the inner sanctum twice yearly), and throughout Egypt where solar temples oriented to capture the rising or setting sun.

Festivals

  • The Wepet Renpet (New Year Festival): Celebrated the heliacal rising of Sirius (Sopdet) which coincided with the Nile flood and Ra's cosmic renewal. Marked the beginning of the Egyptian calendar year. Involved processions, offerings of first fruits, and renewal of temple fires from the sun's rays.
  • Solar Alignment Festivals: At Abu Simbel, twice yearly (February 21 and October 21), the sun's rays penetrate 60 meters into the temple to illuminate statues of Ra-Horakhty, Amun-Ra, and Ramesses II in the inner sanctuary. Celebrated as the sun god's direct blessing upon the pharaoh.

Offerings

Morning offerings at sunrise were essential: bread, beer, oxen, fowl, incense (frankincense and myrrh), blue lotus flowers (which open at dawn), gold and precious metals (solar substances), linen, natron for purification. The morning hymn declared: "Awake in peace, Great God, awake in peace!" Offerings ensured Ra's strength to complete his daily journey and defeat Apep.

Prayers & Invocations

Prayers to Ra emphasized his life-giving power and protection: "Hail to you, Ra, at your rising! You shine, you shine, you rise, you rise! Your rays are upon all faces. You are unknowable, no god can perceive your form. You are gold-hued, maker of brightness, who lights the darkness. Your name is hidden yet your manifestations are before our eyes. Praise to you, Ra, who creates the gods and fashions humankind!"

The "Litany of Ra" contained 75 names of the sun god, recited during funerary rites to help the deceased navigate the Duat by invoking Ra's protection in all his forms.

Forms and Manifestations

Ra appeared in multiple forms throughout the day and night cycle:

Khepri (Morning)

Scarab beetle form, the rising sun, representing transformation and rebirth. Name means "he who comes into being."

Ra-Horakhty (Noon)

Falcon-headed form, "Ra, Horus of the Horizon," the sun at its zenith, representing full power.

Atum (Evening)

Elder human form, the setting sun, representing completion and the descent into the underworld.

Auf-Ra (Night)

Ram-headed form navigating the Duat, "flesh of Ra," requiring protection from Apep.

Amun-Ra

Syncretic fusion with Amun of Thebes, "the hidden sun," combining transcendent mystery with manifest light.
