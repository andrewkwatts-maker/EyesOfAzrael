Anubis (Inpu, Anpu)

Guardian of the Dead, Lord of Mummification, Guide of Souls

Anubis is the ancient jackal-headed god of death, mummification, and the afterlife. As the original lord of the necropolis and guide of souls through the Duat, Anubis stands as protector of the dead and guardian of the scales of judgment. His presence ensures proper burial rites and safe passage to the realm of Osiris.

Mythology & Stories

Anubis's mythology centers on death, preservation, and judgment. As one of the oldest gods in the Egyptian pantheon, his role evolved from lord of the dead to psychopomp and guardian of mummification secrets.

Key Myths:

  • The Mummification of Osiris: When Set murdered and dismembered Osiris, Anubis invented mummification to preserve Osiris's body. Working with Isis and Nephthys, Anubis performed the first embalming, creating the techniques that would be used throughout Egyptian history. This established mummification as a sacred act that could grant eternal life.
  • Guardian of the Scales: In the Hall of Two Truths, Anubis oversees the weighing of the heart ceremony. He places the deceased's heart on one side of the scales and the feather of Ma'at (truth and justice) on the other. If the heart is lighter than the feather, the soul may pass to the Field of Reeds. If heavier with sin, the heart is devoured by Ammit. Thoth records the judgment, but Anubis ensures the scales remain true.
  • Battle Against Set: After helping restore Osiris, Anubis fought alongside Horus against Set. In some versions, Anubis captured Set and flayed him, wearing his skin as a warning to those who would desecrate tombs. This established Anubis as protector against forces of chaos that threaten the dead.
  • Guide Through the Duat: Anubis leads souls through the perilous twelve regions of the Duat, protecting them from demons and guiding them past obstacles. His knowledge of secret paths and passwords allows the properly prepared dead to navigate safely to judgment.
Sources: Pyramid Texts, Book of the Dead (especially Spell 125 - Judgment Scene), Papyrus of Ani, Temple inscriptions at Abydos, Coffin Texts, Amduat

Worship & Rituals

Sacred Sites

Anubis's primary cult center was Cynopolis (Hardai, "City of the Dog") in Upper Egypt. He was also worshipped at necropolis sites throughout Egypt, particularly at Abydos, Memphis, and Thebes. Every tomb and embalming workshop was considered sacred to Anubis, and his priests (called "Imy-ut" or "He who is in the place of embalming") performed funerary rites.

Festivals

  • The Festival of Wag: Celebrated during the first month of the flood season, honoring the dead with offerings and processionals to tombs. Anubis received special offerings as guardian of the necropolis.
  • Opening of the Mouth Ceremony: Performed on mummies and tomb statues, this ritual reanimated the deceased's senses. Anubis priests presided, using ritual instruments to symbolically open the mouth, eyes, and ears of the dead.

Offerings

Offerings to Anubis included: myrrh and frankincense (embalming materials), natron (purification salt), linen bandages, black dogs or jackals (symbolic), bread and beer, ox meat, incense. Offerings were placed at tomb entrances and in funerary temples, asking for Anubis's protection of the deceased and safe passage through the underworld.

Prayers & Invocations

Prayers to Anubis emphasized his protective role: "Homage to you, Anubis, who dwells in the embalming chamber. Grant that I may enter the Hall of Two Truths with a pure heart. Guard my body in the sacred land. Lead me safely through the passages of the Duat. Let your hand be upon me in the hour of judgment. May I stand justified before Osiris, my heart lighter than the feather of Ma'at."

Embalmers invoked: "Anubis, Lord of Secrets, guide my hands in this sacred work. Preserve this flesh that the ba may return. Purify this body that it may be worthy of eternal life. As you preserved Osiris, so preserve this servant of the gods."

Iconography

Anubis appears in several iconic forms:

  • Jackal-Headed Man: Most common depiction, showing a human body with black jackal head, holding was scepter and ankh
  • Full Jackal: Lying on a shrine or tomb, representing his role as guardian of burial grounds
  • At the Scales: Depicted adjusting or watching the scales in judgment scenes from the Book of the Dead
  • Embalming Table: Shown performing mummification, often with Osiris on the table

His black color represents both the color of decomposition and the fertile black soil of the Nile, symbolizing regeneration and rebirth after death.

  • Interlayer spacing expansion (c-axis):
    • Iodine atoms separate the NbS₂ layers, decreasing interlayer coupling
    • Enhances the 2D nature of superconductivity
    • Suppresses competing phases like Charge Density Waves (CDWs)
    • Makes the material ideal for nanofabrication and quantum device integration
    • Relevant links: Niobium disulfide (Wikipedia), Intercalation chemistry
  • Electronic doping:
    • Iodine is a strong electron acceptor
    • Changes electron density, potentially tuning Tc
    • Moves Fermi level relative to electronic band structure features
    • Similar to iodine doping in cuprate superconductors (Bi₂Sr₂CaCu₂Ox)
  • Increased stability:
    • Less reactive to ambient conditions than pristine 2D flakes
    • Improved long-term stability for device integration
    • Protects the superconducting core from degradation

Chemical Structure:

Formula: Au₃NPU
Oxidation states: Au(+3), N(-3), P(variable), U(+6 or +4)
Structure type: Complex intermetallic with mixed ionic/metallic bonding
Coordination: Gold atoms coordinating nitrogen and phosphorus around uranium core

Reasoning for Charge Balance:

  • Gold (Au₃): Three gold atoms, each at +1 oxidation state = +3 total charge
    • Gold can exist in +1 or +3 states; Au(I) is common in complexes
    • Three Au(I) atoms provide structural framework around central core
  • Nitrogen (N): Single nitrogen at -3 oxidation state (nitride ion, N³⁻)
    • Nitride is highly electronegative and forms strong bonds
    • Provides -3 charge to balance positive charges
  • Phosphorus (P): Single phosphorus, variable oxidation state
    • Can exist as phosphide (P³⁻) or phosphate (PO₄³⁻) depending on oxygen availability
    • Contributes to structural complexity and bonding
  • Uranium (U): Heavy actinide core element
    • U(VI) in UO₂²⁺ form (uranyl) or U(IV) in reduced state
    • Provides mass, radioactivity, and complex f-electron chemistry
    • Central coordinating atom for the entire structure
  • Overall charge balance:
    • If Au(I): 3(+1) from Au + (-3) from N + variable P + U charges balance
    • Alternative: Mixed valence compound with complex electronic structure
    • Result: Dense, complex mineral with mixed metallic/ionic character
