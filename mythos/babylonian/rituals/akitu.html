Akitu Ritual Tablet:BM 45749:Lines 279-289
"On the fifth day, three and one-third hours after sunrise, the high priest shall come forth and take all the insignia of kingship—the crown, the scepter, the ring, and the mace. He shall bring them before the god Bel [Marduk] and place them on a chair before Bel. He shall go out and strike the king's cheek. He shall take the regalia behind Bel. He shall strike the king's cheek. If when he strikes the king's cheek, the tears flow, it means Bel is favorable. If tears do not flow, it means Bel is angry: an enemy will arise and bring about his downfall."
Source: Babylonian Akitu Ritual Text (c. 300 BCE), British Museum BM 45749
Akitu Ritual Tablet:BM 45749:Lines 400-418
"The king shall say: 'I have not sinned, O lord of the lands, I have not been negligent regarding your divinity, I have not destroyed Babylon, I have not caused its dissolution, I have not shaken the Esagila, I have not forgotten your rites, I have not struck the face of a subordinate. I care for Babylon, I have not broken its walls.' After he speaks thus, the priest shall say to the king: 'Have no fear of what Marduk has spoken! Bel will hear your prayers. He will magnify your kingship. He will exalt your sovereignty.'"
Source: Babylonian Akitu Ritual Text (c. 300 BCE), British Museum BM 45749
Akitu Chronicle:ABC 16:Column I:Lines 1-8
"Year 3 of Nabonidus: The king did not come to Babylon for the akitu festival. Nabu did not come to Babylon. Bel did not go out. The akitu festival was omitted. The population was disturbed... Year 5: The king was in Tema; the prince, the officials, and his army were in Akkad. The king did not come to Babylon for the akitu ceremony. Nabu did not come from Borsippa for the procession of Bel. Bel did not go out."
Source: Akitu Chronicle (c. 540 BCE), recording festival cancellations
Herodotus:Histories:Book I:181-183
"In the sacred precinct of Zeus-Bel in Babylon there is a second shrine lower down, in which is a great sitting figure of Zeus, all of gold on a great golden throne, supported on a base of gold, with a golden table standing beside it. The Chaldaeans told me that to make all this more than eight hundred talents of gold were used. Outside the temple is a golden altar... and another great altar on which full-grown animals are sacrificed; only sucklings may be offered on the golden altar. On the greater altar the Chaldaeans burn a thousand talents' weight of frankincense yearly, when they keep the festival of this god."
Source: Herodotus, Histories, Book I (c. 440 BCE) - Greek account of Babylonian festivals
Cyrus Cylinder:Lines 20-22
"From [Shuanna] I returned the gods to their places and let them dwell in eternal abodes. I gathered all their inhabitants and returned them to their dwellings... and at the command of Marduk, the great lord, I settled in their habitations, in pleasing abodes, the gods of Sumer and Akkad, whom Nabonidus, to the anger of the lord of the gods, had brought into Shuanna."
Source: Cyrus Cylinder (c. 539 BCE) - Persian king restoring proper worship
