Poseidon

God of the Sea, Earthquakes, and Horses

Brother of Zeus and Hades, Poseidon rules all waters—seas, oceans, rivers, and springs. Known as "Earth-Shaker," he causes earthquakes and tsunamis with his trident. Tempestuous and powerful, he embodies the sea's dual nature: life-giving bounty and terrifying destruction.

Mythology & Stories

Key Myths:

  • Division of the Cosmos: After defeating the Titans, Zeus, Poseidon, and Hades drew lots to divide the universe. Zeus received the sky, Hades the underworld, and Poseidon the seas—making him lord of all waters and marine life. Though powerful, he chafed under Zeus's supreme authority and occasionally plotted rebellion.
  • Contest for Athens: Poseidon and Athena competed to become patron deity of the greatest city in Attica. Poseidon struck the Acropolis with his trident, creating a saltwater spring (symbolizing naval power and trade). Athena planted an olive tree (symbolizing peace and prosperity). The citizens chose Athena's gift, earning Poseidon's eternal enmity toward Athens.
  • Creation of Horses: In some myths, Poseidon created the first horse, either to impress Demeter (whom he pursued) or in competition with Athena. He was patron of horse-racing and equestrian skills. His own chariot was drawn by golden-maned hippocampi—horses with fish tails.
  • The Odyssey: Poseidon pursued Odysseus with relentless wrath for ten years after Odysseus blinded his son, the Cyclops Polyphemus. He sent storms, shipwrecks, and obstacles, delaying Odysseus's homecoming. Only when Odysseus reached Ithaca did Poseidon's anger abate.
  • The Walls of Troy: Apollo and Poseidon served King Laomedon, building Troy's famous walls. When Laomedon refused payment, Poseidon sent a sea monster to devour Troy's citizens. This betrayal led Poseidon to side with the Greeks during the Trojan War, seeking Troy's destruction.

Poseidon's Nature:

Poseidon embodies the sea's temperament: powerful, unpredictable, generous to those who honor him, and devastating to those who offend. He could grant safe voyages or summon killing storms. His earthquakes toppled cities. Yet he also protected sailors, fishermen, and colonists founding seaside settlements. His many love affairs with nymphs and mortals produced both heroic children and monstrous offspring, reflecting the sea's dual creative and destructive nature.

Sources: Homer's Iliad and Odyssey, Hesiod's Theogony, Homeric Hymn to Poseidon, Apollodorus's Bibliotheca, Pausanias's Description of Greece

Relationships

Family

  • Parents: Kronos and Rhea (swallowed at birth, later regurgitated)
  • Consort: Amphitrite (Nereid, queen of the sea), though he had many affairs
  • Children: Triton (merman herald), Theseus (hero of Athens), Polyphemus (Cyclops), Pegasus and Chrysaor (from Medusa), Orion (giant hunter), numerous sea-nymphs and monsters
  • Siblings: Zeus, Hades, Hera, Demeter, Hestia

Allies & Enemies

  • Allies: Amphitrite (wife), Nereus and the Nereids (sea deities), Oceanus (Titan of ocean), seafaring heroes and coastal cities who honored him
  • Enemies: Athena (rival for Athens and other cities), Odysseus (blinded his son Polyphemus), Troy (after Laomedon's betrayal), those who pollute seas or show disrespect

Worship & Rituals

Sacred Sites

Major temples stood at coastal cities: Corinth (where the Isthmian Games were held), Sounion (dramatic temple on cliff overlooking the Aegean), Taenarum (said to have an entrance to Hades), and throughout the Peloponnese. Sailors made offerings before voyages at coastal shrines. Underwater springs and grottos were considered his sacred places. The Isthmus of Corinth was especially holy to him.

Festivals

  • Isthmian Games: Held every two years at Corinth in Poseidon's honor, featuring athletic competitions, chariot races, and maritime contests. Winners received pine wreaths.
  • Poseideion: Mid-winter festival honoring Poseidon with sacrifices and prayers for spring sailing season.

Offerings

Bulls were the primary sacrifice (their roaring resembled the sea). Horses were offered, though rarely due to their value. Sailors poured libations of wine into the sea before voyages. Fishermen offered first catches. Coastal cities sacrificed black bulls during storms to appease his wrath. Hair was dedicated by young men coming of age. After safe voyages, sailors left votive anchors and ship models at temples.

Prayers & Invocations

Sailors and fishermen invoked Poseidon for safe passage and bountiful catches. Coastal cities prayed for protection from tsunamis and sea raiders. Horse-tamers and charioteers sought his blessing. During earthquakes, people made hasty offerings to appease the Earth-Shaker. Colonists founding new seaside settlements performed elaborate rites to gain his favor. Oaths were sworn by Poseidon when the sea or earthquakes were invoked as witnesses.
