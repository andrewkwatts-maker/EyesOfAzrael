Odysseus

The Cunning Hero of the Long Journey Home

Odysseus, king of Ithaca, stands as Greek mythology's quintessential trickster-hero—a man who survives not through strength but through intelligence, adaptability, and cunning (metis). The central figure of Homer's Odyssey, his ten-year journey home from the Trojan War became Western literature's archetypal quest narrative, a story of perseverance, identity, homecoming, and the price of heroism. Where Achilles represents martial excellence, Odysseus embodies resourcefulness and the triumph of wit over force.

Early Life and the Trojan War

Odysseus was born to Laertes, king of Ithaca, and Anticlea. His maternal grandfather was Autolycus, a master thief and trickster, from whom Odysseus inherited his cunning nature. He married Penelope, daughter of King Icarius of Sparta, and they had a son named Telemachus. Odysseus's kingdom of Ithaca was rocky and poor, but he loved it deeply—a love that would sustain him through years of wandering.

When Helen of Sparta was abducted by Paris (or eloped, depending on the version), all her former suitors had sworn an oath to defend her chosen husband, Menelaus. This oath bound them to join the war against Troy. Odysseus, however, had no desire to leave his infant son and beloved wife. When the Greek leaders came to recruit him, he feigned madness, yoking an ox and a horse together and plowing his fields with salt. But the clever Palamedes placed baby Telemachus in front of the plow; Odysseus swerved to avoid his son, revealing his sanity. Compelled to join the war, Odysseus never forgave Palamedes—later arranging his death through false accusations of treason.

At Troy, Odysseus proved invaluable not as the mightiest warrior but as the wisest counselor. He retrieved Achilles from Skyros, discovered Philoctetes (whose magic bow was necessary for victory), and served as mediator in countless disputes. His greatest contribution came when the war reached a stalemate after ten years: Odysseus devised the plan of the Wooden Horse. The Greeks built an enormous hollow wooden horse, hid their best warriors inside (including Odysseus), and pretended to sail away. The Trojans, believing the horse a religious offering, dragged it inside their walls. That night, the Greeks emerged, opened the gates, and Troy fell. This stratagem made Odysseus both hero and villain—celebrated for his ingenuity, condemned for his deception.

The Journey Begins: Cicones and Lotus-Eaters

The Odyssey begins in medias res (in the middle of things) with Odysseus trapped on Calypso's island, then recounts his adventures in flashback. After leaving Troy, Odysseus first raided the Cicones at Ismarus—a pointless act of piracy that cost him many men when the Cicones counterattacked. This established a pattern: Odysseus's intelligence often saved his crew, but his pride and poor judgment endangered them.

Next, they reached the land of the Lotus-Eaters, where inhabitants consumed a flower that induced forgetfulness and blissful apathy. Several of Odysseus's men ate the lotus and lost all desire to return home. Odysseus had to drag them back to the ships by force, their memories of home and purpose dissolved by the plant's narcotic effect. This episode illustrates a central theme: the tension between the comfort of forgetting and the painful necessity of remembering one's identity and home.

The Cyclops Polyphemus

Odysseus's encounter with the Cyclops Polyphemus showcases both his brilliance and his tragic flaw (hubris). Landing on the Cyclopes' island, Odysseus and twelve men entered Polyphemus's cave seeking hospitality. The giant Cyclops, son of Poseidon, trapped them inside and began eating the men two at a time.

Odysseus devised a brilliant escape plan. He offered the Cyclops potent wine, and when Polyphemus asked his name, Odysseus replied "Nobody" (Outis in Greek). After the giant fell into drunken sleep, Odysseus and his men heated a massive wooden stake and drove it into Polyphemus's single eye. When the blinded Cyclops cried for help, saying "Nobody is hurting me," the other Cyclopes left, thinking him mad. Odysseus tied his men under the bellies of sheep; when Polyphemus let his flock out to graze (feeling their backs but not their undersides), the Greeks escaped.

But here Odysseus made a fatal mistake: as they sailed away, he couldn't resist shouting his real name, mocking the blinded giant. Polyphemus hurled boulders at the ship and prayed to his father Poseidon to curse Odysseus—that he would reach home late, after losing all his companions, in a stranger's ship, to find trouble at home. This curse set the tenor for the entire journey.

Aeolus, Laestrygonians, and Circe

Next, Aeolus, keeper of the winds, gave Odysseus a bag containing all the adverse winds, allowing only the favorable west wind to blow them home. They came within sight of Ithaca, but while Odysseus slept, his suspicious crew opened the bag, thinking it contained treasure. The released winds blew them all the way back to Aeolus, who refused to help again—the gods clearly opposed their return.

They reached the land of the Laestrygonians, giant cannibals who destroyed all but one of Odysseus's twelve ships, killing most of his fleet. The survivors landed on Aeaea, island of the witch-goddess Circe. She transformed half the crew into pigs with her potions. Odysseus, protected by the herb moly (given by Hermes), resisted her magic and forced her to restore his men. They remained on Aeaea for a year, and Circe became Odysseus's lover. She bore him one or more sons and eventually advised him that to reach home, he must first descend to the Underworld to consult the prophet Tiresias.

Descent to the Underworld

In one of Greek literature's most profound episodes, Odysseus performed the ritual of nekyia—summoning the spirits of the dead by offering blood sacrifices. In the Underworld, he encountered shades of the departed: his mother Anticlea (whose death from grief he hadn't known), Achilles (who said he'd rather be a living slave than king of the dead), Ajax (who refused to speak, still angry that Odysseus won Achilles' armor), and Agamemnon (murdered by his wife upon returning home).

Tiresias warned Odysseus not to harm the cattle of Helios on the island of Thrinacia, and prophesied that even after reaching home, he would face trials—suitors besieging his wife, consuming his wealth. Only after restoring order would he find peace. This knowledge both comforted and haunted Odysseus throughout his remaining journey.

Sirens, Scylla and Charybdis, and Helios's Cattle

Returning from the Underworld, Circe warned Odysseus of dangers ahead. First came the Sirens, whose enchanting songs lured sailors to their deaths on rocky shores. Odysseus longed to hear their song, so he had his men fill their ears with beeswax and tie him to the mast, ordering them not to release him no matter how he begged. He heard the Sirens' song—they promised knowledge of all things past and future—but his bonds held, and they sailed past safely.

Next came the strait where Scylla (a six-headed monster) and Charybdis (a massive whirlpool) dwelt on opposite sides. No ship could avoid both. Following Circe's advice, Odysseus chose to pass near Scylla, sacrificing six men to her six heads rather than risk losing the entire ship to Charybdis's vortex. This terrible choice—knowingly sending men to their deaths—haunted him, but it saved the survivors.

They landed on Thrinacia, island of the sun-god Helios, where sacred cattle grazed. Odysseus warned his crew not to touch them, but when contrary winds trapped them for a month and provisions ran out, the starving men slaughtered several cattle while Odysseus slept. Helios demanded punishment, and Zeus destroyed the ship with a thunderbolt. Only Odysseus survived, clinging to wreckage. Polyphemus's curse was complete—he reached home late, alone, in another's ship.

Calypso and Release

Odysseus washed ashore on Ogygia, island of the nymph Calypso ("she who conceals"). She held him there for seven years (some sources say five), offering immortality if he would remain as her husband. Despite her beauty and promise of eternal youth, Odysseus refused, spending his days weeping on the shore, gazing toward Ithaca. His refusal of immortality for the sake of home and mortality defines his character—unlike the gods, he values the specific and particular over the eternal and general.

Finally, Athena convinced Zeus to order Calypso to release Odysseus. She reluctantly obeyed, and Odysseus built a raft. Poseidon, still angry, sent a storm that destroyed the raft, but Odysseus swam for two days and nights, finally washing ashore on Scheria, land of the Phaeacians.

The Phaeacians and Return to Ithaca

The Phaeacians, ruled by King Alcinous, welcomed the shipwrecked stranger. Princess Nausicaa found him on the beach and guided him to the palace. During a feast, the blind bard Demodocus sang of the Trojan War, and Odysseus wept at hearing his own story. He revealed his identity and recounted his adventures. Moved, the Phaeacians loaded him with gifts and sailed him to Ithaca while he slept—finally, after twenty years (ten at Troy, ten wandering), he reached home.

The Slaughter of the Suitors

Odysseus found his palace overrun by 108 suitors courting Penelope, consuming his wealth, and pressuring her to accept that Odysseus was dead. Disguised as a beggar by Athena, he assessed the situation. Only his dog Argos recognized him immediately, then died, having waited twenty years for his master's return.

Penelope, who had fended off suitors for years by weaving and unweaving a burial shroud, announced a contest: she would marry whoever could string Odysseus's great bow and shoot an arrow through twelve axe handles. The suitors all failed. The beggar asked to try. Despite their mockery, he easily strung the bow—then turned it on the suitors. With help from Telemachus (now grown), the swineherd Eumaeus, and Athena, Odysseus slaughtered every suitor in a brutal reckoning.

Penelope, cautious, tested him with a trick: she ordered their bed moved. Odysseus objected that this was impossible—he had built the bed around a living olive tree rooted in the ground. Only the real Odysseus would know this secret. Convinced, Penelope finally embraced her husband. They reunited after twenty years apart, their recognition scene one of literature's most moving moments.

Character and Legacy

Odysseus represents intelligence as heroism. While Achilles embodies physical prowess, Odysseus triumphs through metis—cunning, adaptability, and strategic thinking. He survives by disguise, by words, by tricks. Yet his intelligence has moral complexity: his lies serve both noble purposes (escaping the Cyclops) and ignoble ones (framing Palamedes). His epithet "polytropos" means both "much-traveled" and "much-turning" or "versatile"—suggesting both literal wandering and moral flexibility.

The Odyssey explores themes still resonant: the meaning of home, the possibility of return and restoration, the construction of identity through storytelling, the relationship between hospitality and civilization. Odysseus's ten-year journey transforms him—the man who returns is not the man who left. His story asks whether true homecoming is ever possible after such transformative experiences.

As Western literature's archetypal wanderer, Odysseus influenced countless later works—Virgil's Aeneas, Dante's Divine Comedy, Joyce's Ulysses. His cunning made him controversial: Romans distrusted his trickery; medieval writers cast him as villain. But his endurance, his loyalty to home and family, his very humanity—capable of tears, mistakes, and growth—make him perhaps mythology's most relatable hero.

Ancient Sources: Homer's Odyssey (primary source), Homer's Iliad, Sophocles' Ajax and Philoctetes, Euripides' Hecuba and Cyclops, Apollodorus's Bibliotheca, Virgil's Aeneid, Ovid's Metamorphoses

Relationships

Family

  • Father: Laertes, king of Ithaca
  • Mother: Anticlea, daughter of the master-thief Autolycus
  • Wife: Penelope, daughter of Icarius—renowned for her fidelity and cleverness
  • Son: Telemachus, who grew to manhood during Odysseus's absence and aided in slaying the suitors
  • Other Children: Telegonus (by Circe), and possibly Nausithous and Nausinous (by Calypso)

Allies and Enemies

  • Divine Patron: Athena, goddess of wisdom, who protected and guided him throughout his journey
  • Allies: Eumaeus (loyal swineherd), Philoetius (loyal cowherd), Mentor (advisor), Diomedes (war companion), the Phaeacians
  • Enemies: Poseidon (cursed him for blinding Polyphemus), the suitors of Penelope, Polyphemus the Cyclops, Ajax (bitter over the armor judgment), various monsters and hostile forces
