Jason

Leader of the Argonauts and Seeker of the Golden Fleece

Jason, prince of Iolcus in Thessaly, led the greatest assemblage of heroes in Greek mythology on a quest to retrieve the Golden Fleece from the distant land of Colchis. His story epitomizes the heroic quest narrative‚Äîthe gathering of companions, the perilous voyage to distant lands, impossible tasks overcome through divine aid, and the tragic consequences of success. Yet Jason himself remains one of mythology's most ambiguous heroes: neither the strongest warrior nor the wisest strategist, he succeeds through the help of others, particularly the sorceress Medea, and his later betrayal of her transforms him from hero to cautionary figure about the price of ambition and the danger of broken oaths.

Birth and Exile

Jason was born to Aeson, rightful king of Iolcus, and Polymede (or Alcimede). His uncle Pelias had usurped the throne, killing all potential rivals to secure his power. To save her infant son, Polymede faked Jason's death, mourning loudly while secretly smuggling the baby away to Mount Pelion, where the wise centaur Chiron raised him‚Äîthe same Chiron who had tutored Achilles and Heracles.

For twenty years, Jason learned the arts of hunting, music, medicine, and warfare under Chiron's tutelage, growing into a strong and handsome youth. An oracle had warned Pelias to beware a man wearing only one sandal, who would be his doom. This prophecy would soon manifest.

The One-Sandaled Man

When Jason came of age, he decided to reclaim his father's throne. Journeying to Iolcus, he encountered an old woman at a swollen river, asking to be carried across. Jason, displaying the kindness that would characterize his better moments, agreed. The current was fierce, and in the crossing, he lost one of his sandals in the mud. Only after reaching the other side did the old woman reveal herself as Hera, queen of the gods, testing mortals' hospitality. Impressed by Jason's compassion, she became his patron, though her help would ultimately prove as dangerous as it was beneficial.

Jason entered Iolcus wearing only one sandal, fulfilling the prophecy. Pelias recognized him immediately but concealed his fear. When Jason demanded the throne, Pelias pretended to consider it but claimed he was troubled by dreams sent by the spirit of Phrixus (who had originally brought the Golden Fleece to Colchis). The old king proposed a bargain: if Jason would retrieve the Golden Fleece and lay Phrixus's ghost to rest, Pelias would relinquish the throne. It was a suicide mission‚Äîno one had ever returned from Colchis‚Äîbut Jason, young and ambitious, accepted the impossible quest.

The Gathering of Heroes

To accomplish this feat, Jason needed the greatest ship ever built and the finest heroes in Greece. He commissioned Argus to construct the Argo, a fifty-oared vessel with a magical beam in its prow made from the oak of Dodona (sacred to Zeus), which could speak prophecies.

The call went out across Greece, and heroes flocked to join the quest‚Äîan assemblage never equaled before or since. Among the Argonauts were:

  • Heracles (greatest of all heroes)
  • Orpheus (legendary musician whose songs charmed all creation)
  • Castor and Polydeuces (the Dioscuri, divine twins)
  • Atalanta (legendary huntress, only woman among the Argonauts in some versions)
  • Peleus (father of Achilles)
  • Telamon (father of Ajax)
  • Theseus (in some versions)
  • Meleager, Admetus, Ancaeus, and dozens of others‚Äîa veritable catalog of heroic Greece

With this legendary crew, Jason set sail from Iolcus, beginning one of mythology's greatest voyages.

The Voyage East: Adventures of the Argonauts

The journey to Colchis was long and perilous, filled with adventures that would each make a tale worthy of legend:

Lemnos: The Argonauts landed on Lemnos, an island inhabited only by women who had killed all their husbands for taking Thracian concubines. The Lemnian women, needing to repopulate, welcomed the heroes enthusiastically. Jason fathered twin sons with Queen Hypsipyle. The Argonauts lingered so long that Heracles finally shamed them into continuing their quest.

The Loss of Heracles: At Kios, the beautiful youth Hylas (Heracles' companion) went to fetch water and was dragged into a spring by enamored nymphs. Heracles searched frantically, refusing to leave without him. Eventually, the Argonauts sailed on without their mightiest member‚Äîa devastating loss, but necessary, as Heracles' own destiny lay elsewhere.

The Harpies and Phineus: In Thrace, they found the blind prophet Phineus tormented by Harpies‚Äîfoul creatures who spoiled his food before he could eat, leaving him starving despite abundant provisions. The winged Boreads (sons of the North Wind) chased the Harpies away. In gratitude, Phineus provided crucial advice about the Symplegades.

The Clashing Rocks (Symplegades): At the entrance to the Black Sea stood the Symplegades‚Äîmassive rocks that smashed together, crushing any ship attempting to pass. Following Phineus's advice, they released a dove to fly between the rocks. The bird made it through, losing only tail feathers. The Argonauts rowed furiously through the momentarily open gap; the rocks smashed shut, shearing off the Argo's stern ornament but leaving the ship intact. By this feat, they "tamed" the Symplegades, which thereafter remained fixed and harmless.

Arrival at Colchis and the Tasks

Finally, the Argonauts reached Colchis, at the eastern edge of the Black Sea, ruled by King Aeetes (son of Helios, the sun god). The Golden Fleece hung in a sacred grove, guarded by a sleepless dragon that never closed its eyes. Aeetes had no intention of surrendering it but agreed to set Jason "tasks" instead of refusing outright.

The tasks were designed to be impossible:

1. Yoking the Bronze Bulls: Jason must yoke two massive brazen bulls with bronze hooves that breathed fire, then use them to plow a field.

2. Sowing the Dragon's Teeth: He must plant dragon's teeth (the same type Cadmus had sown at Thebes), which would sprout into fully armed warriors who would attempt to kill him.

3. Defeating the Warriors: He must defeat these spartoi (sown men) without help.

Jason faced certain death‚Äîno mortal could survive these ordeals. But Hera and Athena intervened, convincing Aphrodite to have Eros shoot Aeetes's daughter Medea with an arrow of irresistible love.

Medea's Aid

Medea, a powerful sorceress and priestess of Hecate, fell desperately in love with Jason. Torn between loyalty to her father and passion for the stranger, she chose Jason. Meeting him secretly, she provided magical aid in exchange for his oath to marry her and take her to Greece.

Medea gave Jason a magic ointment that made him invulnerable to fire and weapons for one day. She instructed him how to defeat the spartoi: when they sprouted, he should throw a stone among them, causing confusion‚Äîthey would attack each other instead of him. With this knowledge and protection, Jason succeeded in the impossible tasks, yoking the fire-breathing bulls, plowing the field, planting the dragon's teeth, and watching the spartoi destroy themselves.

But Aeetes, enraged at being outmaneuvered, refused to honor his bargain and planned to burn the Argo with its crew inside. Medea warned Jason and led him that very night to the sacred grove. There she sang enchantments and used her drugs to put the sleepless dragon into its first sleep. Jason snatched the Golden Fleece, and they fled with Medea's young brother Absyrtus aboard.

The Terrible Flight

Aeetes pursued them in his swifter ships, gaining rapidly. In the most horrifying moment of the quest, Medea committed an act that would define her character: to delay her father's pursuit, she murdered her own brother Absyrtus and cut his body into pieces, scattering them in the sea. Aeetes had to stop to gather his son's remains for proper burial, allowing the Argonauts to escape. This fratricide would haunt Medea‚Äîand Jason‚Äîforever.

The journey home was long and fraught with further adventures: they encountered the Sirens (whose song Orpheus drowned out with his music), skirted the island of the Phaeacians, and finally returned to Iolcus after months at sea, bearing the Golden Fleece in triumph.

The Usurpation and Medea's Revenge

Jason presented the Golden Fleece to Pelias, demanding the throne. But Pelias refused, and during Jason's absence, Aeson had died (or been murdered). Medea, seeing Jason's cause threatened, took revenge. She approached Pelias's daughters and demonstrated her magic by cutting up an old ram, boiling it with herbs, and producing a young lamb. She convinced Pelias's daughters that she could similarly rejuvenate their aged father.

The daughters killed Pelias and cut him into pieces, but Medea provided no herbs‚Äîshe had tricked them into murdering their own father. This horrific act, though avenging Aeson, made Jason and Medea exiles from Iolcus. They fled to Corinth, where they lived for ten years and had two sons.

The Betrayal and Tragedy

In Corinth, Jason's ambition overcame his honor. King Creon offered his daughter Glauce (or Creusa) in marriage, which would make Jason heir to Corinth's throne. Jason agreed, preparing to divorce Medea and abandon her and their children. His justification was coldly pragmatic: their sons would have better prospects with a Greek mother than a foreign sorceress.

Medea, betrayed after sacrificing everything‚Äîher family, her homeland, her honor‚Äîfor Jason, descended into murderous rage. She sent Glauce a beautiful poisoned robe as a wedding gift. When the princess put it on, it burst into unquenchable flames, killing her and Creon (who tried to save her). Then, in the most notorious act in Greek tragedy, Medea killed her own children by Jason, destroying his legacy and future before fleeing in a chariot drawn by dragons, sent by her grandfather Helios.

Jason was left alone, his glory turned to ashes, his future destroyed. According to some versions, he wandered as a broken man until, sitting in the shadow of the rotting Argo, the ship's prow fell and killed him‚Äîa pathetic end to a once-glorious hero. Other versions have him die forgotten and unhappy, cursed by the gods for his broken oath to Medea.

Character and Legacy

Jason represents the problematic hero‚Äîsuccessful not through personal excellence but through others' aid. Unlike Achilles (who embodies prowess), Odysseus (who embodies cunning), or Theseus (who embodies intelligence), Jason's defining trait is his ability to attract help: Hera's favor, the Argonauts' loyalty, and especially Medea's love and magic. When he betrays that support system‚Äîparticularly Medea‚Äîhe falls catastrophically.

His story warns about ambition without honor, about breaking sacred oaths for political advantage. The Golden Fleece brought Jason no lasting happiness; his greatest achievement became the source of his greatest tragedy. Medea's revenge demonstrates that some betrayals cannot be forgiven, that broken oaths to the gods (he had sworn by all the gods to remain faithful) bring terrible consequences.

The Argonauts' voyage influenced all subsequent quest narratives: the gathering of companions, the journey to distant lands, the winning of a treasure, the return home. Yet Jason's ultimate failure reminds us that winning the prize means nothing if we lose our souls in the process. His tale questions what heroism really means‚Äîis the one who leads the expedition but needs constant help truly a hero, or merely lucky until his luck runs out?

Euripides' Medea remains one of Greek tragedy's masterpieces, presenting Medea's perspective and making Jason appear callous, ungrateful, and self-justifying. Apollonius's Argonautica tells the quest's full story, emphasizing romance and adventure. The tale warns that great deeds achieved through others' sacrifices create debts that must be honored, and that broken oaths‚Äîespecially to those who loved and saved us‚Äîpoison everything they touch.

Ancient Sources: Apollonius Rhodius's Argonautica (primary source for the quest), Euripides' Medea, Pindar's Pythian Ode 4, Apollodorus's Bibliotheca, Ovid's Metamorphoses, various lost epics and fragments

Relationships

Family

  • Father: Aeson, rightful king of Iolcus (killed or driven to suicide by Pelias)
  • Mother: Polymede (or Alcimede)
  • Uncle: Pelias, usurper of Iolcus (killed by his own daughters through Medea's trickery)
  • Wife: Medea, princess of Colchis, sorceress, and ultimately his destroyer
  • Sons: Mermeros and Pheres (or Thessalus and Alcimenes), killed by Medea; twin sons by Hypsipyle of Lemnos

Companions and Enemies

  • Divine Patron: Hera, queen of the gods (though her favor brought mixed blessings)
  • The Argonauts: Greece's greatest heroes who accompanied him on the quest‚ÄîHeracles, Orpheus, Castor, Polydeuces, Peleus, Telamon, and dozens more
  • Allies: Medea (before the betrayal), the Argonauts, various helpers encountered during the voyage
  • Enemies: Pelias (usurping uncle), King Aeetes of Colchis, the guardian dragon, and ultimately Medea herself after his betrayal
