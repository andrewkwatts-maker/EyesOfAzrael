Enoch's Translation to Heaven

"By faith Enoch was taken up so that he should not see death, and he was not found, because God had taken him." (Hebrews 11:5)

Enoch is one of only two Old Testament figures explicitly said to be taken to heaven without dying. His translation (or "assumption") became a central theme in Jewish apocalyptic literature and a model for understanding divine favor, victory over death, and the possibility of human transformation.

New Testament Witness

The New Testament Validates Enoch's Assumption

Two New Testament passages explicitly confirm Enoch's translation and even quote from extra-biblical Enochic literature, showing early Christian acceptance of these traditions.

Hebrews 11:5 - Faith and Translation

c. 60s-90s CE
"By faith Enoch was taken up so that he should not see death, and he was not found, because God had taken him. Now before he was taken he was commended as one who pleased God."

Key Elements:

  • "Taken up" - Greek μετετέθη (metatethē) from μετατίθημι = to transfer, transport, translate
  • "Should not see death" - Direct statement: Enoch never died
  • "Was not found" - Disappeared, no body discovered
  • "God had taken him" - God's active role in translation
  • "Pleased God" - LXX translation of "walked with God"

Context in Hebrews 11:

Enoch appears in the "Hall of Faith" alongside:

  • Abel (v. 4) - righteous sacrifice
  • Enoch (v. 5) - faith translation
  • Noah (v. 7) - faith salvation from flood
  • Abraham (v. 8-19) - faith obedience

Jude 14-15 - Prophecy Validation

c. 65-80 CE
"It was also about these that Enoch, the seventh from Adam, prophesied, saying, 'Behold, the Lord comes with ten thousands of his holy ones, to execute judgment on all and to convict all the ungodly of all their deeds of ungodliness that they have committed in such an ungodly way, and of all the harsh things that ungodly sinners have spoken against him.'"

Remarkable Features:

  • Direct quote from 1 Enoch 1:9!
  • "Seventh from Adam" - affirms genealogical position
  • "Prophesied" - validates Enoch as prophet
  • "Ten thousands of holy ones" - angelic armies
  • Judgment theme - Enoch foresaw final judgment

Comparison with 1 Enoch 1:9:

"Behold, he comes with ten thousands of the holy ones, to execute judgment upon them and to destroy the impious, and to contend with all flesh concerning everything which the sinners and the impious have done and wrought against him."

Nearly word-for-word quote! This shows Jude's familiarity with and acceptance of 1 Enoch as containing authentic prophecy.

Theological Significance:

Both NT passages affirm:

  1. Enoch was translated without dying - bodily assumption to heaven
  2. Faith was the reason - "walked with God" = pleased God through faith
  3. Enoch prophesied - first prophet, before even Noah
  4. Extra-biblical Enoch traditions are valid - Jude quotes 1 Enoch as scripture-like authority

Ancient Jewish Witnesses

c. 180 BCE

Sirach (Ecclesiasticus) 44:16

"Enoch pleased the Lord and was taken up, an example of repentance to all generations."

Earliest extra-biblical Jewish witness to Enoch's assumption. Part of "Praise of the Fathers" (Sirach 44-50), alongside Noah, Abraham, Moses, etc.

c. 180 BCE

Sirach 49:14

"No one like Enoch has been created on earth, for he was taken up from the earth."

Emphasizes Enoch's uniqueness - no one else like him. Translation from earth to heaven unprecedented.

c. 160-150 BCE

Book of Jubilees 4:23

"He was taken from amongst the children of men, and we conducted him into the Garden of Eden in greatness and honor, and behold there he writes down the condemnation and judgment of the world, and all the wickedness of the children of men. And on account of it (God) brought the waters of the flood upon all the land of Eden; for there he was set as a sign and that he should testify against all the children of men, that he should recount all the deeds of the generations until the day of condemnation."

Key details:

  • Taken to Garden of Eden (not just "heaven" vaguely)
  • In "greatness and honor" - exalted status
  • Continues to write condemnation and judgment
  • Set as "sign" and "witness" against wickedness
  • Active role until "day of condemnation" (final judgment)
c. 50 BCE - 50 CE

Wisdom of Solomon 4:10-11

"There were some who pleased God and were loved by him, and while living among sinners were taken up. He was caught up so that evil might not change his understanding or guile deceive his soul."

Though not explicitly naming Enoch, likely allusion:

  • "Pleased God" - Enoch's defining characteristic
  • "Taken up" - assumption/translation
  • "Living among sinners" - antediluvian wickedness
  • "Caught up" - removed before corruption
  • Reason for early translation: Protection from evil influence
c. 93 CE

Josephus, Antiquities 1.3.4

"Now Enoch, although he was not a human child, became acceptable to God; and he returned to the deity, wherefore there is no account of his death."

Greek: εἰς τὸ θεῖον μετέστη - "departed/migrated to the deity"

Josephus rationalizes tradition for Greco-Roman audience while maintaining assumption tradition.

c. 20 BCE - 50 CE

Philo, Questions on Genesis 1.86

"What is the meaning of the expression, 'Enoch was well-pleasing to God, and was not found'? ...This means that while living he underwent a change, and became transformed into a divine nature."

Philo allegorizes Enoch as embodiment of repentance and virtue. Translation = transformation to divine nature (in philosophical terms).

Comparative Assumption Traditions: Enoch and Elijah

Enoch

Genesis 5:24 / Hebrews 11:5

Details:

  • When: After walking with God 300 years
  • Age: 365 years old
  • How: "God took him" - no details given
  • Witnesses: None mentioned
  • Reason: Walked with God, pleased God by faith
  • Result: "He was not" - disappeared, not found
  • After: In heaven (tradition: Garden of Eden, writing judgment)

Characteristics:

  • Cryptic, minimal biblical description
  • Seventh from Adam (symbolic position)
  • Before the Flood (antediluvian)
  • Removed from wicked generation

Elijah

2 Kings 2:1-12

Details:

  • When: After ministry to Israel, training Elisha
  • How: Whirlwind to heaven + chariot/horses of fire
  • Witnesses: Elisha sees it happen; 50 prophets see from distance
  • Reason: God's will (after completing prophetic ministry)
  • Result: Elisha receives double portion of spirit
  • After: Expected to return before "day of the LORD" (Malachi 4:5)

Characteristics:

  • Vivid, detailed biblical description
  • Dramatic theophany (fire, whirlwind)
  • After Israel's apostasy (Northern Kingdom)
  • Prophetic succession (passes mantle to Elisha)

Why Only Two?

The uniqueness of Enoch and Elijah's assumption raises theological questions:

  • Death as universal: "It is appointed for man to die once" (Heb 9:27) - but these two exceptions prove death not absolute
  • Foreshadowing resurrection: Victory over death possible, anticipating Christ's resurrection
  • Two witnesses: Both appear at Transfiguration with Christ (Matt 17:3 - Moses and Elijah; some traditions suggest Moses = Enoch typologically)
  • Revelation 11:3-12: Two witnesses killed and resurrected - often identified as Enoch and Elijah returning

Moses: Disputed Assumption

The Mystery of Moses's Death and Burial

Deuteronomy 34:5-6

"So Moses the servant of the LORD died there in the land of Moab, according to the word of the LORD, and he buried him in the valley in the land of Moab opposite Beth-peor; but no one knows the place of his burial to this day."

Who buried Moses?

  • Hebrew: "he buried him" - ambiguous subject (God? Angels?)
  • LXX: "they buried him" (plural)
  • Burial location unknown - prevents veneration/idolatry?

Jude 9 - Dispute Over Moses's Body

"But when the archangel Michael, contending with the devil, was disputing about the body of Moses, he did not presume to pronounce a blasphemous judgment, but said, 'The Lord rebuke you.'"

Questions raised:

  • Why would Satan dispute over Moses's body?
  • Did Michael take Moses's body to heaven (assumption)?
  • Source: Likely from lost Assumption of Moses (Testament of Moses)

Testament/Assumption of Moses (Lost)

  • Early Christian writers reference this text
  • Contained Moses's assumption tradition
  • Origen, Clement of Alexandria, Didymus mention it
  • Jude 9 appears to quote from it

Transfiguration Evidence

"And behold, there appeared to them Moses and Elijah, talking with him." (Matthew 17:3)

Moses appears with Elijah (assumed) and Jesus. How can Moses appear if he died and his body remained in grave? Possible explanations:

  • Resurrection/assumption: Moses was raised/assumed like Elijah
  • Spiritual appearance: Soul/spirit manifested temporarily
  • Vision: Not literal but visionary revelation

Universal Human Longing

Assumption traditions appear across cultures because they express universal human hopes:

  • Victory over death - mortality is not final
  • Divine favor - exceptional humans can transcend human limits
  • Access to heaven - bridge between earth and divine realm
  • Ongoing existence - death does not end consciousness/influence
  • Eschatological hope - assumed figures often return at end times

Theological Significance

Enoch's assumption demonstrates that death is not inevitable - that God can translate faithful ones directly to his presence without the normal boundary of mortality. This prefigures the Christian hope of bodily resurrection and Christ's own ascension. Enoch becomes a living proof that communion with God can overcome even death itself. He is the first human to demonstrate what later theology would call "victory over death." His translation also raises the question: where is Enoch now? According to Jubilees, he remains in the Garden of Eden, writing down judgment and serving as witness against human wickedness until the final day. He is both historical figure and eschatological sign - one who has already attained what believers hope for, yet whose work is not finished until history itself concludes.
