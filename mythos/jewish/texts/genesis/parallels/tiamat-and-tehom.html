Tiamat and Tehom

The Chaos Waters: From Combat Myth to Sovereign Creation

The Parallel: Primordial Chaos Waters

One of the most striking parallels between the Babylonian Enuma Elish and Genesis 1 involves the primordial chaos waters that exist before creation. In both accounts, the cosmos emerges through the ordering and separation of these watery depths—but the theological frameworks could not be more different.

Enuma Elish (Babylonian)

When on high the heaven had not been named,
Firm ground below had not been called by name,
Naught but primordial Apsu, their begetter,
And Mummu-Tiamat, she who bore them all,
Their waters commingling as a single body... — Enuma Elish, Tablet I, lines 1-5

Tiamat: The saltwater ocean personified as a primordial goddess. She is the chaotic mother of the gods who eventually becomes their enemy.

Apsu: The freshwater deep, Tiamat's consort, personified as the male counterpart.

Together they represent the undifferentiated chaos that existed before creation.

Genesis 1 (Hebrew Bible)

In the beginning God created the heavens and the earth.
The earth was without form and void,
and darkness was over the face of the תְּהוֹם (tehom),
and the Spirit of God was hovering over the face of the waters. — Genesis 1:1-2
And God said, "Let there be a רָקִיעַ (raqia, firmament)
in the midst of the waters, and let it separate the waters from the waters."
And God made the expanse and separated the waters that were under the expanse
from the waters that were above the expanse. — Genesis 1:6-7

Tehom (תְּהוֹם): The deep, the abyss—the primordial waters over which darkness hovers before God's creative word brings order.

Linguistic Connection: Tiamat ↔ Tehom

Language Word Meaning Usage
Akkadian ti'āmtu / tâmtu Sea, ocean Personified as goddess Tiamat in Enuma Elish
Hebrew תְּהוֹם (tehom) Deep, abyss, primordial waters Unpersonified cosmic waters in Genesis 1:2
Ugaritic thm Deep, abyss Cosmic waters in Canaanite cosmology
Etymology Probable common Semitic root: *tiham- or *taham- (deep, sea)

Scholarly Debate: Cognate or Coincidence?

The relationship between Tiamat and tehom remains debated among scholars:

  • Linguistic cognates: Many scholars see them as deriving from a common Semitic root meaning "sea" or "deep," suggesting shared linguistic heritage.
  • Direct borrowing: Some argue Genesis deliberately echoes Enuma Elish, using familiar terminology but transforming its meaning.
  • Coincidental similarity: Others suggest the similarity is overemphasized, pointing to different grammatical forms and semantic ranges.

What's certain: Both terms represent primordial chaos waters that must be ordered for creation to occur. The theological transformation is undeniable—Tiamat is a deity; tehom is merely a created element.

The Enuma Elish: Marduk vs. Tiamat

The Story in Brief

Act 1: The Primal Chaos

Before creation, only Apsu (sweet/fresh water) and Tiamat (salt water) existed. Their waters mingled, and from them the gods were born. But the younger gods became noisy and disturbed Apsu, who plotted to destroy them.

Act 2: Divine Rebellion

The wise god Ea (Enki) discovered the plot and killed Apsu. Enraged, Tiamat decided to avenge her consort. She created an army of monsters and chose the god Kingu as her new consort and war leader.

Mother Hubur [Tiamat], who fashions all things,
Made in addition weapons invincible; she spawned monster-serpents,
Sharp of tooth, and merciless of fang.
With poison, instead of blood, she filled their bodies.
Fierce monster-vipers she clothed with terror,
With splendor she decked them, she made them of lofty stature.
Whoever beheld them, terror overcame him... — Enuma Elish, Tablet I

Act 3: Marduk's Champion

The gods were terrified. None dared face Tiamat until young Marduk volunteered—on the condition that if he succeeded, he would become king of the gods. The divine assembly agreed.

Act 4: The Cosmic Battle

The Lord [Marduk] raised up the flood-storm, his mighty weapon.
He mounted the storm-chariot irresistible and terrifying...
Then joined issue Tiamat and Marduk, wisest of gods.
They strove in single combat, locked in battle.
The Lord spread out his net to enfold her,
The Evil Wind, which followed behind, he let loose in her face.
When Tiamat opened her mouth to consume him,
He drove in the Evil Wind that she close not her lips.
As the fierce winds charged her belly,
Her body was distended and her mouth was wide open.
He released the arrow, it tore her belly,
It cut through her insides, splitting the heart.
Having thus subdued her, he extinguished her life. — Enuma Elish, Tablet IV

Act 5: Creation from Tiamat's Corpse

The Lord trod on the legs of Tiamat,
With his unsparing mace he crushed her skull.
He split her like a shellfish into two parts:
Half of her he set up and ceiled it as sky,
Pulled down the bar and posted guards.
He bade them to allow not her waters to escape.
He crossed the heavens and surveyed the regions.
He squared Apsu's quarter, the abode of Nudimmud [Ea],
As the Lord measured the dimensions of Apsu.
The Great Abode, its likeness, he fixed as Esharra,
The Great Abode, Esharra, which he made as the firmament. — Enuma Elish, Tablet IV

The cosmic structure:

  • Upper half of Tiamat's body → Heaven/sky
  • Lower half of Tiamat's body → Earth/sea
  • Tiamat's waters held back by the firmament, guarded to prevent escape
  • Marduk then creates stars, assigns them to gods, establishes the calendar

Act 6: The Creation of Humanity

After creating the cosmos from Tiamat's corpse, Marduk kills Kingu (Tiamat's consort and battle leader) and uses his blood to create humanity:

"Blood I will mass and cause bones to be.
I will establish a savage, 'man' shall be his name.
Verily, savage-man I will create.
He shall be charged with the service of the gods
That they might be at ease!" — Enuma Elish, Tablet VI

Humanity is created from the blood of a slain rebel god, formed explicitly to serve the gods and relieve them of their toil.

Genesis 1: Sovereign Creation by Word

The Separation of Waters

In Genesis, there is no battle. God does not fight tehom; He simply speaks, and the waters obey:

Day 1: Light from Darkness

And God said, "Let there be light," and there was light.
And God separated the light from the darkness. — Genesis 1:3-4

Day 2: Waters from Waters

And God said, "Let there be an expanse in the midst of the waters,
and let it separate the waters from the waters."
And God made the expanse and separated the waters
that were under the expanse from the waters that were above the expanse.
And it was so. — Genesis 1:6-7

Structural Parallels

Key Theological Differences

1. Combat Myth → Sovereign Word

Enuma Elish: Creation requires violent battle. Marduk must physically defeat and kill Tiamat. The cosmos is literally built from the corpse of a slain goddess.

Genesis: No battle occurs. God speaks: "Let there be..." and it is so. The waters are not defeated enemies but obedient elements. Creation by effortless divine word demonstrates absolute sovereignty.

"By the word of the LORD the heavens were made, and by the breath of his mouth all their host." (Psalm 33:6)

2. Goddess → Created Element

Enuma Elish: Tiamat is a divine being—the primordial mother goddess. She is eternal (existed before the other gods) and powerful enough to threaten the entire pantheon.

Genesis: Tehom is not personified, not divine, not named as a deity. It's simply "the deep"—part of the created order that exists in the formless void before God organizes it. The lack of definite article in Hebrew (just תְּהוֹם, not הַתְּהוֹם) further indicates it's not a proper name or personage.

3. Struggle → Ease

Enuma Elish: The gods are terrified of Tiamat. Marduk demands kingship as payment for taking the risk. The battle is violent, desperate, uncertain. Creation is hard-won through conflict.

Genesis: God faces no resistance. "And God said... and it was so" is repeated throughout. The waters are separated effortlessly. No negotiation, no struggle, no risk. God's sovereignty is absolute and unchallenged.

4. Polytheistic Conflict → Monotheistic Order

Enuma Elish: A polytheistic theogony (origin of gods). Younger gods rebel against older gods. The cosmos emerges from divine family drama and violence. Chaos is divine, order is imposed through superior force.

Genesis: Radical monotheism. One God, no theogony, no divine genealogy, no conflict. Chaos (tohuwabohuw, "formless and void") is simply the pre-creation state that God orders. The universe reflects divine wisdom, not divine warfare.

Echoes of Combat Myth in the Psalms and Prophets

Interestingly, while Genesis 1 demythologizes the creation account, other biblical texts preserve poetic echoes of the older combat myth tradition—but always subordinated to Israel's monotheism:

Psalm 74:13-14

"You divided the sea by your might; you broke the heads of the dragons (tannin) on the waters. You crushed the heads of Leviathan; you gave him as food for the creatures of the wilderness."

Psalm 89:9-10

"You rule the raging of the sea; when its waves rise, you still them. You crushed Rahab like a carcass; you scattered your enemies with your mighty arm."

Isaiah 27:1

"In that day the LORD with his hard and great and strong sword will punish Leviathan the fleeing serpent, Leviathan the twisting serpent, and he will slay the dragon that is in the sea."

Isaiah 51:9

"Awake, awake, put on strength, O arm of the LORD; awake, as in days of old, the generations of long ago. Was it not you who cut Rahab in pieces, who pierced the dragon?"

Job 26:12-13

"By his power he stilled the sea; by his understanding he shattered Rahab. By his wind the heavens were made fair; his hand pierced the fleeing serpent."

Job 38:8-11

"Or who shut in the sea with doors when it burst out from the womb, when I made clouds its garment and thick darkness its swaddling band, and prescribed limits for it and set bars and doors, and said, 'Thus far shall you come, and no farther, and here shall your proud waves be stayed'?"

The pattern: These texts use the language of combat myth (dragons, Leviathan, Rahab, fleeing serpent) but always in service of monotheism. There's no actual battle between gods. Rather, Yahweh's sovereignty over chaos is so complete that subduing the waters is described in metaphorical, poetic terms drawn from the cultural vocabulary of the ancient Near East.

Scholarly Analysis: Demythologization

What Genesis Does

Biblical scholars widely recognize that Genesis 1 engages in a process called "demythologization"—taking familiar mythological motifs from the ancient Near Eastern world and deliberately transforming them:

  1. Uses familiar imagery: Tehom (chaos waters), firmament separating waters, etc.
  2. Strips divine/mythic status: Tehom is not a goddess like Tiamat; it's just water. The sun and moon (often worshipped as deities) are reduced to "lights" (Hebrew: me'oroth) created by God on Day 4—not even named!
  3. Eliminates conflict: No battle, no violence, no struggle. Just divine word and immediate compliance.
  4. Asserts sovereignty: The message is clear: Yahweh is not one god among many who must fight for supremacy. He is the only God, and all creation—including what other cultures worship as gods—is merely the work of His hands.

Polemical Intent?

Some scholars argue Genesis 1 is deliberate polemic against Babylonian religion:

  • Written/compiled during or after the Babylonian exile (6th century BCE)
  • Israelites surrounded by Babylonian culture, temples, and the Enuma Elish (recited at the Akitu/New Year festival)
  • Genesis 1 responds: "Your Marduk didn't defeat Tiamat. Our God spoke and all things—including the waters you call divine—came into being."

The Theological Revolution

Whether or not the polemic is intentional, Genesis represents a radical theological transformation:

From Theogony to Creation

Ancient Near Eastern pattern: Cosmogony (creation of the world) is inseparable from theogony (origin of the gods). The gods are born, fight, die. Creation is a byproduct of divine conflict.

Genesis: No theogony. God is eternal, uncreated, alone. Creation is not the result of conflict but the expression of divine will. "In the beginning, God"—not "in the beginning, the gods were born."

From Chaos as Divine to Chaos as Creature

Ancient Near East: Chaos (Tiamat, Apep, Yam) is personified, divine, powerful, and dangerous. Order must be violently imposed and constantly maintained against chaos's threat.

Genesis: Chaos (formlessness, void, darkness, waters) is not divine. It's the pre-ordered state that God effortlessly arranges. Chaos has no personality, no will, no power. God's word transforms it.

From Force to Word

Ancient Near East: Creation by combat, by physical force, by defeating enemies. Power is demonstrated through violence.

Genesis: Creation by word. "And God said, 'Let there be...'" Ten times. Power is demonstrated through effortless speech. "By the word of the LORD the heavens were made" (Ps 33:6). This points forward to John 1:1-3: "In the beginning was the Word... all things were made through him."

Conclusion: The Significance of the Parallel

The parallel between Tiamat and tehom is one of the most fascinating intersections of Genesis and ancient Near Eastern mythology. Whether linguistic cognates or deliberate allusion, the connection is undeniable—and so is the transformation.

What the parallel shows:

  • Genesis was written in a world saturated with creation myths
  • The biblical authors were familiar with these traditions
  • They used similar imagery and concepts

What the differences show:

  • Genesis is not just another ancient myth—it's a theological response to myth
  • The God of Israel is categorically different from the gods of the nations
  • Creation is not the result of divine conflict but divine will
  • Chaos is not eternal, not divine—it's subordinate to God's sovereign word

The movement from Tiamat to tehom—from personified chaos goddess to mere deep waters—represents the biblical revolution: demythologization in service of monotheistic worship of the one true Creator God.
