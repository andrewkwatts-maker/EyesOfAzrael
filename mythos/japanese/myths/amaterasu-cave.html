The Return of the Sun - When Darkness Covered the World

The myth of Amaterasu hiding in the Heavenly Rock Cave is one of the most celebrated stories in Japanese mythology, recorded in the Kojiki (712 CE) and Nihon Shoki (720 CE). When Amaterasu, the sun goddess, sealed herself in a cave to escape her brother Susanoo's violent rampage, the world plunged into eternal darkness. The myth explains the origins of sacred Shinto rituals, especially kagura dance, and illustrates how cleverness, cooperation, and sacred celebration can overcome even cosmic calamity.

📖 The Story

🌊

Susanoo's Rampage

After losing a contest with his sister Amaterasu, the storm god Susanoo should have departed from heaven peacefully. Instead, wild and resentful, he went on a destructive rampage through Takamagahara (the High Plain of Heaven). He broke down the rice paddy ridges that Amaterasu had carefully built, filled in the irrigation ditches, and defecated in her sacred harvest hall - polluting the food offerings meant for the gods.

Crimes: Destroying rice fields, polluting sacred spaces

Nature: Chaos attacking order, storm defying sun

Amaterasu's Response: Initially patient, making excuses for him

🐎

The Flayed Horse

Susanoo's violence escalated to its horrifying climax. During the sacred weaving ceremony, when Amaterasu and her attendants were weaving the garments of the gods, Susanoo tore a hole in the roof of the weaving hall. Through this opening, he hurled a piebald horse that he had flayed alive - skinned backward from tail to head. The shock was so great that one of Amaterasu's weaving maidens struck herself with the shuttle and died.

Victim: Wakahirume (Young Sun Woman), weaving maiden

Sacrilege: Violence in sacred space, death pollution

Symbolism: Ultimate violation of order and purity

🌌️

Amaterasu Hides

Horrified, grief-stricken, and enraged by her brother's violence, Amaterasu withdrew from the world. She fled to the Ama-no-Iwato (the Heavenly Rock Cave), entered its depths, and sealed the entrance with a great boulder. With the sun goddess hidden away, her light vanished from the world. Both Takamagahara (heaven) and Ashihara-no-Nakatsukuni (the middle land of the reed plains - earth) were plunged into complete darkness.

Location: Ama-no-Iwato (Heavenly Rock Cave)

Consequence: Universal darkness, eternal night

Emotion: Grief, trauma, withdrawal

🌓

World in Darkness

Without Amaterasu's light, the cosmos fell into chaos. Eternal night reigned over heaven and earth. Rice paddies could not grow. Evil kami multiplied and ran rampant like swarms of summer flies, their buzzing filling the darkness. Calamities and disasters arose everywhere. The voices of myriad misfortunes echoed through the black void. The order of the universe was collapsing into primal chaos.

Effects: Eternal night, crop failure, evil spirits

Threat: Complete cosmic collapse

Urgency: The sun must return or all is lost

🌍

Divine Council

The eight million kami (yaoyorozu-no-kami) gathered in desperate council on the dry riverbed of the Heavenly River (Ama-no-Yasukawa). Led by Omoikane-no-Kami (the god of wisdom and intelligence), they debated strategies to lure Amaterasu from her cave. Simple requests and pleas had failed - she would not emerge. They needed cleverness, ceremony, and something unexpected to reignite her curiosity about the world.

Location: Riverbed of the Heavenly River

Leader: Omoikane (Wisdom God)

Challenge: Lure a goddess who has lost all interest in the world

🦅

The Sacred Rooster

The gods began their ritual. First, they gathered the long-crying roosters (nagaki-no-tori) - birds whose crowing announces the dawn - and made them crow loudly outside the cave. These eternal roosters called out as if morning had come, though darkness still reigned. Their crowing was both a summons to the sun and a sympathetic magic, attempting to create dawn by performing its sounds.

Sacred Animal: Nagaki-no-tori (eternal roosters)

Function: Announcing dawn, summoning light

Magic: Sympathetic ritual - sound creates reality

🌿

The Sacred Tree

The gods uprooted a sacred sakaki tree (Cleyera japonica) from the heavenly mountain. On its upper branches they hung the Yasakani-no-Magatama - the great curved jewels that would become part of the Imperial Regalia. On the middle branches they hung the Yata-no-Kagami, the eight-span sacred mirror. On the lower branches they hung white and blue cloth offerings (shide). This sacred tree became the prototype of all Shinto offering stands (tamagushi).

Tree: Sacred sakaki (evergreen)

Upper Branches: Yasakani Magatama (curved jewels)

Middle Branches: Yata-no-Kagami (sacred mirror)

Lower Branches: White and blue cloth offerings

💃

Ame-no-Uzume's Dance

Then Ame-no-Uzume-no-Mikoto, the goddess of dawn, mirth, and meditation, climbed atop an overturned washtub (creating the first drum) in front of the cave. She began to perform a wild, ecstatic, and explicitly erotic dance. Stamping her feet rhythmically, she went into divine possession (kamigakari), her movements increasingly frenzied. She exposed her breasts and pushed down her skirt, revealing her genitals - a shocking, sacred, and hilarious display.

Performer: Ame-no-Uzume (Dawn & Revelry Goddess)

Dance Type: Ecstatic, erotic, shamanic

Elements: Drumming, possession, exposure

Origin: First kagura (sacred dance)

😂

Divine Laughter

The eight million kami watching Uzume's outrageous performance erupted into thunderous laughter. Their divine hilarity shook the heavens - eight million gods laughing simultaneously at the bawdy, sacred comedy unfolding before them. The sound was tremendous, cosmic, irresistible - a celebration of life, fertility, and joy so powerful it could break through any darkness, any grief, any withdrawal.

Sound: Eight million kami laughing in unison

Quality: Sacred, cosmic, overwhelming

Function: Life-affirming celebration breaks through despair

👁

Amaterasu's Curiosity

Inside her dark cave, Amaterasu heard the roosters crowing and the tremendous laughter of the gods. Confused and curious, she wondered: "I thought the world would be plunged into eternal darkness because of my absence. Why are the gods laughing? Why do they sound so joyful?" Unable to resist her curiosity, she opened the cave door just a crack to peek out and see what could possibly be causing such celebration in a world without light.

Emotion: Confusion, curiosity, wounded pride

Question: "Why are they happy without me?"

Action: Opens the cave slightly

💎

The Mirror Trick

Ame-no-Uzume called out: "We rejoice because there is a deity more glorious than you, and we are celebrating!" The gods held up the sacred mirror (Yata-no-Kagami) positioned perfectly to catch the cave opening. When Amaterasu peeked out, she saw her own brilliant reflection in the mirror - a radiant goddess of supreme beauty. Never having seen herself before, she was amazed, thinking perhaps another sun goddess had indeed appeared.

Deception: "A greater deity than you has appeared"

Tool: Yata-no-Kagami (sacred mirror)

Effect: Amaterasu sees her own glory, forgets her grief

💪

Seized by the Strong God

Fascinated by the beautiful goddess in the mirror (her own reflection), Amaterasu leaned out further from the cave to get a better look. At that precise moment, Ame-no-Tajikarao-no-Kami (the god of strength) - who had been hiding beside the entrance - seized her hand with his mighty grip and pulled her fully out of the cave. Instantly, her radiance flooded back across heaven and earth.

Hero: Ame-no-Tajikarao (Strength God)

Action: Seizes and pulls Amaterasu out

Result: Light returns to the world

🪝

Sealing the Cave

Before Amaterasu could retreat back into the cave, another god (Futodama-no-Mikoto in some versions) quickly stretched a sacred rope (shimenawa) across the cave entrance, declaring: "You must not go back beyond this point!" The shimenawa created a sacred boundary that Amaterasu could not cross. This sacred rope became the prototype of all shimenawa used in Shinto to mark holy spaces and prevent impurity from entering.

Barrier: Shimenawa (sacred straw rope)

Function: Prevents return to darkness

Prototype: All Shinto boundary ropes

☀️

Light Restored

With Amaterasu restored to the world, light flooded across Takamagahara and down to the earth below. The sun blazed in the sky once more. The rice paddies could grow. Order was restored. The evil kami fled back into hiding. The cosmos breathed again. Joy and relief swept through all creation. Amaterasu, reminded of her importance and welcomed back with celebration, resumed her rightful place as ruler of heaven.

Effect: Universal restoration of light and order

Resolution: Sun goddess returns to her duty

Theme: Community effort restores cosmic balance

⛓️

Susanoo's Punishment

The gods turned their attention to the one who caused this catastrophe. They imposed severe punishments on Susanoo: they cut off his beard, pulled out his fingernails and toenails, imposed a heavy fine (one thousand tables of restitutive gifts), and banished him from heaven forever. Cast down to earth, Susanoo would eventually be redeemed through heroic deeds - but he could never return to Takamagahara.

Punishments: Beard cut, nails pulled, fined, banished

Exile: Cast down to earth permanently

Future: Will find redemption through heroism

👤 Key Characters

☀️

Amaterasu - The Sun Goddess

Amaterasu-omikami is the supreme sun goddess and ruler of Takamagahara. Her withdrawal into the cave represents trauma, grief, and depression in response to violence and violation. Her return shows that even the most powerful need community support and reminders of their worth. She is both vulnerable and essential - powerful yet able to be wounded.

Role: Sun goddess, supreme deity

Journey: Withdrawal → Isolation → Curiosity → Return

Symbols: Sun, mirror, light, order

🌊

Susanoo - The Storm God

Susanoo-no-Mikoto is the tempestuous storm god whose violent rampage causes the crisis. Wild, emotional, and destructive, he represents chaos opposing order, storm defying sun. His actions show the dangerous power of uncontrolled emotion and the destructive potential of divine beings. Yet his later redemption reveals that even the destructive can transform.

Role: Storm god, agent of chaos

Crimes: Defiling sacred spaces, causing death

Nature: Wild, resentful, violent but later redeemable

💃

Ame-no-Uzume - Dawn Goddess

Ame-no-Uzume-no-Mikoto is the goddess of dawn, meditation, and revelry. Her ecstatic, erotic dance becomes the key to solving the cosmic crisis. She represents the power of sacred laughter, the healing force of celebration, and the shamanic ability to use seemingly inappropriate behavior for sacred purpose. She is both holy and bawdy, proper and outrageous.

Role: Dawn & celebration goddess

Action: Ecstatic shamanic dance

Significance: Creator of kagura (sacred Shinto dance)

Nature: Wise fool, sacred comedian

🧠

Omoikane - God of Wisdom

Omoikane-no-Kami is the deity of wisdom, intelligence, and clever planning. He devises the elaborate strategy to lure Amaterasu from the cave, demonstrating that intelligence and creativity can solve problems that force cannot. He represents the Shinto value of wisdom-in-council and the importance of clever planning over brute action.

Role: Wisdom & strategy god

Function: Devises the plan to retrieve the sun

Quality: Intelligence, creativity, foresight

💪

Ame-no-Tajikarao - God of Strength

Ame-no-Tajikarao-no-Kami is the deity of strength and physical power. Hidden beside the cave entrance, he seizes Amaterasu at the crucial moment and pulls her back into the world. He shows that wisdom needs strength to execute its plans - strategy and power must work together. His patience and perfect timing make the plan succeed.

Role: Strength god

Action: Seizes and pulls Amaterasu from cave

Quality: Power, patience, perfect timing

🌿

Futodama - God of Ritual

Futodama-no-Mikoto is the deity of rituals and divination. He stretches the sacred rope (shimenawa) across the cave entrance, preventing Amaterasu's return to darkness. He represents ritual propriety and the power of sacred symbols to maintain boundaries and prevent backsliding. His action shows that crisis resolution must be secured with ritual structure.

Role: Ritual & divination god

Action: Places the shimenawa barrier

Legacy: Establishes boundary rope tradition

💡 Symbolic Interpretations

🌓

Depression and Recovery

Amaterasu's withdrawal can be read as a mythic portrayal of depression or trauma response. Overwhelmed by violence and grief, she retreats into darkness and isolation. Her recovery requires community effort, celebration, curiosity, and gentle coaxing - not demands or force. The myth validates withdrawal while showing the importance of connection for healing.

Psychological: Trauma → Withdrawal → Community support → Re-engagement

Modern Reading: Depression, PTSD, recovery

🌌️

Eclipse Myth

Many scholars interpret this as a mythological explanation for solar eclipse - the sun temporarily disappearing from the sky. The myth transforms the terrifying natural phenomenon of the sun vanishing into a narrative with cause (offense), protagonists (gods), and solution (ritual). It gives human agency to an overwhelming cosmic event.

Natural Phenomenon: Solar eclipse

Mythic Function: Explains temporary loss of sun

💃

Sacred Sexuality

Uzume's erotic dance reveals the sacred power of sexuality and fertility. Her exposed body and wild dancing are not obscene but holy - expressions of life force potent enough to overcome death and darkness. This shows Shinto's generally positive view of sexuality as sacred energy rather than sin or pollution.

Theme: Sexuality as life-affirming sacred power

Function: Fertility energy defeats darkness

😂

Laughter as Sacred Medicine

The divine laughter of eight million kami is the turning point. Laughter - joy, community celebration, life affirmation - breaks through Amaterasu's isolation. The myth teaches that joy and humor are not frivolous but cosmically powerful forces that can heal grief and restore balance. Sacred need not always be solemn.

Theme: Healing power of joy and laughter

Wisdom: Celebration as sacred medicine

💎

The Mirror as Self-Recognition

Amaterasu's fascination with her own reflection represents self-recognition and remembering one's worth. Trapped in darkness and grief, she had forgotten her own brilliance. The mirror allows her to see herself as others see her - glorious, essential, beautiful. The Yata-no-Kagami becomes one of the Imperial Regalia, representing truth and self-knowledge.

Symbol: Mirror as self-knowledge

Psychological: Remembering one's worth

Spiritual: Seeing the divine in oneself

🎭

Origin of Kagura Dance

Uzume's dance becomes the prototype of kagura - sacred Shinto dance performed at shrines to entertain and honor the kami. The myth establishes that dance, music, and ritual performance are not merely human entertainment but divine activities that maintain cosmic order. Art and ritual are functionally identical in Shinto.

Origin Story: First kagura dance

Function: Sacred performance as cosmic maintenance

🪝

The Shimenawa Boundary

The sacred rope stretched across the cave entrance becomes the prototype of all shimenawa - the twisted straw ropes marking Shinto sacred spaces. These boundaries separate pure from impure, prevent pollution from entering sacred ground, and ensure that what has been restored cannot relapse. Proper boundaries maintain proper order.

Symbol: Sacred boundary marker

Function: Maintains separation of pure/impure

Wisdom: Recovery requires maintained boundaries

🌍

Community Over Individual Power

No single god could solve this crisis - not strength, not wisdom, not dance alone. The solution required the coordinated effort of multiple kami, each contributing their unique gift. The myth teaches that even cosmic problems need community solutions, that cooperation is more powerful than individual prowess.

Theme: Community cooperation over individual heroism

Lesson: Complex problems need diverse talents

🎭 Cultural Impact

💃

Kagura Sacred Dance

The myth establishes kagura as a central Shinto ritual. Sacred dances performed at shrines reenact Uzume's performance, maintaining cosmic order through ritual repetition. Kagura preserves the idea that celebration, dance, and joy are not secular entertainment but sacred cosmic maintenance activities.

Practice: Shrine kagura performances

Purpose: Honor kami, maintain cosmic order

Tradition: Continuous since ancient times

🪝

Shimenawa Sacred Ropes

Every shimenawa stretched across shrine entrances, around sacred trees, or marking sacred rocks traces its origin to this myth. These sacred boundaries are visual reminders of the rope that prevented Amaterasu from returning to darkness. They maintain purity and prevent backsliding into chaos.

Use: Shrine entrances, sacred objects, New Year

Meaning: Boundary between sacred and profane

💎

The Sacred Mirror

The Yata-no-Kagami used to lure Amaterasu became one of the Three Sacred Treasures of the Imperial Regalia, housed at Ise Grand Shrine. Mirrors in Shinto represent the presence of kami and truth - they show things as they truly are. Small mirrors in home shrines (kamidana) represent Amaterasu herself.

Imperial Treasure: Yata-no-Kagami at Ise

Home Practice: Mirrors in kamidana

Meaning: Truth, purity, divine presence

🌿

Tamagushi Offerings

The sacred sakaki tree decorated with jewels, mirror, and cloth offerings became the prototype of tamagushi - sakaki branches offered to kami at shrines. Worshippers present these during rituals, reenacting the gods' offering to Amaterasu. The practice connects every shrine visit to this cosmic myth.

Ritual Object: Sakaki branch with paper streamers

Use: Presented during shrine prayers

Origin: Divine offering to lure Amaterasu

☀️

Ise Grand Shrine

The Ise Jingu (Grand Shrine) dedicated to Amaterasu is the holiest site in Shinto, housing the sacred mirror. The shrine's importance traces directly to this myth - it is where the sun goddess's primary symbol resides. Millions of pilgrims visit annually to honor the goddess who illuminates the world.

Location: Mie Prefecture

Significance: Holiest Shinto shrine

Housing: Yata-no-Kagami (sacred mirror)

🌜

Winter Solstice Connection

Some scholars connect the myth to winter solstice celebrations - the sun's lowest point, followed by its gradual return. The Amaterasu cave myth parallels solstice myths worldwide where the sun is endangered but rescued, ensuring spring will come. The theme of light returning from darkness resonates with solar cycle observations.

Astronomical: Possible solstice myth

Pattern: Light endangered, then rescued

🎭

Japanese Theater

The myth has been adapted countless times in Japanese theater - Noh, Kabuki, and modern forms. Uzume's dance influenced performing arts, and the myth's dramatic structure (crisis, strategy, suspense, resolution) makes it naturally theatrical. It remains one of the most frequently performed mythological stories.

Arts: Noh, Kabuki, modern theater

Influence: Performance traditions, dramatic structure

📚

Modern Interpretations

Modern retellings emphasize the psychological and feminist dimensions - a goddess recovering from trauma through community support, the power of female solidarity and creativity, and resistance to male violence. The myth speaks to contemporary audiences about depression, recovery, and the importance of joy and community in healing.

Contemporary Readings: Trauma recovery, feminism

Themes: Mental health, community support
