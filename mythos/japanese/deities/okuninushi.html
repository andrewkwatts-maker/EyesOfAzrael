Great Master of the Land

The benevolent deity of nation-building, agriculture, medicine, and magic, Okuninushi is the great civilizer who shaped the land of Japan and taught humanity the arts of survival and prosperity. Through cunning, perseverance, and compassion, he overcame impossible trials to win his bride and build a nation. Though he ultimately ceded rulership of the visible world to Amaterasu's descendants, he remains the supreme lord of the unseen realm, governing the mysterious forces of fate, healing, and the afterlife.

Mythology & Stories

Okuninushi's myths chronicle the transformation of a youngest son, bullied and overlooked, into the master of the Japanese archipelago. Recorded in the Kojiki and Nihon Shoki , his tales emphasize compassion, cleverness, perseverance through trials, and the wisdom to know when to yield power for the greater good.

Family Relationships

Divine Family

  • Father: Susanoo-no-Mikoto (according to most accounts) or alternatively a sixth-generation descendant of Susanoo
  • Mother: Kushinada-hime (the princess Susanoo saved from Yamata-no-Orochi) or Kamimusubi-no-kami
  • Grandfather/Father-in-law: Susanoo-no-Mikoto - the storm god who tested him with impossible trials
  • Chief Wife: Suseri-hime (Susanoo's daughter) - helped him survive the trials and fled with him from Ne-no-Kuni
  • Other Wives: Yakami-hime (Princess of Inaba), Nunakawa-hime (Princess of Koshi), Takiri-bime-no-mikoto, and several others
  • Children: Over 180 children according to some accounts, including Kotoshironushi-no-kami (god of oracles and fishing) and Takeminakata-no-kami (god of wind and hunting)
  • Brothers: The Yasogami (eighty gods) who persecuted him in his youth
  • Divine Companion: Sukunabikona-no-kami - tiny deity who helped build the nation before departing to the eternal land

Divine Relationships

  • Ally: Sukunabikona - partner in civilizing Japan, god of medicine, hot springs, and sake brewing
  • Protected: The White Rabbit of Inaba - divine messenger whose suffering he relieved
  • Rival/Superior: Amaterasu and the heavenly kami - to whom he ceded the visible world
  • Successor in visible world: Ninigi-no-Mikoto (Amaterasu's grandson) - received rulership after Okuninushi's abdication

Worship & Sacred Sites

Izumo Taisha (Grand Shrine of Izumo)

Izumo Taisha in Shimane Prefecture is the most sacred site dedicated to Okuninushi and one of the oldest and most important shrines in Japan. According to the Kuniyuzuri myth, Amaterasu promised to build this magnificent shrine as part of the agreement for Okuninushi to cede the visible world. The main hall (honden) is the tallest shrine building in Japan, and ancient records suggest it was once even more massive, possibly reaching over 48 meters in height with enormous pillars. The shrine uses the unique taisha-zukuri architectural style, the oldest shrine construction method in Japan.

During the 10th lunar month (Kannazuki - "Month Without Gods"), all the kami of Japan are believed to gather at Izumo Taisha for a divine assembly where Okuninushi presides over determining the romantic fates and marriages for the coming year. Thus, in Izumo, the 10th month is called Kamiarizuki ("Month With Gods"), and Okuninushi is especially venerated as the deity of matchmaking and marriage.

Other Major Shrines

  • Miho Shrine (Shimane): Dedicated to Okuninushi and his wife Mihotsuhime, overlooking Miho Bay
  • Hakuto Shrine (Tottori): Located at the legendary site of the White Rabbit of Inaba story
  • Oomiwa Shrine (Nara): One of Japan's oldest shrines, identifies Okuninushi with Omononushi, the mountain deity
  • Keta Taisha (Ishikawa): Major shrine in the Hokuriku region honoring Okuninushi
  • Numerous regional shrines: As the nation-builder, Okuninushi is honored throughout Japan, particularly in agricultural and coastal communities

Festivals

  • Kamiari-sai (November): The Divine Assembly festival at Izumo Taisha welcoming the gathering of all kami
  • Reisai (May 14-16): The Grand Festival of Izumo Taisha with processions and sacred performances
  • Shinjiko Festival (August): Celebrating Okuninushi's nation-building and blessing of the harvest
  • Marriage Blessing Ceremonies: Year-round rituals for couples seeking Okuninushi's blessing for successful marriages

Worship Practices

Worship of Okuninushi emphasizes gratitude for civilization's gifts - agriculture, medicine, and social harmony. Uniquely at Izumo Taisha, devotees perform four claps instead of the standard two during prayer (two claps to call the kami, two to express gratitude). Offerings include rice, sake, salt, and medicinal herbs. Couples seeking marriage blessings or marital harmony frequently visit Izumo Taisha, as Okuninushi governs the invisible threads of fate that bind people together. Farmers pray to him for abundant harvests, healers invoke him for medical knowledge, and merchants seek his blessing for prosperity in commerce. The ritual of "musubi" (divine connection and binding) is central to his worship, reflecting his role in creating harmonious relationships between humans, nature, and the divine.
