The Great Prostitute Seated on Many Waters

The apocalyptic imagery of Revelation 17-18—the "Whore of Babylon" seated on a scarlet beast, drunk with the blood of the saints, adorned in purple and scarlet and gold—finds one of its most powerful precursors in the figure of Ishtar, the Mesopotamian goddess of love, sex, fertility, and war. As patron goddess of Babylon, Ishtar presided over temple prostitution, sacred sexual rites, and the intoxicating power of both erotic desire and military conquest.

When John the Revelator envisions "Babylon the Great, mother of prostitutes and of earth's abominations," he draws on centuries of prophetic tradition that personified idolatrous cities as harlots—but the ultimate referent is the historical and spiritual reality of Babylon itself, where Ishtar reigned supreme, where the Ishtar Gate stood as monument to her glory, and where sacred prostitution was woven into the very fabric of religious and economic life.

Ishtar/Inanna: The Goddess of Babylon

Divine Domains

  • Love and Sexual Desire: Goddess of erotic love, seduction, fertility
  • War and Conquest: Patron of warriors, "Lady of Battle"
  • Political Power: Divine authority behind kingship
  • Sacred Prostitution: Temple prostitutes served her cult
  • Death and Resurrection: Descent to underworld and return (fertility cycle)

Babylonian Presence

  • Ishtar Gate: Magnificent entrance to Babylon (blue glazed brick, dragon/bull reliefs)
  • Temples: Multiple shrines throughout city
  • Processional Way: Sacred route to her temples
  • Economic Power: Temple prostitution generated revenue
  • Political Authority: Kings claimed her favor, performed sacred marriage rite
"I am Ishtar of Arbela. I will go before and behind you. Fear not! You are paralyzed, but in the midst of woe I will rise and sit [as protector]."
— Ishtar Oracle to Esarhaddon, Neo-Assyrian period

Tablet VI: Gilgamesh Rejects Ishtar

Ishtar's Proposal and Gilgamesh's Refusal

The Proposal (Tablet VI, lines 1-21):

After Gilgamesh and Enkidu slay Humbaba (guardian of the Cedar Forest), Gilgamesh cleanses himself, puts on his royal regalia, and crowns himself. The goddess Ishtar sees his beauty and desires him:

"Come, Gilgamesh, be my lover! Grant me the gift of your fruit! You shall be my husband, and I shall be your wife. I shall harness for you a chariot of lapis lazuli and gold... Kings, rulers, and princes shall bow down before you... Your goats shall bear triplets, your ewes twins..."
— Epic of Gilgamesh, Tablet VI

Gilgamesh's Rejection (lines 22-79):

Gilgamesh refuses in the most scathing terms, recounting the terrible fates of Ishtar's previous lovers. Each one she destroyed after tiring of them:

The Fates of Ishtar's Lovers

Tammuz/Dumuzi (shepherd god): "For Tammuz, the lover of your youth, you decreed wailing year after year." (Condemned to die annually, descent to underworld—dying-rising god cycle.)
The Many-Colored Roller Bird: "You loved him, yet you struck him and broke his wing. Now he stands in the woods crying 'My wing!'"
The Lion: "You loved the lion, perfect in strength, yet you dug for him seven and seven pits."
The Stallion: "You loved the stallion, eager for the fray, yet you ordained for him the whip, the goad, and the lash, you ordained for him to gallop seven leagues, you ordained for him to drink muddy water."
The Shepherd: "You loved the shepherd, the herdsman, who constantly poured out for you the glowing ashes [incense], who daily slaughtered kids for you; yet you struck him and turned him into a wolf, so that his own herd boys drive him away, and his own dogs bite his thighs."
Ishullanu, Your Father's Gardener: "He brought you baskets of dates every day... You cast your eye on him and went to him: 'My Ishullanu, let us taste your strength!' But he said, 'What do you want of me? Has my mother not baked? Have I not eaten?'... When you heard this, you struck him and turned him into a frog/mole, left to dwell in the middle of his garden."
"And me! You will love me and then treat me just like them!"
— Gilgamesh's conclusion (Tablet VI)

Ishtar's Rage and Revenge:

  • Ishtar, furious at the rejection, goes to her father Anu (sky god)
  • Demands he send the Bull of Heaven to destroy Gilgamesh
  • Threatens to break down the gates of the underworld and release the dead if refused
  • Anu reluctantly gives her the Bull of Heaven
  • Gilgamesh and Enkidu fight and kill the Bull of Heaven
  • Enkidu throws the Bull's thigh at Ishtar in contempt
  • The gods decree Enkidu must die for this blasphemy (Tablet VII)

Theological Significance: Gilgamesh recognizes Ishtar's pattern: seduction → consumption → destruction. He refuses to become another victim. This rejection of the false goddess prefigures the Biblical pattern of rejecting Canaanite Baals and Asherahs, and ultimately, Revelation's judgment on the Whore of Babylon.

Revelation 18: The Fall of Babylon

"After this I saw another angel coming down from heaven, having great authority, and the earth was made bright with his glory. And he called out with a mighty voice, 'Fallen, fallen is Babylon the great! She has become a dwelling place for demons, a haunt for every unclean spirit, a haunt for every unclean bird, a haunt for every unclean and detestable beast.'"
— Revelation 18:1-2 (ESV, echoing Isaiah 21:9)

Sexual Immorality (18:3)

"All nations have drunk the wine of the passion of her sexual immorality, and the kings of the earth have committed immorality with her."

Ishtar parallel: Temple prostitution, sacred sexual rites, seduction of kings.

Economic Wealth (18:3, 11-19)

"The merchants of the earth have grown rich from the power of her luxurious living... The merchants of the earth weep and mourn for her, since no one buys their cargo anymore."

Babylon parallel: Trade empire, Ishtar temples as economic centers, luxury goods.

Dwelling of Demons (18:2)

"She has become a dwelling place for demons, a haunt for every unclean spirit."

Ishtar parallel: Ishtar's temples, cult prostitutes, pagan worship as demon habitation.

Blood of Prophets (18:24)

"In her was found the blood of prophets and of saints, and of all who have been slain on earth."

Babylon parallel: Destruction of Jerusalem (586 BCE), Babylonian exile, persecution.

Sudden Destruction (18:8)

"For this reason her plagues will come in a single day, death and mourning and famine, and she will be burned up with fire."

Historical parallel: Babylon's fall to Persia (539 BCE), prophesied in Isaiah, Jeremiah.

No More Music (18:22)

"The sound of harpists and musicians, of flute players and trumpeters, will be heard in you no more."

Ishtar parallel: Temple rituals with music, festivals, sacred marriage ceremony silenced.

"Come out of her, my people, lest you take part in her sins, lest you share in her plagues; for her sins are heaped high as heaven, and God has remembered her iniquities."
— Revelation 18:4-5 (echoing Jeremiah 51:6, 9, 45)

Theological Synthesis: The Three Babylons

Revelation's "Babylon the Great" operates on three levels simultaneously, all of which connect back to Ishtar and the historical Mesopotamian empire:

1. Literal Babylon

  • Historical Mesopotamian empire (2000-539 BCE)
  • Ishtar worship, Ishtar Gate, temple prostitution
  • Conquered Israel/Judah (586 BCE)
  • Destroyed Jerusalem temple, exiled Jews
  • Prophecies: Isaiah 13-14, 21; Jeremiah 50-51
  • Fell to Persia in 539 BCE (Daniel 5)

2. Symbolic Babylon (Rome)

  • First-century Rome in John's context
  • Persecuted Christians, martyred saints
  • Imperial cult (emperor worship) = false religion
  • "Seven hills" (17:9) = Rome's seven hills
  • Economic/military empire like Babylon
  • Will fall like Babylon fell

3. Archetypal Babylon

  • All corrupt religious-political-economic systems
  • Satanic counterpart to New Jerusalem
  • Seductive, wealthy, violent, idolatrous
  • Final manifestation in end times (Antichrist system)
  • Ultimate judgment of all false religion
  • Ishtar = archetype of false goddess/whore

Ishtar as Archetypal Pattern: Ishtar embodies the ultimate false goddess—sexually seductive yet violently destructive, promising blessing but delivering death, demanding worship through prostitution, and destroying those who serve her. Revelation's "Whore of Babylon" is Ishtar writ large across all history: the personification of satanic religious-political power that seduces the nations, intoxicates with false worship, and ultimately faces divine judgment.

The Whore vs. The Bride: Ultimate Contrast

Revelation presents two women: the Whore of Babylon (Rev 17) and the Bride of Christ (Rev 19:7-9, 21:2, 9-10). They are opposites in every way:

The Whore (Babylon/Ishtar)

  • Name: "Babylon the Great, mother of prostitutes" (17:5)
  • Attire: Purple, scarlet, gold, jewels, pearls (17:4) — gaudy, seductive
  • Cup: Golden cup full of abominations and impurities (17:4)
  • Nature: Prostitute, adulterous, unfaithful
  • Relationship: "Committed sexual immorality with kings" (17:2)
  • Dwelling: Seated on beast (17:3), many waters (17:1) = chaos
  • Character: Drunk with blood of saints (17:6), violent, oppressive
  • Fate: "Made desolate and naked... devoured... burned with fire" (17:16, 18:8)
  • Source: Satanic (beast = Antichrist system, 17:3)
  • Prototype: Ishtar, pagan goddesses, false religion

The Bride (New Jerusalem/Church)

  • Name: "The wife of the Lamb," "the holy city Jerusalem" (21:9-10)
  • Attire: Fine linen, bright and pure (19:8) — righteous deeds of saints
  • Glory: Radiance like jasper, clear as crystal (21:11), glory of God
  • Nature: Bride, virgin, faithful
  • Relationship: "Marriage of the Lamb" (19:7), union with Christ
  • Dwelling: Descends from heaven (21:2) = divine origin
  • Character: "Bride has made herself ready" (19:7), pure, holy
  • Fate: "God will dwell with them" (21:3), eternal blessing, no more tears
  • Source: Divine (from God out of heaven, 21:2)
  • Prototype: Faithful Israel, purified Church, eternal covenant

Where Ishtar/Whore of Babylon seduces, exploits, and destroys, the Bride of Christ loves, serves, and redeems. Where Babylon falls to eternal judgment, New Jerusalem descends to eternal glory. The choice before humanity: Which city? Which woman? Which worship?
