Love Surpassing the Love of Women

Among the most poignant parallels between the Epic of Gilgamesh and Biblical narratives is the theme of intense male friendship—a covenant bond that transcends ordinary relationships and proves stronger than death itself. Gilgamesh and Enkidu's friendship, forged in wrestling combat and sealed in shared heroic exploits, ends in devastating grief when Enkidu dies. The Biblical friendship of David and Jonathan, similarly intense and covenantal, also ends in death and lament.

Both narratives use remarkably similar language to describe these bonds: Gilgamesh says he "loved [Enkidu] like a wife," while David declares Jonathan's love "surpassing the love of women" (2 Samuel 1:26). Both friendships involve covenant loyalty, mutual sacrifice, shared dangers, and profound mourning. These parallels reveal ancient Near Eastern traditions of covenant brotherhood—bonds so deep they rival or exceed marital love.

Gilgamesh and Enkidu (Tablets I-VIII)

Meeting and Brotherhood (Tablet II):

"They seized each other, they crouched like wrestlers, they shattered the doorpost, the wall shook. They seized each other at the door of the house of brides, they fought in the street, in the square of the land... Gilgamesh knelt, one foot on the ground, his fury abated, he turned away. When he turned away, Enkidu to him spoke, to Gilgamesh: 'As one unique your mother bore you, the wild cow of the fold, Ninsun, exalted you above all other men!'"
— Epic of Gilgamesh, Tablet II (wrestling match and recognition)

Their Bond:

  • Origin: Wrestling match—Enkidu blocks Gilgamesh from taking a bride. Fight to a draw.
  • Recognition: Mutual respect, see each other as equals (both superhuman strength)
  • Embrace: Kiss and embrace after wrestling—instant brotherhood
  • Shared Exploits: Together defeat Humbaba (Cedar Forest guardian), kill Bull of Heaven sent by Ishtar
  • Mutual Dependence: Encourage each other, face fears together

Gilgamesh's Love for Enkidu:

"The dream I had, my mother, I loved him like a wife, I caressed him and fondled him, I took him and made him my brother."
— Tablet I (Gilgamesh's dream interpreted by Ninsun)

Enkidu's Death and Gilgamesh's Grief (Tablets VII-VIII):

  • Divine Punishment: Gods decree Enkidu must die for killing Bull of Heaven (Tablet VII)
  • Slow Death: Enkidu ill for twelve days, dreams of underworld, dies in Gilgamesh's presence
  • Denial: Gilgamesh refuses to bury Enkidu for seven days, touches his heart, waits for him to wake
  • Lament: Elaborate funeral, offerings to underworld gods, public mourning
  • Despair: "Shall I not die also? Am I not like Enkidu?" Triggers quest for immortality
"Enkidu, my friend, whom I loved so dearly, who with me went through every hardship, the fate of mortals has overtaken him! Six days I wept for him and seven nights: I did not surrender his body for burial until a maggot dropped from his nostril. Then I was afraid that I too would die, I grew fearful of death, so I wandered the wild."
— Tablet X (Gilgamesh to Siduri)

David and Jonathan (1 Samuel 18-20, 2 Samuel 1)

Meeting and Covenant (1 Samuel 18):

"As soon as he had finished speaking to Saul, the soul of Jonathan was knit to the soul of David, and Jonathan loved him as his own soul. And Saul took him that day and would not let him return to his father's house. Then Jonathan made a covenant with David, because he loved him as his own soul. And Jonathan stripped himself of the robe that was on him and gave it to David, and his armor, and even his sword and his bow and his belt."
— 1 Samuel 18:1-4 (ESV)

Their Bond:

  • Origin: After David defeats Goliath, Jonathan's soul "knit" to David's
  • Covenant: Jonathan makes formal covenant with David (1 Sam 18:3, 20:16-17)
  • Gift-Giving: Jonathan gives robe, armor, sword, bow, belt—symbols of status, power, identity
  • Mutual Loyalty: Jonathan protects David from Saul's murderous jealousy (1 Sam 19-20)
  • Sacrifice: Jonathan risks father's wrath, gives up throne rights for David

Jonathan's Loyalty Despite Conflict:

"Jonathan spoke well of David to Saul his father and said to him, 'Let not the king sin against his servant David, because he has not sinned against you, and because his deeds have brought good to you... Why then will you sin against innocent blood by killing David without cause?'"
— 1 Samuel 19:4-5 (ESV)

Jonathan's Death and David's Lament (2 Samuel 1):

  • Battle Death: Jonathan killed with Saul at Mt. Gilboa by Philistines (1 Sam 31)
  • David Receives News: Amalekite brings report, expects reward, David has him executed for touching "LORD's anointed"
  • Public Mourning: David tears clothes, mourns, fasts until evening (2 Sam 1:11-12)
  • Lament Composed: "Song of the Bow," taught to people of Judah (2 Sam 1:17-27)
  • Covenant Honored: David later seeks Jonathan's descendants, finds Mephibosheth, shows kindness "for Jonathan's sake" (2 Sam 9)
"I am distressed for you, my brother Jonathan; very pleasant have you been to me; your love to me was extraordinary, surpassing the love of women. How the mighty have fallen, and the weapons of war perished!"
— 2 Samuel 1:26-27 (ESV, David's lament)

The Laments: Parallel Expressions of Grief

Gilgamesh's Lament for Enkidu (Tablet VIII)

"Hear me, O young men, listen to me! Hear me, O elders of Uruk, listen to me! I mourn my friend Enkidu, I weep for him bitterly, like a wailing woman. He was the axe at my side in which my arm trusted, the sword at my belt, the shield before me, my festive garment, my belt of joy. An evil fate rose up and robbed me of him! My friend, my little brother, who chased the wild ass of the hills and the panther of the steppe, Enkidu my friend, my little brother, who chased the wild ass of the hills and the panther of the steppe, we who met and scaled the mountain, who seized and slew the Bull of Heaven, who felled Humbaba that dwelt in the Forest of Cedar: my friend whom I loved so dearly, who with me went through every hardship—the fate of mortals has overtaken him!"
— Epic of Gilgamesh, Tablet VIII

Elements:

  • Public address: "Hear me, O young men... O elders"
  • Weeping "like a wailing woman"
  • Metaphors: axe, sword, shield, festive garment
  • Recounts shared exploits: Bull of Heaven, Humbaba
  • Repeats "my friend whom I loved"
  • Laments mortality: "fate of mortals has overtaken him"

David's Lament for Jonathan (2 Samuel 1:17-27)

"Your glory, O Israel, is slain on your high places! How the mighty have fallen! Tell it not in Gath, publish it not in the streets of Ashkelon, lest the daughters of the Philistines rejoice, lest the daughters of the uncircumcised exult... How the mighty have fallen in the midst of the battle! Jonathan lies slain on your high places. I am distressed for you, my brother Jonathan; very pleasant have you been to me; your love to me was extraordinary, surpassing the love of women. How the mighty have fallen, and the weapons of war perished!"
— 2 Samuel 1:19-27 (ESV, excerpts)

Elements:

  • Public lament, taught to people of Judah (2 Sam 1:18)
  • Refrain: "How the mighty have fallen!" (repeated 3x)
  • Metaphor: weapons of war perished
  • Addresses Jonathan directly: "my brother Jonathan"
  • Declares Jonathan's love "surpassing the love of women"
  • Mourns national loss (Saul + Jonathan together)

Parallel Themes:

  • Both are public, formal laments (not private grief)
  • Both recount shared history/exploits
  • Both use refrains and poetic structure
  • Both compare friendship to other forms of love (wife/women)
  • Both express profound, inconsolable grief
  • Both honor the dead friend's memory publicly

Covenant Brotherhood in Ancient Near Eastern Context

Both the Gilgamesh epic and the David-Jonathan narrative reflect ancient Near Eastern traditions of covenant brotherhood—formal bonds of loyalty that transcend ordinary friendship and carry legal/religious weight.

Ancient Near Eastern Covenant Features:

  • Formal Agreement: Covenant involves mutual oath, not mere affection
  • Symbolic Acts: Exchange of gifts, garments, weapons (David-Jonathan); shared exploits (Gilgamesh-Enkidu)
  • Kinship Language: "Brother," "my soul," etc.—indicates family-level bond
  • Loyalty to Death: Covenant survives even death (David honors Mephibosheth "for Jonathan's sake")
  • Public Recognition: Community acknowledges the bond (Gilgamesh's public lament, David's song taught to Judah)
  • Sacrifice Expected: Willingness to die for covenant partner, renounce personal gain

Theological Difference: Human vs. Divine Covenant

Gilgamesh-Enkidu: Purely human covenant. No divine sanction or involvement. Ends in death, which Gilgamesh cannot overcome. Friendship does not transcend mortality.

David-Jonathan: Human covenant within divine covenant framework. Jonathan recognizes God's election of David: "The LORD shall be between me and you, and between my offspring and your offspring, forever" (1 Sam 20:42). Covenant grounded in YHWH's sovereignty, thus endures beyond death into descendants.

Ultimate Fulfillment: Christ's "Greater love has no one than this, that someone lay down his life for his friends" (John 15:13). Where Enkidu dies and Gilgamesh can't save him, and Jonathan dies leaving David to mourn, Christ voluntarily lays down His life and rises again, offering eternal covenant to all who believe. He is both the friend who dies and the friend who conquers death.
