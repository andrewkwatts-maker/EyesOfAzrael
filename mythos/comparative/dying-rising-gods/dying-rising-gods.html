Dying and Rising Gods

Osiris, Jesus, and the Ancient Near Eastern Resurrection Mythology Pattern

Introduction: The Death-and-Rebirth Archetype

Across ancient cultures, myths tell of deities who die and return to life. These "dying-and-rising gods" typically embody vegetation cycles, seasonal renewal, or cosmic order restored after chaos. Osiris (Egypt), Tammuz/Dumuzi (Mesopotamia), Baal (Canaan), Attis (Phrygia), Adonis (Greece/Phoenicia), and Dionysus (Greece) share common patterns. The resurrection of Jesus Christ in Christian theology bears superficial similarities, leading to centuries of debate: Did Christianity borrow from pagan myths? Are these universal archetypes? Or does Jesus's resurrection differ fundamentally in nature and theological implication?

Osiris: Egyptian Lord of the Underworld

Osiris (Wsir, Ausar) Egyptian (c. 2400 BCE - Roman Era)

The Myth of Osiris's Death and "Resurrection"

The Story (from Plutarch's "Isis and Osiris" and Pyramid Texts)

  1. Benevolent King: Osiris rules Egypt, teaches agriculture, civilization, and laws. Beloved by humanity.
  2. Murder by Set: Osiris's jealous brother Set tricks him into a coffin, seals it, and throws it into the Nile.
  3. Isis's Search: Osiris's wife/sister Isis searches for the body, finds it lodged in a tamarisk tree in Byblos (Lebanon).
  4. Dismemberment: Set discovers the recovered body, dismembers Osiris into 14 (or 42) pieces, scattering them across Egypt.
  5. Reconstruction: Isis gathers all pieces except the phallus (eaten by fish), creates a golden replacement.
  6. Magical Revivification: Isis uses heka (magic) to temporarily restore Osiris to life, conceives Horus.
  7. Descent to Duat: Osiris cannot remain in the land of the living; he descends to the Duat (underworld) to become Lord of the Dead.
  8. Judgment Role: Osiris presides over the Hall of Maat, weighing hearts of the deceased against the feather of truth.

Nature of "Resurrection"

  • Not bodily return to earthly life
  • Becomes ruler of underworld, not this world
  • Remains dead but reanimated in Duat
  • Mummified, preserved, but not restored to living state

Symbolic Significance

  • Death and rebirth of Nile (annual flood cycle)
  • Vegetation god (grain dies, is buried, sprouts anew)
  • Cosmic order: Death is passage, not end
  • Pharaohs become Osiris at death

Ritual Reenactment

  • Annual festivals reenact death and revival
  • Mystery rites at Abydos (Osiris's cult center)
  • Cyclical, repeatable myth
  • Not historical event but timeless pattern

Key Sources

  • Pyramid Texts (c. 2400-2300 BCE): Earliest references, spells for pharaoh's afterlife identification with Osiris
  • Coffin Texts (c. 2100-1800 BCE): Democratization of Osiris myth for non-royal dead
  • Book of the Dead (c. 1550 BCE onward): Spells for navigating Duat, judgment before Osiris
  • Plutarch, "Isis and Osiris" (c. 100 CE): Most complete narrative version (late, Hellenistic influence)

Jesus Christ: Christian Resurrection

Jesus Christ (Yeshua HaMashiach) Christian (c. 30-33 CE)

The Resurrection Narrative

The Story (from Four Gospels and Acts)

  1. Crucifixion: Jesus, condemned by Pontius Pilate, is crucified at Golgotha (c. 30-33 CE) (Matt 27, Mark 15, Luke 23, John 19).
  2. Death Confirmed: Roman soldiers verify death; spear pierces side, blood and water flow (John 19:34).
  3. Burial: Joseph of Arimathea places body in new tomb, sealed with stone, guarded by soldiers (Matt 27:57-66).
  4. Three Days: Jesus remains in tomb from Friday evening to Sunday morning.
  5. Empty Tomb: Women find tomb empty, angels announce "He is risen" (Matt 28:1-7, Mark 16:1-7, Luke 24:1-8, John 20:1-10).
  6. Post-Resurrection Appearances: Jesus appears to Mary Magdalene, disciples, over 500 witnesses over 40 days (1 Cor 15:3-8).
  7. Physical Body: Jesus eats fish, invites Thomas to touch wounds, demonstrates corporeal resurrection (Luke 24:36-43, John 20:24-29).
  8. Ascension: Jesus ascends to heaven 40 days post-resurrection (Acts 1:9-11).
  9. Eschatological Return: Will return to judge living and dead at end of age (Matt 25:31-46, Rev 20:11-15).

Nature of Resurrection

  • Bodily, physical resurrection (not just spirit)
  • Transformed body (passes through walls, yet tangible)
  • Return to earthly realm before ascending
  • One-time historical event, not cyclical

Theological Significance

  • Defeat of death itself (not seasonal cycle)
  • "Firstfruits" of general resurrection (1 Cor 15:20-23)
  • Redemption of humanity from sin
  • Validates Jesus's divine claims

Historical Claims

  • Eyewitness testimony emphasized (Luke 1:1-4, 1 Cor 15:3-8)
  • Specific time and place (Pontius Pilate, Jerusalem)
  • Willing to die for claim (martyrdom of apostles)
  • Empty tomb never disputed by opponents

Key Sources

  • Gospel of Mark (c. 65-70 CE): Earliest Gospel, ends with empty tomb and angel's announcement
  • 1 Corinthians 15 (c. 53-55 CE): Paul's "creed" (vv. 3-8), earliest written resurrection account
  • Gospels of Matthew, Luke, John (c. 70-100 CE): Expanded resurrection appearances, physical details
  • Acts of the Apostles (c. 80-90 CE): Post-resurrection ministry, ascension

Other Dying-and-Rising Gods: Comparative Survey

Tammuz / Dumuzi Mesopotamian (Sumerian/Babylonian)

Myth: Dumuzi (Sumerian) / Tammuz (Babylonian) is a shepherd-god and fertility deity. After his death, he descends to the underworld (Kur). His wife/lover Inanna/Ishtar negotiates his periodic return (6 months in underworld, 6 months on earth), explaining seasonal cycles.

Death: Killed by demons sent by underworld goddess Ereshkigal (Inanna's sister).

Resurrection: Periodic return from underworld; seasonal, cyclical.

Significance: Vegetation god; death = dry season, return = spring/harvest.

Biblical Reference: Ezekiel 8:14 condemns "women weeping for Tammuz" as idolatry in Jerusalem temple.

Baal (Hadad) Canaanite / Ugaritic

Myth: Baal, storm and fertility god, battles Mot (Death/Sterility). Mot kills Baal, who descends to the underworld. Baal's sister Anat searches for him, defeats Mot, and Baal returns to life, bringing rains and fertility.

Death: Swallowed by Mot (god of death and drought).

Resurrection: Anat defeats Mot; Baal rises, storms return, vegetation blooms.

Significance: Cyclical: rainy season (Baal alive) vs. dry season (Baal dead).

Source: Baal Cycle (Ugaritic texts, c. 1400-1200 BCE).

Attis Phrygian (Asia Minor)

Myth: Attis, consort of Cybele (Great Mother goddess), dies (various versions: self-castration, gored by boar, killed by jealous Cybele). Cybele mourns him; Attis is resurrected or transformed (into pine tree or immortal).

Death: Dies beneath a pine tree (self-inflicted or by boar).

Resurrection: Annual resurrection celebrated in spring festival (March 22-25).

Significance: Vegetation god; death-and-rebirth linked to seasons and fertility.

Mystery Cult: Initiates participated in ritual death and rebirth; possible influence on early Christian environment.

Adonis Greek / Phoenician

Myth: Adonis, beloved of Aphrodite, is gored by a boar while hunting. He dies, descends to Hades. Aphrodite mourns; Zeus/Hades allows Adonis to return for part of the year (typically spring/summer), then descend again (fall/winter).

Death: Killed by boar (sent by jealous Ares or Artemis, depending on version).

Resurrection: Annual return from underworld; cyclical.

Significance: Vegetation deity; anemone flower sprouts from his blood; seasonal myth.

Source: Greek poets (Ovid's Metamorphoses, Bion's Lament for Adonis).

Dionysus (Bacchus) Greek / Roman

Myth: Dionysus, god of wine, fertility, and ecstasy, is dismembered by Titans as an infant (Zagreus form). His heart is saved by Athena; Zeus swallows it and Dionysus is reborn from Zeus's thigh (or Semele's womb in another version).

Death: Titans tear infant Dionysus apart, boil and consume him.

Resurrection: Zeus resurrects him; Dionysus becomes immortal god of wine and revelry.

Significance: Mystery cult centered on death, rebirth, intoxication, divine madness; initiates ritually consumed wine (Dionysus's "blood").

Note: Orphic mystery religion made Dionysus central; some scholars see parallels to Eucharist (wine as divine essence).

Did Christianity Borrow from Dying-and-Rising God Myths?

The Case for Borrowing (Religionsgeschichtliche Schule)

  • Hellenistic Environment: Early Christianity spread in a world saturated with mystery religions (Isis-Osiris, Cybele-Attis, Dionysian mysteries)
  • Surface Similarities: Death, resurrection, salvation, ritual meals, initiation rites
  • Syncretism: Religious ideas freely mixed in Greco-Roman world
  • Scholarly Proponents: 19th-20th century "History of Religions School" (e.g., Richard Reitzenstein, Wilhelm Bousset) argued for heavy pagan influence

The Case Against Borrowing (Current Scholarly Consensus)

1. Chronological Issues

  • Most detailed dying-and-rising god myths post-date Christianity (e.g., full Attis resurrection myth develops 2nd-3rd century CE)
  • Isis-Osiris cult's "resurrection" motif develops gradually; Plutarch's version (100 CE) is late
  • Mystery religions may have borrowed from Christianity, not vice versa

2. Fundamental Theological Differences

  • Cyclical vs. Linear: Pagan myths explain recurring seasons; Jesus's resurrection is one-time historical event
  • Underworld vs. Heaven: Osiris remains dead, rules underworld; Jesus ascends, reigns in heaven
  • Myth vs. History: Pagan gods operate in timeless myth; Jesus in documented history (Pilate, Herod, etc.)
  • Bodily vs. Spiritual: Most pagan resurrections are spiritual/periodic; Jesus's is physical, permanent

3. Jewish Roots, Not Pagan

  • Resurrection hope rooted in Hebrew Bible (Job 19:25-27, Daniel 12:2, Isaiah 26:19)
  • Pharisaic Judaism already believed in bodily resurrection (Acts 23:6-8)
  • Jesus's resurrection fulfills Jewish messianic/prophetic expectation, not pagan fertility myth
  • Early Christians were Jews hostile to paganism, unlikely to borrow from mystery cults

4. The "Dying-and-Rising God" Category Itself is Questionable

  • Modern scholarship (e.g., Jonathan Z. Smith, Mark S. Smith) questions whether "dying-and-rising god" is valid category
  • Osiris doesn't "rise" (stays dead in underworld)
  • Tammuz, Baal, Adonis have seasonal disappearances/returns, not resurrections
  • Only Attis and Dionysus have true "resurrection," but evidence is late
  • Category may be scholarly construct, not ancient reality

Current Scholarly Consensus

Most contemporary New Testament scholars (even non-Christians like Bart Ehrman, E.P. Sanders) reject the "Christianity borrowed from mystery religions" thesis as overstated. While early Christians used language familiar in their Hellenistic context, the core resurrection claim is rooted in Jewish apocalyptic hope and the specific historical claim that Jesus of Nazareth rose bodily from the dead.

C.S. Lewis: "Myth Became Fact"

Archetypal Interpretation

C.S. Lewis, in his essay "Myth Became Fact" (1944), offered a mediating view:

"The heart of Christianity is a myth which is also a fact. The old myth of the Dying God, without ceasing to be myth, comes down from the heaven of legend and imagination to the earth of history. It happens—at a particular date, in a particular place, followed by definable historical consequences. We pass from a Balder or an Osiris, dying nobody knows when or where, to a historical Person crucified (it is all in order) under Pontius Pilate. By becoming fact it does not cease to be myth: that is the miracle."

Lewis's Argument:

  • Pagan myths express deep human longing for death defeated, life renewed, divine-human union
  • These are "good dreams" sent by God, preparing humanity for the reality
  • In Jesus, the archetype became historical event: "myth became fact"
  • Christianity doesn't borrow from myths but fulfills them
  • The similarities point to universal human hope; the differences show how Christ transcends and completes the pattern

This view respects both the parallels (archetypal patterns) and the distinctions (historical, bodily, one-time event).

Theological Implications: Why Jesus's Resurrection Differs

Unique Claims of Christian Resurrection

  1. Firstfruits of General Resurrection: Jesus's resurrection inaugurates the final resurrection of all believers (1 Cor 15:20-23)—not just seasonal renewal but eschatological transformation.
  2. Defeat of Death Itself: "Death is swallowed up in victory" (1 Cor 15:54). Not accommodation to death (as Osiris in underworld) but victory over it.
  3. Validation of Divine Claims: Jesus claimed to be God incarnate; resurrection validates this (Rom 1:4). Pagan gods were always divine; Jesus became human, died, and was vindicated.
  4. Redemption from Sin: "Raised for our justification" (Rom 4:25). Not just explaining nature but redeeming humanity.
  5. New Creation: Resurrection is "new creation" (2 Cor 5:17), not restoration of old cycle. Linear, not cyclical.
  6. Historical Verifiability Claimed: Paul appeals to 500+ witnesses, most still alive when he wrote (1 Cor 15:6). Invites investigation, not ritual participation.

Conclusion: Archetype and Fulfillment

The dying-and-rising god motif appears across ancient cultures because death and the hope for life beyond it are universal human concerns. Osiris, Tammuz, Baal, Attis, Adonis, and Dionysus express this hope through seasonal, cyclical, mythological narratives tied to vegetation, fertility, and cosmic order.

Jesus's resurrection stands apart in crucial ways: it is claimed as one-time historical fact, bodily and physical, eschatological rather than cyclical, rooted in Jewish monotheism rather than pagan polytheism, and centered on redemption from sin rather than explanation of natural cycles.

Whether one interprets the parallels as cultural borrowing (unlikely), universal archetypes (plausible), or divine preparation culminating in Christ (C.S. Lewis's view), the distinctions remain profound. Osiris never left the underworld. Jesus walked out of the tomb. That difference—historical, theological, existential—defines Christianity's unique claim: "Christ is risen. He is risen indeed."
