Hanuman

The Monkey God, Supreme Devotee, Divine Servant

The divine vanara (monkey) warrior and supreme devotee of Rama, embodying perfect devotion, courage, strength, and selfless service. Born as the son of the wind god Vayu, Hanuman possesses supernatural powers including flight, shape-shifting, and immense strength. His unwavering dedication to Rama and his heroic exploits in the Ramayana make him one of the most beloved deities in Hinduism - the living symbol of bhakti (devotion) and the power that comes from complete surrender to the divine.

Mythology & Stories

Hanuman is central to the Ramayana epic and has become one of Hinduism's most worshipped deities. Unlike most gods who are worshipped for granting boons, Hanuman is revered for his character - his absolute devotion, his humility despite possessing godlike powers, his courage in the face of any danger, and his selfless service. He represents the ideal devotee who has no ego, no personal desires, existing only to serve his beloved lord. His worship transcends sectarian boundaries - both Vaishnavas and Shaivas revere him, and he is invoked by people of all backgrounds for strength, courage, and protection.

Key Myths & Exploits:

  • Divine Birth: Hanuman was born to Anjana (an apsara cursed to be born as a vanara) and Kesari (a vanara king), but his true father was Vayu, the wind god. When Anjana prayed intensely to Shiva for a child, the wind god Vayu carried the divine essence to her womb. Thus Hanuman is considered an avatar or son of Shiva in some traditions, and son of Vayu in all traditions. He was born with incredible strength and supernatural abilities, an auspicious birth foretold to be crucial for dharma's victory.
  • Swallowing the Sun: As a child, Hanuman was mischievous and unaware of his divine nature. Seeing the rising sun, he mistook it for a ripe fruit and leaped into the sky to eat it. Indra, seeing this threat to cosmic order, struck the child with his thunderbolt, injuring Hanuman's jaw (hence his name, which can mean "one with a broken jaw"). Enraged, Vayu withdrew all air from the world, suffocating all beings. The gods quickly apologized and granted young Hanuman numerous boons: immortality from Brahma, invulnerability in battle from Indra, and various powers from other deities. However, due to his mischief, he was cursed to forget his powers until reminded of them at the right time.
  • Meeting Rama - The Purpose Revealed: When Rama and Lakshmana searched for the abducted Sita, they met Hanuman on Mount Rishyamukha. The moment Hanuman beheld Rama, his life found its purpose. He instantly recognized him as his lord and master, and devoted himself completely to Rama's service. This meeting represents the soul (Hanuman) recognizing the divine (Rama) and surrendering completely. From this moment, Hanuman existed only to serve Rama, finding in this service his ultimate fulfillment.
  • The Leap to Lanka: When no one could cross the vast ocean to Lanka to find Sita, the vanaras despaired. Jambavan reminded Hanuman of his forgotten powers. Suddenly remembering his divine strength, Hanuman grew to enormous size and leaped across the ocean in one mighty bound - a distance of 100 yojanas (about 800 miles). This leap is one of the most iconic images in Hindu iconography, symbolizing the power that comes when one acts in service of the divine. During his flight, he was tested by various beings but overcame all obstacles through wit and strength.
  • Finding Sita - The Perfect Messenger: In Lanka, Hanuman made himself tiny to search undetected. He found Sita in the Ashoka grove, gave her Rama's ring as proof, and offered to carry her back. But Sita refused - she wanted Rama himself to rescue her and defeat Ravana, upholding dharma. Hanuman understood this wisdom perfectly. His role was not to solve the problem himself, but to serve Rama's larger purpose. This demonstrates perfect devotion - knowing when to act and when to step back, having no ego about being the hero.
  • Burning of Lanka: Before leaving, Hanuman allowed himself to be captured to meet Ravana. When the demons tied his tail with oil-soaked cloth and set it on fire, he broke free, made his tail grow enormously long, and used it to set fire to the golden city of Lanka, burning it down except for Sita's location. This act demonstrated his power and delivered a warning to Ravana of the destruction to come. The burning tail became another iconic image of Hanuman's might.
  • Bringing the Sanjeevani Mountain: During the great battle, when Lakshmana lay near death from an arrow, the only cure was the Sanjeevani herb from the Himalayas. Hanuman flew to the mountain, but unable to identify which herb it was in the darkness, he uprooted the entire mountain and carried it to Lanka. This extraordinary feat - carrying a mountain through the air across India - shows that for true devotion, nothing is impossible. His practical solution also demonstrates wisdom: when in doubt, bring everything needed.
  • Rama in the Heart: After Sita's rescue, when Sita gave Hanuman a pearl necklace as thanks, he broke it apart, examining each pearl. When asked what he was doing, he said he was looking for Rama - anything without Rama's name had no value to him. Some doubted his devotion, so Hanuman tore open his chest to reveal Rama and Sita literally inscribed on his heart. This is the ultimate image of bhakti - the beloved lord residing permanently in the devotee's heart.
  • The Immortal Chiranjivi: When the time came for Rama to leave his earthly form, Hanuman was overcome with grief. Rama blessed him to remain on earth for the entire Kali Yuga (current age), living wherever the Ramayana is sung and Rama's name is chanted. Thus Hanuman is considered a Chiranjivi (immortal being), and devotees believe he is invisibly present whenever his name or Rama's name is spoken with devotion.
  • Meeting Krishna: In the Mahabharata, when Bhima (another son of Vayu) tried to move what appeared to be an old monkey's tail blocking his path, he couldn't budge it. The old monkey revealed himself as Hanuman and blessed Bhima. Later, during the great war, Hanuman resided on Arjuna's chariot flag at Krishna's request, his roar terrifying enemies and protecting the Pandavas. This shows Hanuman serving both major avatars of Vishnu - Rama and Krishna.
Sources: Valmiki Ramayana, Tulsidas' Ramcharitmanas, Mahabharata, Hanuman Chalisa, Various Puranas

Character & Symbolism

Hanuman embodies ideal qualities that make him universally beloved:

Perfect Devotion (Bhakti)

His love for Rama is completely selfless. He wants nothing for himself, only to serve. This is bhakti yoga in its purest form - the path of devotion where the devotee's ego dissolves in love for the divine.

Strength in Humility

Despite possessing the strength to move mountains and leap oceans, Hanuman remains humble, never boastful. He uses his powers only when needed for service, never for personal glory. True strength, his example teaches, is combined with humility.

Celibacy and Focus

As a lifelong celibate (Brahmachari), Hanuman channeled all his energy into devotion and service. His celibacy wasn't mere abstinence but complete dedication - his love for Rama left no room for any other attachment.

Intelligence and Wisdom

Hanuman is not just strong but wise. He knows Sanskrit, composes poetry, and solves problems through cleverness as much as might. His wisdom lies in knowing that all his powers come from grace, not from himself.

Protector and Guardian

He is invoked for protection from evil spirits, negative energies, and dangers. His very name is believed to dispel fear and difficulties. Homes display his image to ward off malevolent forces.

Relationships

Family & Divine Connections

  • Mother: Anjana - An apsara who was cursed to be born as a vanara
  • Earthly Father: Kesari - King of the vanaras
  • Divine Father: Vayu - God of wind, granting him speed and flight
  • Lord and Master: Rama - His entire existence is devoted to Rama's service
  • Relationship with Sita: He is her devoted servant and protector, treating her as the divine mother
  • Brother: Bhima (both are sons of Vayu, though from different mothers)
  • Connection to Shiva: Considered an avatar or aspect of Shiva in some traditions

Companions & Allies

  • The Vanara Army: Fought alongside the monkey warriors including Sugriva, Angada, Nala, and Nila
  • Jambavan: The wise bear who reminded Hanuman of his forgotten powers
  • Lakshmana: Rama's brother, whom Hanuman saved by bringing the Sanjeevani herb
  • The Pandavas: Protected them during the Mahabharata war at Krishna's request

Worship & Rituals

Worship Practices

Hanuman temples are found throughout India, often located on hilltops or at village entrances as guardians. Unlike many deities worshipped on specific days, Hanuman is traditionally worshipped on Tuesdays and Saturdays. Wrestlers, athletes, and martial artists especially revere him for strength and discipline. Students worship him for wisdom and concentration.

Sacred Sites

Major Hanuman temples include: Sankat Mochan Temple (Varanasi), Hanuman Temple (Connaught Place, Delhi - where worship continues 24 hours), Hanuman Garhi (Ayodhya), Balaji Temple (Mehandipur - famous for spirit exorcism), and countless local temples. Naturally occurring Hanuman images on mountains and rocks are considered especially powerful.

Festivals

  • Hanuman Jayanti: Birthday celebration on the full moon of Chaitra month (March-April). Devotees fast, recite Hanuman Chalisa, and visit temples.
  • Ram Navami: Also celebrated as Hanuman's birth according to some traditions.
  • Every Tuesday: Special Hanuman worship day throughout the year.

Offerings

Red flowers (especially hibiscus), red cloth, Sindoor (vermillion - devotees smear it on his image and themselves for his blessings), Ladoos (sweet balls), Bananas, Besan ladoo (chickpea flour sweets), coconut, and sesame oil. Physical exercise, wrestling, and martial arts practice are also considered forms of worship, as is reading the Ramayana.

Prayers & Texts

The Hanuman Chalisa (40 verses composed by Tulsidas in the 16th century) is the most popular prayer, recited daily by millions. The Bajrang Baan is a powerful hymn for protection. The Sundara Kanda (beautiful chapter) from the Ramayana describing Hanuman's exploits in Lanka is considered especially auspicious. The mantra "Om Han Hanumate Namah" and "Jai Bajrang Bali" are commonly chanted.
