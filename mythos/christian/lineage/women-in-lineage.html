Women in the Messianic Lineage

The remarkable and unusual inclusion of five women in Matthew's genealogy - Tamar, Rahab, Ruth, Bathsheba ("wife of Uriah"), and Mary - demonstrates God's grace working through outsiders, foreigners, and those considered scandalous, foreshadowing the universal scope of salvation.

üîç Why Include Women? Breaking Ancient Convention

In ancient Jewish genealogies, women were rarely mentioned. The lineage typically traced through fathers and sons only. Matthew's deliberate inclusion of five specific women is therefore highly significant and intentional.

Theological Significance of Their Inclusion

  • Grace to Outsiders: Four of the five had questionable backgrounds or were non-Israelites
  • God's Unconventional Choices: God works through unexpected people, not just the "righteous"
  • Gentile Inclusion: Three were Gentiles (Tamar likely, Rahab, Ruth), foreshadowing salvation for all nations
  • Sexual Scandal: Each had some association with sexual irregularity or scandal, yet God used them
  • Faith and Courage: Despite their circumstances, each showed remarkable faith
  • Messianic Foreshadowing: Mary, the virgin mother, is the culmination of this pattern

1. Tamar üìú

"Righteousness Through Desperation" - Genesis 38

Tamar was a Canaanite woman who married Er, son of Judah. When Er died, she married his brother Onan (levirate marriage), but Onan also died. Judah refused to give her his third son Shelah, leaving Tamar in perpetual widowhood without an heir.

The Story

Desperate for an heir and to fulfill her rights, Tamar disguised herself as a prostitute and slept with her father-in-law Judah. When Judah discovered she was pregnant and prepared to have her burned for adultery, she revealed his own signet and staff as proof that he was the father. Judah declared, "She is more righteous than I" (Genesis 38:26), acknowledging his failure to give her Shelah.

"And Judah acknowledged them, and said, She hath been more righteous than I; because that I gave her not to Shelah my son. And he knew her again no more." ‚Äî Genesis 38:26 (KJV)

Significance: Tamar's bold action, though scandalous, ensured the continuation of Judah's line, from which the Messiah would come. She demonstrated that God's purposes can be fulfilled even through morally ambiguous situations.

2. Rahab üèõÔ∏è

"The Canaanite Prostitute Who Became a Hero of Faith" - Joshua 2

Rahab was a prostitute living in Jericho when Israelite spies entered the city. Despite being a Canaanite (Israel's enemy), she recognized the God of Israel as the true God and hid the spies from the king's men, risking her own life.

The Story

In exchange for protecting the spies, Rahab asked for mercy for herself and her family when Israel conquered Jericho. She was saved, along with her household, when Jericho fell. Rahab married Salmon and became the mother of Boaz, making her the great-great-grandmother of King David.

"By faith the harlot Rahab perished not with them that believed not, when she had received the spies with peace." ‚Äî Hebrews 11:31 (KJV)
"Likewise also was not Rahab the harlot justified by works, when she had received the messengers, and had sent them out another way?" ‚Äî James 2:25 (KJV)

Significance: Rahab, a Gentile prostitute, became a hero of faith (Hebrews 11:31) and an ancestor of Christ. Her inclusion demonstrates that no background is too shameful for God's redemptive purposes. Faith, not ethnicity or past sins, qualifies one for God's kingdom.

3. Ruth üåæ

"The Moabite Who Chose Israel's God" - Book of Ruth

Ruth was a Moabite woman (Moabites were excluded from the assembly of the Lord - Deuteronomy 23:3) who married an Israelite man living in Moab. When her husband died, she chose to return to Israel with her mother-in-law Naomi rather than stay in her homeland.

The Story

Ruth's famous declaration of loyalty - "Your people shall be my people, and your God my God" (Ruth 1:16) - demonstrated her genuine conversion and faith. Through God's providence, she married Boaz (son of Rahab), becoming the great-grandmother of King David and an ancestor of Jesus.

"And Ruth said, Intreat me not to leave thee, or to return from following after thee: for whither thou goest, I will go; and where thou lodgest, I will lodge: thy people shall be my people, and thy God my God: Where thou diest, will I die, and there will I be buried: the LORD do so to me, and more also, if ought but death part thee and me." ‚Äî Ruth 1:16-17 (KJV)

Significance: Ruth, a foreigner from a despised nation, chose the God of Israel and became the great-grandmother of David. Her story is a beautiful picture of redemption, kinsman-redeemer (Boaz foreshadowing Christ), and the inclusion of Gentiles in God's plan.

4. Bathsheba ("Wife of Uriah") üëë

"From Adultery to Royalty" - 2 Samuel 11-12

Bathsheba is notably referred to not by her own name but as "her that had been the wife of Uriah" - a reminder of David's great sin: adultery with Bathsheba and the murder of her husband Uriah the Hittite.

The Story

King David, from his rooftop, saw Bathsheba bathing and committed adultery with her. When she became pregnant, David arranged for her husband Uriah (a loyal soldier) to be killed in battle. The prophet Nathan confronted David with his sin. The child died, but David and Bathsheba later had Solomon, who would inherit David's throne.

"And David sent and enquired after the woman. And one said, Is not this Bathsheba, the daughter of Eliam, the wife of Uriah the Hittite? And David sent messengers, and took her; and she came in unto him, and he lay with her... And the woman conceived, and sent and told David, and said, I am with child." ‚Äî 2 Samuel 11:3-5 (KJV)

Significance: Matthew's reference to "Uriah's wife" emphasizes the scandal rather than hiding it. Despite this dark beginning, God brought Solomon - the wisest king and builder of the Temple - from this union. This demonstrates God's ability to bring redemption even from the worst human sin.

5. Mary (The Virgin) ‚≠ê

"Theotokos - The God-Bearer" - Matthew 1:18-25, Luke 1:26-38

Mary, the Virgin, is the culmination of the pattern of surprising women in Jesus's genealogy. She was a young virgin from Nazareth, betrothed to Joseph, when the angel Gabriel announced that she would conceive by the Holy Spirit and bear the Son of God.

The Story

Mary's virgin conception would have brought scandal and potential death by stoning for suspected adultery. Yet Mary responded with faith: "Behold the handmaid of the Lord; be it unto me according to thy word" (Luke 1:38). Joseph, warned in a dream, took her as his wife, protecting her from shame.

"And the angel said unto her, Fear not, Mary: for thou hast found favour with God. And, behold, thou shalt conceive in thy womb, and bring forth a son, and shalt call his name JESUS. He shall be great, and shall be called the Son of the Highest: and the Lord God shall give unto him the throne of his father David." ‚Äî Luke 1:30-32 (KJV)
"Now the birth of Jesus Christ was on this wise: When as his mother Mary was espoused to Joseph, before they came together, she was found with child of the Holy Ghost." ‚Äî Matthew 1:18 (KJV)

Significance: Mary's virgin birth is the ultimate fulfillment of the pattern. Like the other women, her situation appeared scandalous - pregnant before marriage. But unlike them, her conception was entirely miraculous, by the Holy Spirit. Mary is honored as Theotokos (God-Bearer), the most blessed woman who brought God Himself into human flesh. ‚ú® The Pattern: Grace for All

What These Women Teach Us

  • God chooses the unlikely: Prostitutes, foreigners, adulterers - not just the "righteous"
  • Gentiles are included: Rahab (Canaanite), Ruth (Moabite), possibly Tamar and Bathsheba
  • Sexual scandal doesn't disqualify: Each woman's story involved sexual irregularity, yet God used them
  • Faith matters more than background: Rahab and Ruth showed extraordinary faith despite being outsiders
  • God redeems our messes: David and Bathsheba's adultery led to Solomon and the messianic line
  • Women are valued: In a patriarchal culture, Matthew highlights women as crucial to God's plan
  • Grace is universal: These women foreshadow the universal salvation offered through Christ
"But God hath chosen the foolish things of the world to confound the wise; and God hath chosen the weak things of the world to confound the things which are mighty; And base things of the world, and things which are despised, hath God chosen, yea, and things which are not, to bring to nought things that are." ‚Äî 1 Corinthians 1:27-28 (KJV)

Through these five remarkable women, Matthew demonstrates that the Gospel is for everyone - Jews and Gentiles, righteous and sinners, men and women. No one is beyond the reach of God's redeeming grace.
