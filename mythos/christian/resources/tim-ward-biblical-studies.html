Honoring Tim Ward's Biblical Scholarship

Deep Scriptural Studies on God as Spirit, Christ Revealing the Invisible Father, and the Indwelling Holy Spirit

This website's exploration of Gnostic Christianity and the indwelling Spirit has been deeply informed by Tim Ward's biblical studies.

His careful examination of Scripture's teaching on God as invisible Spirit, Christ's mission to reveal the Father within, and the transformation from external religion to internal spiritual reality has profoundly shaped our understanding of the divine nature and humanity's relationship with the sacred.

We are grateful for his scholarly work challenging unbiblical devil mythology and revealing the true nature of "the Satan" as adversary role rather than cosmic supernatural being.

Core Biblical Themes from Tim Ward's Studies

God is Spirit - Not Physical Form

John 4:24
"God is a Spirit: and they that worship him must worship him in spirit and in truth."

Tim Ward's foundational insight: God has no physical body. The commandment against graven images (Exodus 20:4) forbids us from creating physical representations of God because God transcends all physical form. Our anthropomorphic images of God - whether as an old man on a cloud or a physical Jesus mediating before a distant throne - violate this core biblical truth.

Christ Reveals the Invisible God

Colossians 1:15
"Who is the image of the invisible God, the firstborn of every creature."
John 14:9
"Jesus saith unto him, Have I been so long time with you, and yet hast thou not known me, Philip? he that hath seen me hath seen the Father; and how sayest thou then, Shew us the Father?"

Christ reveals the Father spiritually, not physically. When Jesus says "he who has seen me has seen the Father," He speaks of spiritual vision, not physical sight. The disciples looked at Jesus physically yet still failed to "see" the Father until the Spirit came.

No Man Has Seen God - Spiritual Vision Only

John 1:18
"No man hath seen God at any time"; the only begotten Son, which is in the bosom of the Father, he hath declared him.
1 John 4:12
"No man hath seen God at any time. If we love one another, God dwelleth in us, and his love is perfected in us."

Ward emphasizes: No physical eyes have ever beheld God. Even Jacob's claim to have "seen God face to face" (Genesis 32:30) must be understood spiritually - he wrestled with an angel, not God's literal physical form. God is invisible to flesh and blood.

The Pure in Heart See God Internally

Matthew 5:8
"Blessed are the pure in heart: for they shall see God."
Jeremiah 29:13
"And ye shall seek me, and find me, when ye shall search for me with all your heart."

The heart - not physical eyes - is the organ of spiritual sight. God is found through internal spiritual searching, not external physical observation. This is why God's form is "reserved for only the pure in heart" - it requires spiritual transformation, not better physical vision.

The Father Dwells in Us - Indwelling Presence

John 14:10
"Believest thou not that I am in the Father, and the Father in me? the words that I speak unto you I speak not of myself: but the Father that dwelleth in me, he doeth the works."
1 John 4:12-13
"No man hath seen God at any time. If we love one another, God dwelleth in us, and his love is perfected in us. Hereby know we that we dwell in him, and he in us, because he hath given us of his Spirit."

Ward's revolutionary insight: The Father doesn't dwell in a distant heaven requiring a physical Jesus to mediate - the Father dwells in us through the Spirit. This is the mystery hidden for ages: Christ in you, the hope of glory.

Your Body is the Temple of the Holy Spirit

1 Corinthians 6:19
"What? know ye not that your body is the temple of the Holy Ghost which is in you, which ye have of God, and ye are not your own?"
1 Corinthians 3:16-17
"Know ye not that ye are the temple of God, and that the Spirit of God dwelleth in you?"

The temple is no longer an external building in Jerusalem - it is our very bodies. God's dwelling place has shifted from external stone to internal spiritual reality. This transforms all of religion from external ritual to internal transformation.

Christ in You, the Hope of Glory

Colossians 1:27
"To whom God would make known what is the riches of the glory of this mystery among the Gentiles; which is Christ in you, the hope of glory."
John 14:20
"At that day ye shall know that I am in my Father, and ye in me, and I in you."

The mystery revealed: Christ is not merely a historical figure who lived 2000 years ago, but a living spiritual presence dwelling within believers now. This indwelling Christ is our transformation, our salvation, our eternal life in the present moment.

Challenging Devil Mythology: The Satan as Adversary Role

"The Satan" in Job - Not a Personal Devil

Job 1:12
"And the Lord said unto Satan, Behold, all that he hath is in thy power; only upon himself put not forth thine hand. So Satan went forth from the presence of the Lord."

Ward's careful analysis reveals that "the Satan" (ha-satan in Hebrew) means "the adversary" or "the accuser" - a role or function, not a personal name. In Job, this figure appears in God's heavenly court as one who tests and accuses, subject entirely to God's authority. The Satan cannot act independently but only as God permits.

Job 2:10
"But he said unto her, Thou speakest as one of the foolish women speaketh. What? shall we receive good at the hand of God, and shall we not receive evil? In all this did not Job sin with his lips."

Remarkably, Job never once blames "Satan" for his suffering. He attributes everything - both good and evil - directly to God's sovereign hand. This shows that the biblical understanding of "the Satan" differs drastically from later Christian devil mythology.

66 Questions Revealing Devil Misconceptions

Tim Ward compiled 66 penetrating questions that expose the unbiblical nature of traditional devil theology:

  • Question 1: If God alone is omnipresent, how can Satan tempt all people everywhere at once?
  • Question 3: Why does Jesus say sin comes from within the human heart (Matthew 15:19), not from external demonic influence?
  • Question 4: Why did God repent of making man because man's heart was evil (Genesis 6:5-6), not repent of making Satan?
  • Question 5: Can you find even one case of demonic possession in the entire Old Testament?
  • Question 7: When Jesus called Peter "Satan" (Matthew 16:23), did He say Peter spoke "the things of men" or "the things of the devil"?
  • Question 9: How can the human heart be "deceitful above all things" (Jeremiah 17:9) if Satan is supposed to be the ultimate deceiver?
  • Question 10: If Christ destroyed "him that had the power of death, that is, the devil" (Hebrews 2:14), why is Satan supposedly still active?
  • Question 12: Is Satan the serpent in Genesis, or was it a beast of the field cursed to crawl on its belly?
  • Question 23: How did King David never write one word about Satan in all the Psalms?
  • Question 34: Why does the Bible say "the carnal mind is enmity against God" (Romans 8:7) instead of Satan?

These questions systematically dismantle the mythology of an omnipresent, supernatural devil and return us to the biblical teaching: sin originates in the human heart, and "the Satan" is a role of adversary/accuser, not a cosmic rebel angel.

Satan: Hebrew for "Adversary" or "Opponent"

Matthew 16:23
"But he turned, and said unto Peter, Get thee behind me, Satan: thou art an offence unto me: for thou savourest not the things that be of God, but those that be of men."

When Jesus calls Peter "Satan," He clearly means Peter is acting as an adversary or opponent. Jesus doesn't say Peter is possessed by a supernatural devil - He says Peter is thinking with human wisdom ("the things of men") rather than divine wisdom. The word "Satan" is functional, not a proper name.

Sin Comes from the Human Heart

Matthew 15:18-20
"But those things which proceed out of the mouth come forth from the heart; and they defile the man. For out of the heart proceed evil thoughts, murders, adulteries, fornications, thefts, false witness, blasphemies: These are the things which defile a man."
James 1:14-15
"But every man is tempted, when he is drawn away of his own lust, and enticed. Then when lust hath conceived, it bringeth forth sin: and sin, when it is finished, bringeth forth death."
Jeremiah 17:9
"The heart is deceitful above all things, and desperately wicked: who can know it?"

Ward's studies emphasize: The Bible consistently locates the source of sin in the human heart, not in external demonic forces. We are tempted by our own internal desires, not by a supernatural tempter. This places moral responsibility squarely on human choice.

Connection to Gnostic Christianity

How Tim Ward's Biblical Insights Align with Gnostic Themes

God as Pure Spirit (The Monad)

Ward's Insight: God is Spirit (John 4:24), invisible, without physical form.

Gnostic Teaching: The Monad is the ineffable, invisible, unknowable source beyond all form and materiality.

Alignment: Both emphasize God's transcendence beyond physical manifestation, rejecting anthropomorphic imagery.

Christ Reveals the Invisible Father

Ward's Insight: Christ is "the image of the invisible God" (Col 1:15), revealing the Father spiritually.

Gnostic Teaching: Christ the Logos descends to reveal the unknown Father, making the invisible visible through gnosis.

Alignment: Both see Christ as the revealer of divine mystery, bringing knowledge of the Father's nature.

Divine Spark Within (Indwelling Spirit)

Ward's Insight: "Christ in you, the hope of glory" (Col 1:27) - God dwells within us.

Gnostic Teaching: Humans contain a divine spark, a fragment of the Pleroma trapped in matter, awaiting awakening.

Alignment: Both emphasize internal divine presence rather than external mediation.

Spiritual Vision vs. External Religion

Ward's Insight: "The pure in heart shall see God" (Matt 5:8) - spiritual perception, not physical sight.

Gnostic Teaching: Gnosis is spiritual knowledge/vision that transcends external religious forms and physical senses.

Alignment: Both prioritize internal spiritual transformation over external religious observance.

Internal Kingdom vs. External Institutions

Ward's Insight: Your body is the temple (1 Cor 6:19) - not an external building in Jerusalem.

Gnostic Teaching: "The Kingdom of God is within you" (Luke 17:21) - salvation through internal gnosis, not institutional religion.

Alignment: Both shift focus from external religious institutions to internal spiritual reality.

Flesh and Blood Cannot Inherit the Kingdom

Ward's Insight: "Flesh and blood cannot inherit the kingdom of God" (1 Cor 15:50) - spiritual resurrection.

Gnostic Teaching: Matter is inferior to spirit; salvation involves liberation from material existence into spiritual reality.

Alignment: Both emphasize spiritual over material, though Gnostics sometimes more radically devalue matter.

Mystery Hidden and Now Revealed

Ward's Insight: The mystery of "Christ in you" was hidden but now revealed (Col 1:26-27).

Gnostic Teaching: Secret knowledge (gnosis) reveals hidden spiritual truths concealed from the masses.

Alignment: Both speak of revelation of previously hidden spiritual mysteries.

Sin from Ignorance, Not External Devil

Ward's Insight: Sin comes from the human heart (Matt 15:19), not from a supernatural devil.

Gnostic Teaching: Evil results from ignorance (agnoia) of divine truth, not from cosmic spiritual warfare.

Alignment: Both locate the problem in human condition/consciousness rather than external supernatural evil forces.
