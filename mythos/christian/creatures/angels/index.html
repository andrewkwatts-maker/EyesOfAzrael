The seven archangels stand before the throne of God (Revelation 8:2), serving as chief messengers and warriors in the divine plan. While only Michael, Gabriel, and Raphael are named in canonical scripture, the Book of Enoch and other apocryphal sources identify the complete seven.

Domains: Protection, warfare, judgment

üî• The Nine Choirs - Triadic Hierarchy

According to Pseudo-Dionysius the Areopagite's Celestial Hierarchy (5th-6th century), angels are organized into nine orders arranged in three hierarchies or triads. Each triad represents different levels of proximity to God and different functions in the divine plan.

üåü First Triad - Highest (Closest to God)

The first hierarchy dwells in immediate proximity to God, contemplating divine essence and radiating holiness.

üìñ Biblical & Apocryphal Sources

Canonical Scripture

  • Isaiah 6:1-7 - Seraphim surrounding God's throne
  • Ezekiel 1, 10 - Cherubim and the living creatures (Ophanim)
  • Daniel 7-12 - Michael as prince and protector, angelic warfare
  • Luke 1 - Gabriel's annunciation to Mary
  • Matthew 18:10 - Guardian angels doctrine
  • Colossians 1:16 - "Thrones, dominions, principalities, powers"
  • Ephesians 6:12 - Spiritual warfare against principalities and powers
  • Revelation 12 - Michael vs. the Dragon, war in heaven
  • Revelation 4-5 - Heavenly worship, thousands of angels
  • Hebrews 1:14 - Angels as "ministering spirits"

Apocryphal & Deuterocanonical

  • Book of Tobit - Raphael as healer and guide
  • 1 Enoch - Seven archangels, Watchers, extensive angelology
  • 2 Esdras 4 - Uriel as revealer of mysteries
  • Book of Jubilees - Angels of presence, sanctification

Early Church Tradition

  • Pseudo-Dionysius the Areopagite - Celestial Hierarchy (5th-6th century), systematized the nine choirs
  • Thomas Aquinas - Summa Theologica, detailed angelology
  • Gregory the Great - Homilies on angelic orders
