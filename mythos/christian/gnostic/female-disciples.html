The Women the Church Tried to Erase

üë∏üìñ‚ú®

Before patriarchal Christianity silenced them, women were prophets, teachers, apostles, and priests. They led communities, wrote gospels, celebrated sacraments, and transmitted secret teachings. Gnostic Christianity preserved what orthodoxy tried to destroy: the memory of women's spiritual authority and leadership. Meet Salome, Martha, Mary of Bethany, the women disciples of the canonical gospels, and the female prophets who terrified the Church Fathers into writing laws against women's ministry.

üìñ Women Disciples in the Canonical Gospels

Even in the heavily edited canonical texts, we see evidence of women's prominence in Jesus's movement. They traveled with him, funded his ministry, stayed at the crucifixion when men fled, and were first witnesses of the resurrection.

The Women Who Followed Jesus (Luke 8:1-3)

"After this, Jesus traveled about from one town and village to another, proclaiming the good news of the kingdom of God. The Twelve were with him, and also some women who had been cured of evil spirits and diseases: Mary (called Magdalene) from whom seven demons had gone out; Joanna the wife of Chuza, the manager of Herod's household; Susanna; and many others. These women were helping to support them out of their own means." ‚Äî Luke 8:1-3

Notice what this passage reveals:

  • They traveled with Jesus: Not just occasional followers, but part of the traveling community
  • They financially supported the ministry: Women had resources and agency
  • They are named: Mary Magdalene (first), Joanna, Susanna - these were leaders
  • "Many others": This was a large group of women disciples
  • Healed by Jesus: Like the male disciples, they had encountered divine power

Joanna - The Aristocratic Disciple

Joanna (wife of Chuza, Herod's household manager) represents the upper-class women in Jesus's movement:

  • Elite connections: Her husband managed Herod Antipas's household
  • Financial resources: She could afford to support Jesus's ministry
  • Political danger: Following Jesus put her family at risk
  • Resurrection witness: Luke 24:10 names her among those who found the empty tomb
  • Later tradition: Some identify her with Junia, the female apostle in Romans 16:7

Susanna - The Forgotten Disciple

Susanna is mentioned only once (Luke 8:3), but her inclusion in this short list signals importance:

  • Named third: After Mary Magdalene and Joanna, suggesting prominence
  • Supported ministry financially: Had independent means
  • Tradition: Some identify her with the woman at the well (John 4)
  • Erasure: Later Church tradition forgot her completely - no feast day, no legends

Mary and Martha of Bethany

Sisters Mary and Martha (and their brother Lazarus) were Jesus's close friends who hosted him in Bethany.

Martha - The Apostolic Confessor

"Yes, Lord," she replied, "I believe that you are the Messiah, the Son of God, who is to come into the world." ‚Äî John 11:27 (Martha's confession)

Martha makes the same christological confession as Peter (Matthew 16:16) - yet she never gets credit as "rock of the church." This is identical apostolic faith, spoken by a woman.

Mary - The Devoted Student

"Mary sat at the Lord's feet listening to what he said." ‚Äî Luke 10:39

"Sitting at the feet" was the position of a rabbinical student. Mary takes the role of disciple, learning from the master - a role normally reserved for men. Jesus defends her right to learn theology rather than serve food.

Some Gnostic traditions identify Mary of Bethany with Mary Magdalene, creating a composite figure: wealthy supporter, devoted student, resurrection witness, and teacher of gnosis. Whether one person or two, these Marys represent women's full discipleship.

The Women at the Cross and Empty Tomb

All four gospels agree: when male disciples fled, women stayed. When Sabbath ended, women came first to the tomb.

‚ò• Women in Gnostic Gospels

The Gnostic texts go further than the canonical gospels, showing women as teachers, visionaries, and recipients of special revelation.

Salome - The Questioning Disciple

Salome appears in Mark's gospel (15:40, 16:1) and prominently in the Gospel of Thomas and the Gospel of the Egyptians.

"Salome said, 'Who are you, man, that you have come up on my couch and eaten from my table?' Jesus said to her, 'I am he who exists from the undivided. I was given some of the things of my Father.' Salome said, 'I am your disciple.' Jesus said to her, 'Therefore I say, if he is undivided, he will be filled with light, but if he is divided, he will be filled with darkness.'" ‚Äî Gospel of Thomas, Saying 61

Notice the dynamics:

  • She questions Jesus boldly: "Who are you?" - not submissive, but intellectually engaged
  • Intimate setting: "My couch... my table" - she hosts Jesus, like Martha
  • She declares discipleship: "I am your disciple" - direct claim to student status
  • Jesus teaches her: Deep doctrine about divine unity and division
"When Salome asked when the things she had inquired about would be known, the Lord said: 'When you have trampled on the garment of shame, and when the two become one and the male with the female is neither male nor female.'" ‚Äî Gospel of the Egyptians (quoted by Clement of Alexandria)

Salome asks about eschatology (end times), and Jesus teaches her about gender transcendence and the restoration of primordial unity. This is advanced theological discourse with a female disciple.

Mary the Mother - Theological Voice

In Gnostic texts, Mary the mother of Jesus is more than passive vessel - she is a theological voice and questioner.

"His mother Mary came forth and said to him: 'Holy son, why did you leave us and go away?' And Jesus answered and said: 'I have not left you at all. I have always been with you.'" ‚Äî Dialogue of the Savior

The Questions of Mary (now lost, condemned by Church Fathers) apparently contained radical teachings Jesus gave specifically to his mother - so controversial that Epiphanius called it blasphemous and obscene. What theology did Mary teach that threatened patriarchal order?

Marsanes and the Female Prophets

The Nag Hammadi text Marsanes refers to female prophets and visionaries:

  • Women receiving visions of the divine realms
  • Female mystics ascending through the heavens
  • Women experiencing gnosis directly, not mediated through men
  • Female spiritual authority based on visionary experience

The Dialogue of the Savior - Women as Equals

The Dialogue of the Savior features three disciples questioning Jesus: Matthew, Judas, and Mary (Magdalene). Mary speaks as frequently and authoritatively as the men.

"She uttered this as a woman who had understood completely." ‚Äî Dialogue of the Savior (about Mary Magdalene)

The text explicitly states Mary's complete understanding - she is not learning from men, she is their equal in comprehension.

Pistis Sophia - Women Dominating Dialogue

In the lengthy Pistis Sophia, Mary Magdalene asks 39 of the 46 questions posed to Jesus - 85% of the dialogue! Other women (Mary the mother, Martha, Salome) ask additional questions.

"Mary Magdalene and John the Virgin will surpass all my disciples... They will be on my right and on my left. And I am they, and they are I." ‚Äî Pistis Sophia

Mary Magdalene is paired with "John the Virgin" (possibly code for Mary, or actual John) as Jesus's spiritual equals. The text repeatedly praises her "pure light" and spiritual insight.

‚õ™ Women's Leadership in Gnostic Communities

Gnostic communities allowed women to prophesy, teach, baptize, celebrate Eucharist, and hold leadership positions - practices that enraged orthodox Church Fathers.

What the Church Fathers Said (Complaining About Women Leaders)

The best evidence for women's authority in Gnostic Christianity comes from their enemies - orthodox bishops complaining about it:

"They admit women to the priesthood on the grounds of equality... These heretical women - how audacious they are! They dare to teach, to dispute, to exorcise, to promise cures, perhaps even to baptize!" ‚Äî Tertullian, On the Prescription of Heretics (c. 200 CE)
"What impudence in these heretics! Their women teach, they dispute, they exorcise, they cure, and perhaps they even baptize." ‚Äî Tertullian, On Baptism
"The Marcionites allow women to teach in the churches, and some of the heretics let them baptize... But we do not permit a woman to teach in the church, nor to have authority over men." ‚Äî Tertullian (horrified by women's equality)
"Those wretched women! They are seduced by Marcus [a Gnostic teacher]. Many of them have been led away by these false sisters... Some of their women even presume to teach, to anoint, to baptize - they take part in everything!" ‚Äî Irenaeus, Against Heresies (c. 180 CE)

Read between the lines: These "complaints" are actually evidence that Gnostic women were teaching, baptizing, performing exorcisms, celebrating sacraments, and leading communities - full priestly authority.

Female Prophets and Teachers

Gnostic communities had prominent female prophets:

  • Philoumene: Gnostic prophetess mentioned by Tertullian, received visions and taught theology
  • Priscilla and Maximilla: Montanist prophetesses (proto-Gnostic movement), claimed direct revelation from Holy Spirit
  • The women of Thyatira: "Jezebel" in Revelation 2:20 - likely a female prophet/teacher in Thyatiran church
  • Marcellina: Carpocratian Gnostic teacher in Rome (2nd century), had images of Jesus, Paul, and Pythagoras
  • The "false sisters": Women Irenaeus and Tertullian condemned for teaching and leading

Women Celebrating Sacraments

Evidence suggests Gnostic women performed all sacramental functions:

  • Baptism: Tertullian explicitly complains women baptize in Gnostic sects
  • Eucharist: Women presided over sacred meals and communion
  • Anointing: Women performed ritual anointing with oil
  • Bridal Chamber: The most sacred Gnostic sacrament, administered by both women and men
  • Laying on of hands: Women ordained and blessed others
"Every woman who will make herself male will enter the kingdom of heaven." ‚Äî Gospel of Thomas 114 (not biological, but spiritual authority previously restricted to men)

Why Gnostic Communities Honored Women

Gnostic theology supported gender equality:

  1. Divine feminine: If God includes Mother, Sophia, and feminine Aeons, women reflect divinity
  2. Spiritual authority through gnosis: Knowledge comes through revelation, not male ordination
  3. Gospel precedent: Mary Magdalene's apostolic authority was model
  4. Pentecost promise: "Your daughters shall prophesy" (Acts 2:17)
  5. Galatians 3:28: "No longer male and female in Christ" - baptismal equality formula
  6. Creation theology: Genesis 1:27 - male AND female in God's image

‚öîÔ∏è The Suppression - How Patriarchy Won

Women's leadership didn't end because of theology - it ended because of politics and patriarchy.

The Legal Attack on Women's Ministry

1 Timothy 2:11-12 (Deutero-Pauline, c. 100-140 CE)

"A woman should learn in quietness and full submission. I do not permit a woman to teach or to assume authority over a man; she must be quiet." ‚Äî 1 Timothy 2:11-12 (NOT written by Paul)

Modern scholars agree this letter was written in Paul's name decades after his death, likely to combat women's leadership in Gnostic-influenced communities. Real Paul said "no longer male and female" (Gal 3:28) and praised women co-workers like Junia the apostle, Phoebe the deacon, and Priscilla the teacher.

The Didascalia Apostolorum (3rd century CE)

This church manual explicitly forbids women from teaching or baptizing, directly responding to Gnostic practice:

"We do not advise that women should teach, for this is dangerous... Neither do we advise that women should baptize, for this is improper and perilous."

Why "dangerous" and "perilous"? Because women teaching threatened male power, not divine order.

Council of Laodicea (363 CE)

Canon 11 officially banned women from being ordained as presbyters (priests/elders):

"Women may not be appointed to the presbyterate."

This canon proves women HAD BEEN serving as presbyters - otherwise why ban it?

Archaeological Evidence of Women's Leadership

Despite textual suppression, archaeology preserves evidence:

  • Catacomb of Priscilla (Rome): Fresco of woman celebrating Eucharist (arms raised in orans position over altar)
  • Epitaph of Flavia Vitalia: Called "presbytera" (female priest/elder)
  • Theodora Episcopa: Mosaic in Santa Prassede basilica, Rome - "Theodora the Bishop" (her title later defaced)
  • Church of San Damaso (Cumae): Tomb inscription "Leta Presbytera" - Leta the woman priest
  • Letters of Bitalia and Kale: Two women deacons, 6th century Egypt

Why Orthodoxy Suppressed Women

The reasons were political, not theological:

  1. Roman patriarchal culture: As Christianity became Roman state religion (4th century), it adopted Roman gender hierarchy
  2. Control and standardization: Hierarchical male priesthood was easier to control than charismatic women prophets
  3. Property and inheritance: Male-only clergy kept church property in patrilineal control
  4. Suppressing diversity: Women's leadership was associated with "heretical" Gnostic and Montanist movements
  5. Misogyny: Church Fathers (Jerome, Augustine, Tertullian) had profoundly negative views of women's nature

üåπ Reclaiming Women's Authority Today

The suppression of women's leadership was not divine will - it was patriarchal politics. The original Christian vision included women as apostles, prophets, teachers, and priests.

For Women in Ministry

  • You have biblical precedent: Phoebe, Junia, Priscilla, the four prophesying daughters, Mary Magdalene
  • You have Gnostic tradition: Women teaching, baptizing, celebrating Eucharist, leading communities
  • The "prohibitions" are late: 1 Timothy wasn't written by Paul; councils banned women because they HAD been leading
  • Jesus honored women: First resurrection witness, beloved disciple, equal student
  • The Holy Spirit is Mother: You reflect divine feminine in your ministry
  • Galatians 3:28 still stands: "No longer male and female in Christ Jesus"

For Churches and Denominations

  • Ordain women: Not as modern innovation, but restoration of original practice
  • Teach the full history: Don't hide women disciples and leaders
  • Read Gnostic texts: They preserved what orthodoxy tried to destroy
  • Repent of suppression: Acknowledge centuries of erasing women's authority
  • Honor Mary Magdalene: As apostle to apostles, not repentant prostitute
  • Include feminine divine language: God as Mother, Holy Spirit as She

Study and Advocacy Resources

  • Women's ordination movements: Advocating for full equality in Catholic, Orthodox, Baptist traditions
  • Feminist biblical scholarship: Recovering women's stories from patriarchal texts
  • Gnostic Christian communities: Some modern Gnostic churches ordain women as bishops
  • Historical research: Archaeological and textual evidence of early women leaders
  • Theological education: Women studying and teaching scripture, history, theology
