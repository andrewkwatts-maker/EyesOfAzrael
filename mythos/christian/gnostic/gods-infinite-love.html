God IS Love

The Eternal Nature of the Monad

"Beloved, let us love one another, for love is from God, and whoever loves has been born of God and knows God. Anyone who does not love does not know God, because God is love."
— 1 John 4:7-8

The Foundation: God's Very Nature

The most staggering truth in all of Scripture is not that God loves, but that God IS love. This is not a description of what God does, but a revelation of what God eternally is. Love is not an attribute God possesses among others—love is the very essence, the fundamental nature, the ontological reality of the divine.

"God is love, and whoever abides in love abides in God, and God abides in him."

— 1 John 4:16

What This Means

If God IS love, then everything that flows from God flows from love. Creation itself is an act of love's overflow. The cosmos exists because Love desired to share itself, to commune, to create beings capable of receiving and returning love. Even the fall—the terrible descent into matter and ignorance—cannot stop love from pursuing its beloved.

The Gnostic vision of the Monad—the ultimate, unknowable divine source—finds its deepest truth here: the Monad is pure, infinite, eternal LOVE. The Pleroma (divine fullness) is saturated with love. Every emanation, every aeon, every spark of divine light that descended into matter carries this love as its core nature.

Agape: Unconditional, Self-Giving Love

The Greek word used in Scripture for God's love is agape (ἀγάπη)—a love that is utterly unconditional, completely self-giving, and in no way dependent on the worthiness or response of its object.

Not Based on Merit

"But God shows his love for us in that while we were still sinners, Christ died for us."

— Romans 5:8

God's love is not a reward for good behavior. It is not earned through spiritual achievements or gnosis. Christ died for sinners—for those actively opposed to God, ignorant of their true nature, lost in the archon-ruled cosmos.

Cannot Be Lost

"For I am sure that neither death nor life, nor angels nor rulers, nor things present nor things to come, nor powers, nor height nor depth, nor anything else in all creation, will be able to separate us from the love of God in Christ Jesus our Lord."

— Romans 8:38-39

Nothing—absolutely nothing—can separate us from God's love. Not even death. Not cosmic powers. Not archons. Not our own ignorance or rebellion. The love of God is an unbreakable bond.

Never Fails

"Love never ends. As for prophecies, they will pass away; as for tongues, they will cease; as for knowledge, it will pass away."

— 1 Corinthians 13:8

Love is eternal. All other spiritual gifts and phenomena will pass away, but love endures forever. If love never fails—and God IS love—then God's purpose to save all humanity cannot ultimately fail.

Pursues Relentlessly

"What man of you, having a hundred sheep, if he has lost one of them, does not leave the ninety-nine in the open country, and go after the one that is lost, until he finds it?"

— Luke 15:4

The shepherd doesn't simply wait for the lost sheep to return. He actively, relentlessly pursues. He searches until he finds. God's love is not passive—it is the ultimate divine pursuit across all ages.

Jesus' Explicit Teachings on Love - The Red Letter Words

These are not Paul's interpretations. Not church tradition. Not theological speculation. These are the actual words of Jesus Christ, recorded in the Gospels as His direct teaching. If we claim to follow Jesus, these words must be foundational to our understanding of God's nature.

1. The Greatest Commandment - Love as the Heart of Everything

"Jesus replied: 'Love the Lord your God with all your heart and with all your soul and with all your mind.' This is the first and greatest commandment. And the second is like it: 'Love your neighbor as yourself.' All the Law and the Prophets hang on these two commandments."

— Matthew 22:37-40 (Jesus speaking)

"All the Law and the Prophets Hang" on Love

Jesus didn't say love was important. He said everything in Scripture hangs on these two love commands. The entire Old Testament law, all the prophets, every religious obligation—all of it is summarized in and dependent upon love.

If all Scripture hangs on love, then any interpretation that contradicts love (like eternal torture) is wrong. If God's essence is love, and all divine action must flow from love, then the doctrine of endless conscious torment collapses under its own weight.

"So in everything, do to others what you would have them do to you, for this sums up the Law and the Prophets."

— Matthew 7:12 (Jesus speaking - The Golden Rule)

The Golden Rule's Implications

Jesus says this single principle—treating others as you want to be treated—sums up all the Law and the Prophets. Would you want to be eternally tortured for finite sins? Then how can we believe God treats people that way? Jesus' ethics logically require universal salvation.

2. Love Your Enemies - The Most Radical Teaching

"But I tell you, love your enemies and pray for those who persecute you, that you may be children of your Father in heaven. He causes his sun to rise on the evil and the good, and sends rain on the just and the unjust... Be perfect, therefore, as your heavenly Father is perfect."

— Matthew 5:44-45, 48 (Jesus speaking)

"But to you who are listening I say: Love your enemies, do good to those who hate you, bless those who curse you, pray for those who mistreat you... But love your enemies, do good to them, and lend to them without expecting to get anything back. Then your reward will be great, and you will be children of the Most High, because he is kind to the ungrateful and wicked. Be merciful, just as your Father is merciful."

— Luke 6:27-28, 35-36 (Jesus speaking)

The Crushing Logical Problem for Eternal Hell

Jesus commands us to love our enemies. Not tolerate them. Not ignore them. Actively, genuinely LOVE them. Pray for them. Do good to them. Show them kindness even when they hate us.

If WE—finite, sinful humans—are commanded to love our enemies, does God do less? If WE must show mercy to the ungrateful and wicked, does God eternally torture them instead?

Jesus says we love enemies because that's what our Father does—He "causes his sun to rise on the evil and the good." God shows kindness to "the ungrateful and wicked." This is not describing a God who tortures His enemies forever. This is describing universal, indiscriminate, relentless divine love.

3. God So Loved THE WORLD - Universal Scope

"For God so loved the world that he gave his one and only Son, that whoever believes in him shall not perish but have eternal life. For God did not send his Son into the world to condemn the world, but to save the world through him."

— John 3:16-17 (Jesus speaking)

The Cosmos Loved

The Greek word for "world" here is kosmos (κόσμος)—the entire created order, including the material realm that Gnostics often viewed as the domain of the Demiurge. Yet even this fallen, archon-ruled cosmos is the object of God's love.

This is staggering: God doesn't love only the pneumatic (spiritual) souls, or only those who have received gnosis. God loves the world—all of creation, every human being, every soul trapped in matter, every divine spark scattered in the darkness.

And notice Jesus' explicit statement: "God did not send his Son into the world to condemn the world, but to SAVE the world through him." Not condemn. SAVE. The mission is universal salvation, not selective damnation.

"For I did not come to judge the world, but to save the world."

— John 12:47 (Jesus speaking)

"And I, when I am lifted up from the earth, will draw all people to myself."

— John 12:32 (Jesus speaking)

Jesus' Mission Statement: Save, Not Condemn

Jesus could not be clearer about His purpose. He came to SAVE the world, not to condemn it. He came to draw ALL people to Himself. Not "offer salvation to some." Not "condemn most." SAVE and draw ALL.

If Jesus' mission was to save the world, and most people end up in eternal torment anyway, then His mission was a catastrophic failure. But Jesus doesn't fail. "I will draw ALL people to myself" is a promise, not a maybe.

4. Love One Another - The New Commandment

"A new command I give you: Love one another. As I have loved you, so you must love one another. By this everyone will know that you are my disciples, if you love one another."

— John 13:34-35 (Jesus speaking)

"My command is this: Love each other as I have loved you. Greater love has no one than this: to lay down one's life for one's friends."

— John 15:12-13 (Jesus speaking)

Love as Christ Loved

Jesus doesn't just command love—He models it perfectly. He laid down His life for His friends (and even for His enemies). He loved without condition, without reservation, without limit.

And this is the standard He sets: "As I have loved you, so you must love one another." His love is the pattern. If we are called to love with Christ-like love—patient, forgiving, self-sacrificial, never-giving-up love—how much more does God Himself love this way?

5. The Father's Love for All

"The Father himself loves you because you have loved me and have believed that I came from God."

— John 16:27 (Jesus speaking)

"As the Father has loved me, so have I loved you. Now remain in my love."

— John 15:9 (Jesus speaking)

"I have made you known to them, and will continue to make you known in order that the love you have for me may be in them and that I myself may be in them."

— John 17:26 (Jesus' High Priestly Prayer)

The Love of the Trinity

Jesus reveals that the same love that flows between the Father and the Son is meant to flow through all believers. But even more radical—in John 17:23, Jesus prays that the Father would love His disciples "even as you have loved me." The Father's love for us is meant to be the same quality and intensity as His love for Christ!

Can we imagine the Father loving Christ, then eternally torturing most of Christ's brothers and sisters (humanity)? The love that defines the Godhead is inclusive, not exclusive. It seeks to draw all into the divine circle of love.

6. Forgiveness Without Limit

"Then Peter came to Jesus and asked, 'Lord, how many times shall I forgive my brother or sister who sins against me? Up to seven times?' Jesus answered, 'I tell you, not seven times, but seventy-seven times.'"

— Matthew 18:21-22 (Jesus speaking)

"Father, forgive them, for they do not know what they are doing."

— Luke 23:34 (Jesus speaking from the cross)

Limitless Forgiveness

Jesus tells Peter to forgive "seventy-seven times" (or "seventy times seven")—a way of saying without limit, infinitely. If WE are commanded to forgive without limit, does God forgive less? Does God run out of forgiveness after one lifetime?

And look at Jesus' example: while being murdered, He prays for His killers' forgiveness. He doesn't condemn them. He recognizes their ignorance ("they do not know what they are doing") and asks the Father to forgive. This is the heart of God revealed.

7. Judgment Based on Love

"The King will reply, 'Truly I tell you, whatever you did for one of the least of these brothers and sisters of mine, you did for me.'"

— Matthew 25:40 (Jesus speaking - Parable of the Sheep and Goats)

Love as the Standard

In Jesus' teaching about the final judgment (Matthew 25:31-46), the criterion isn't correct theology or religious observance. It's love expressed in action toward "the least of these"—feeding the hungry, clothing the naked, visiting prisoners.

Jesus identifies with the suffering. How we treat the least is how we treat Him. This reveals that God's judgment is always in service of love, not opposed to it. Even corrective judgment aims at teaching us to love.

"One of the teachers of the law came and heard them debating. Noticing that Jesus had given them a good answer, he asked him, 'Of all the commandments, which is the most important?' 'The most important one,' answered Jesus, 'is this: "Hear, O Israel: The Lord our God, the Lord is one. Love the Lord your God with all your heart and with all your soul and with all your mind and with all your strength." The second is this: "Love your neighbor as yourself." There is no commandment greater than these.'"

— Mark 12:28-31 (Jesus speaking)

The Scribe's Recognition

The scribe responds: "You are right in saying that God is one and there is no other but him. To love him with all your heart... and to love your neighbor as yourself is more important than all burnt offerings and sacrifices." And Jesus tells him, "You are not far from the kingdom of God" (Mark 12:32-34).

Love is more important than all religious rituals and sacrifices. Love is the doorway to the Kingdom. If love is supreme, then a theology built on fear and eternal torture fundamentally misses the Kingdom.

What Jesus NEVER Said

It's crucial to note what Jesus did NOT teach. These are claims often made about Jesus that have no basis in His actual words:

Jesus Never Said:

  • "Most of you are going to hell"
  • "God hates sinners"
  • "Only believers will be saved"
  • "Fear God's eternal wrath"
  • "Correct doctrine is more important than love"
  • "The majority of humanity will be tortured forever"

Jesus DID Say:

  • "Love your enemies" (Matthew 5:44)
  • "I came to save the world, not condemn it" (John 12:47)
  • "God makes His sun rise on evil and good" (Matthew 5:45)
  • "Be merciful as your Father is merciful" (Luke 6:36)
  • "Forgive seventy times seven" (Matthew 18:22)
  • "I will draw ALL people to myself" (John 12:32)

The Implications Are Inescapable

If we take Jesus' actual words seriously:

  • If WE must love enemies, how much more does God?
  • If WE must forgive limitlessly, how much more does God?
  • If WE must be merciful to the wicked, how much more is God?
  • If WE are commanded not to give up on anyone, does God give up on billions?

The doctrine of eternal hell makes God less loving, less forgiving, less merciful than He commands US to be. This is logically and theologically incoherent. Universal salvation is the only position consistent with Jesus' own teachings about love.

Biblical Foundations of Divine Love (Continued)

The Universality: Atonement for ALL

"He is the propitiation for our sins, and not for ours only but also for the sins of the whole world."

— 1 John 2:2

Not just for believers. Not just for the elect. Not just for those with gnosis. But for the whole world. The atonement of Christ covers all humanity—every person who has ever lived or will ever live.

The Desire: God Wants ALL to Be Saved

"This is good, and it is pleasing in the sight of God our Savior, who desires all people to be saved and to come to the knowledge of the truth."

— 1 Timothy 2:3-4

God's Will Cannot Be Thwarted

Here is the crucial question: Does God's will ultimately prevail? If God desires ALL people to be saved, and if God is omnipotent, and if love never fails, then can God's salvific will be eternally frustrated?

To say that some will be lost forever is to say that either: (1) God doesn't truly desire their salvation, or (2) God lacks the power to achieve what He desires, or (3) Love can fail. All three options contradict Scripture's clear testimony about God's nature.

The Patience: God is Willing to Wait

"The Lord is not slow to fulfill his promise as some count slowness, but is patient toward you, not wishing that any should perish, but that all should reach repentance."

— 2 Peter 3:9

God is patient. Eternity is long. From the divine perspective, a thousand years is as a day. God is willing to wait—through lifetimes, through ages, through aeons—for every lost sheep to be found, for every prodigal to come home.

Gnostic Understanding: The Monad's Overflowing Love

Creation as Love's Overflow

In Valentinian Gnostic cosmology, creation begins with the Monad—the ultimate, unknowable One—desiring to share its perfection. The Pleroma (Fullness) emerges as successive emanations of divine attributes, each aeon a further expression of the Monad's nature.

Why did this emanation occur? Why did the perfect One become many? The answer: Love cannot help but overflow. A spring of infinite water cannot be contained. Perfect love must give, must create, must share itself.

Sophia: Love's Longing

"And I, when I am lifted up from the earth, will draw all people to myself."

— John 12:32

The Gnostic myth of Sophia—divine Wisdom who fell from the Pleroma in her longing to know the unknowable Father—is ultimately a story about love. Sophia's fall represents the divine spark's descent into matter, but also love's willingness to enter even the darkest realms to bring light.

Christ's Descent: Love Pursuing the Fallen

When Sophia fell, did the Pleroma abandon her? No! Christ (or the Logos) descends from the Fullness specifically to find her, to restore her, to bring her home. This is the pattern of divine love: it pursues the lost into the uttermost depths.

Christ's descent through the archontic spheres, His veiling of divinity in flesh, His suffering and death—all of this demonstrates love's relentless pursuit. The archons thought they could kill him. Instead, He harrowed hell, freed the captives, and emerged victorious.

The Divine Spark: Love's Indestructible Seed

Every human soul carries a divine spark—a fragment of the Monad's light. This spark can be obscured, forgotten, buried under layers of ignorance and materiality. But it cannot be destroyed. Why? Because it is made of love itself.

Like a Magnet

The divine spark within each soul is magnetically drawn to its source. Just as iron filings cannot resist a powerful magnet, the soul—once awakened—cannot ultimately resist the pull of divine love calling it home to the Pleroma.

Homesickness

The restlessness of the human heart, the longing for transcendence, the sense that we don't belong in this world—all of this is the divine spark's homesickness for the Pleroma. We are exiles who remember, however dimly, our true home.

Eternal Return

The journey of the soul is circular: from the Monad, through the Pleroma, into matter, awakening to gnosis, ascending through the spheres, and returning home. This return is guaranteed because love never abandons what belongs to it.

Meister Eckhart's Vision

The Christian mystic Meister Eckhart proclaimed: "You cannot fall out of God." The divine spark is held in the palm of God's hand. We may forget God, but God never forgets us. We may wander far, but we remain forever in Love's embrace.

Love's Character in Corinthians

"Love is patient and kind; love does not envy or boast; it is not arrogant or rude. It does not insist on its own way; it is not irritable or resentful; it does not rejoice at wrongdoing, but rejoices with the truth. Love bears all things, believes all things, hopes all things, endures all things. Love never ends."

— 1 Corinthians 13:4-8

If this is the nature of love, and if God IS love, then we can trust that God is patient with every soul, bearing with our ignorance, believing in our eventual awakening, hoping for our return, enduring our rebellion—and never, ever giving up.

The Implications: If God Is Love

Hell Cannot Be Eternal

If God is love—infinite, perfect, never-failing love—then eternal conscious torment is impossible. Not because God is "too nice" to punish sin, but because eternal torture is fundamentally incompatible with the nature of love.

A human parent who tortured their child eternally for temporal rebellion would be called a monster, not loving. How much more is this true of the infinite Love that created us? The early Church Fathers who taught universal salvation understood this: God's punishment is always remedial, never vindictive. It is the surgery of love, painful but healing.

Every Knee Will Bow—In Worship

"Therefore God has highly exalted him and bestowed on him the name that is above every name, so that at the name of Jesus every knee should bow, in heaven and on earth and under the earth, and every tongue confess that Jesus Christ is Lord, to the glory of God the Father."

— Philippians 2:9-11

This confession "to the glory of God the Father" is not forced submission but genuine worship. Paul uses the language of conversion and salvation here. Eventually, when all have awakened to the truth of God's love, when the last veil of ignorance has been lifted, when even the archons recognize the supreme authority of Christ—all will bow not in defeat but in grateful worship.

The Pleroma Cannot Be Full Until All Return

The very word "Pleroma" means "fullness" or "totality." How can the divine Fullness be truly full if any divine spark remains lost in matter? How can the Monad be complete if any fragment of its light remains in darkness?

The Gnostic vision of restoration requires that all spiritual seeds return home. The shepherd must find ALL the lost sheep. The woman must recover ALL the lost coins. The Father must welcome home ALL the prodigals. Only then is the family complete. Only then is the celebration fitting. Only then does Love achieve its purpose.

Love Perfects Those It Loves

"And I am sure of this, that he who began a good work in you will bring it to completion at the day of Jesus Christ."

— Philippians 1:6

God doesn't give up on His projects. The work that love begins, love completes. Every soul is God's project—a work of art in progress. The sculptor doesn't abandon the marble before the masterpiece is revealed. The potter doesn't discard the clay while it's still being shaped.

The Heart of the Gospel: Overwhelmingly Good News

You are loved. Not because you're good, spiritual, or enlightened. Not because you have achieved gnosis or performed religious duties. You are loved because God IS love, and Love cannot help loving.

You cannot outrun this love. You cannot exhaust it. You cannot lose it. Even if you spend lifetimes wandering in the far country, feeding among the pigs, forgetting your true home—Love is watching for your return. Love is already running to meet you.

And eventually—whether in this life or beyond, whether through suffering that purifies or revelations that awaken—you will see Love's face. And you will remember who you truly are. And you will come home.

This is not wishful thinking. This is the nature of God. This is the promise of Scripture. This is the hope of the Gospel.
