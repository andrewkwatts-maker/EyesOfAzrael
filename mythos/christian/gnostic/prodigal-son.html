Was Dead, Now Alive

The Greatest Story Ever Told About Coming Home

"But while he was still a long way off, his father saw him and was filled with compassion for him; he ran to his son, threw his arms around him and kissed him."
— Luke 15:20

The Parable: Luke 15:11-32

Jesus said: "There was a man who had two sons. The younger one said to his father, 'Father, give me my share of the estate.' So he divided his property between them. Not long after that, the younger son got together all he had, set off for a distant country and there squandered his wealth in wild living..."

1. Demanding the Inheritance

"Father, give me my share of the estate."

Gnostic Interpretation: Separation from the Divine

The son demanding his inheritance represents the soul's desire to exist independently of God, to explore its own autonomy. In Gnostic terms, this is the descent of divine sparks from the Pleroma into the material realm. We want our "share"—our own existence, our own identity separate from the Source.

The father grants this request. God does not force souls to remain in divine union. Love allows freedom, even the freedom to leave home, to descend into matter, to forget one's true origin.

2. The Far Country

"He set off for a distant country..."

Gnostic Interpretation: Descent into Matter

The "far country" is the material cosmos—the realm ruled by the Demiurge and archons, distant from the Pleroma. It is the world of form, decay, and death. Here, the divine spark finds itself far from home, in a foreign land that does not recognize its true nature.

The distance is not merely spatial but ontological. The soul has descended from the realm of pure spirit into the realm of matter, from light into darkness, from consciousness into forgetfulness.

3. Squandering the Wealth

"...and there squandered his wealth in wild living."

Gnostic Interpretation: Losing Gnosis

The "wealth" is gnosis—the knowledge of one's divine origin. The "wild living" represents attachment to material pleasures, identification with the body and ego, forgetfulness of one's spiritual nature.

We squander our divine inheritance by investing ourselves entirely in the material world, by believing the illusion that we are merely bodies, merely creatures of matter. We forget who we are, where we came from, and who our Father is.

4. Famine and Poverty

"After he had spent everything, there was a severe famine in that whole country, and he began to be in need."

Gnostic Interpretation: Spiritual Hunger

The famine represents the soul's realization that material things cannot satisfy spiritual hunger. No amount of worldly pleasure, success, or knowledge can fill the void left by separation from God.

This is the beginning of awakening—the recognition that something is wrong, that we are starving in a land that cannot feed us, that we need something this world cannot provide.

5. Feeding Pigs

"So he went and hired himself out to a citizen of that country, who sent him to his fields to feed pigs. He longed to fill his stomach with the pods that the pigs were eating, but no one gave him anything."

Gnostic Interpretation: Servitude to Archons

The pigs, unclean animals in Jewish thought, represent the archons and the degradation of the material realm. The son has become a slave to the "citizen of that country"—the Demiurge or the archontic powers that rule the material cosmos.

He is so degraded that he desires even the food of pigs—the lowest spiritual state imaginable. This is the soul's darkest moment: enslaved, degraded, forgotten by all, and nearly forgetting itself.

6. Coming to Himself

"When he came to his senses, he said, 'How many of my father's hired servants have food to spare, and here I am starving to death!'"

Gnostic Interpretation: Gnosis Awakens!

This is the moment of gnosis—awakening to one's true condition. The phrase "came to his senses" is literally "came to himself" in Greek. He remembers who he is: a son, not a pig-feeder. He remembers where he came from: a father's house of abundance, not a land of famine.

This is the divine spark flickering back to life within the soul. Memory returns. Recognition dawns. "I am not this degraded creature. I am a son of the Father. I have a home, and it is not here."

7. The Decision to Return

"I will set out and go back to my father and say to him: Father, I have sinned against heaven and against you. I am no longer worthy to be called your son; make me like one of your hired servants."

Gnostic Interpretation: The Ascent Begins

Having awakened to his true identity, the son decides to return. This is repentance (Greek: metanoia—literally "change of mind"). The soul turns away from matter and toward spirit, from the archons toward the Pleroma.

Notice his humility: "Make me like one of your hired servants." He doesn't presume full restoration. He simply longs to be near his father again, even in the lowest position. But watch what actually happens...

8. The Father RUNS

"But while he was still a long way off, his father saw him and was filled with compassion for him; he ran to his son, threw his arms around him and kissed him."

Gnostic Interpretation: The Divine Descent to Meet Us

This is the most staggering moment in the parable. The father doesn't wait at home. He doesn't stand with arms crossed, demanding an apology. He SEES the son while still far off (he was watching!), and he RUNS.

In first-century Jewish culture, dignified patriarchs did not run—it was beneath their dignity. But this father hikes up his robes and sprints toward his son. This is the divine pursuit! This is Christ descending from the Pleroma to seek Sophia! This is God racing to meet the returning soul!

God doesn't wait for us to complete the journey. God meets us halfway—no, God covers most of the distance! While we are still far off, still degraded, still stinking of pigs—Love races toward us.

9. Full Restoration

"The father said to his servants, 'Quick! Bring the best robe and put it on him. Put a ring on his finger and sandals on his feet. Bring the fattened calf and kill it. Let's have a feast and celebrate. For this son of mine was dead and is alive again; he was lost and is found.'"

Gnostic Interpretation: Return to the Pleroma

The son wanted to be a servant. The father restores him as a SON. Not partial restoration. Not probationary acceptance. Full, immediate, lavish restoration:

  • The best robe - Restoration of glory and honor (the spiritual body)
  • A ring - Authority and sonship (divine identity restored)
  • Sandals - Not a slave (slaves went barefoot) but a free son
  • Fattened calf - The greatest celebration possible

The soul returning to the Pleroma is not grudgingly accepted. It is celebrated! Heaven rejoices! The divine realm is incomplete without this one lost spark, and now it has returned. The fullness is restored.

"Was Dead and Is Alive Again"

This phrase reveals everything. The son wasn't waiting to die—he was already DEAD while living in the far country. Death is separation from God, not physical mortality.

And now? "IS ALIVE AGAIN." Not "will be alive someday if he proves himself." Not "might be alive if he works hard enough." He IS alive—immediately, completely, gloriously alive—the moment he returns to the father's embrace.

This is resurrection. This is salvation. This is the return to the Pleroma. This is coming home.

The Older Brother: The Archon's Religion

But the story doesn't end with the younger son's return. There's a darker counterpoint: the older brother who refuses to celebrate.

The Complaint of the Self-Righteous

"Look! All these years I've been slaving for you and never disobeyed your orders. Yet you never gave me even a young goat so I could celebrate with my friends. But when this son of yours who has squandered your property with prostitutes comes home, you kill the fattened calf for him!"

Gnostic Interpretation: The Archontic Mindset

The older brother represents religious legalism—the mindset of those who believe salvation is earned through works, obedience, and moral superiority. This is the religion of the archons: rule-keeping, sacrifice-demanding, merit-based.

Notice his language: "slaving for you" (he sees himself as a slave, not a son), "never disobeyed" (rule-keeping, not relationship), "you never gave me" (entitled, not grateful). He has lived in the father's house but never known the father's heart.

He cannot celebrate grace because he doesn't understand grace. He has earned his place (he thinks), and therefore the younger son should earn his too. The free gift of restoration offends him. This is the mindset that crucified Jesus.

The Father's Response: "Everything I Have Is Yours"

"My son, you are always with me, and everything I have is yours. But we had to celebrate and be glad, because this brother of yours was dead and is alive again; he was lost and is found."

The father's love extends to both sons. The older brother, too, is invited to the feast. But will he enter? The parable ends with the question unanswered. Jesus leaves it hanging, an invitation to the Pharisees listening: Will you join the celebration? Will you rejoice that sinners are coming home? Or will you stand outside, angry that grace is free?

What This Means for Us

If You Are the Younger Son

No matter how far you've wandered, how much you've squandered, how degraded you feel—the Father is watching for your return. You don't have to clean yourself up first. You don't have to achieve gnosis before approaching. Come as you are—stinking of pigs, covered in shame, broke and broken.

The Father will run to meet you. He will cover your shame with the best robe. He will restore your sonship with a ring. He will celebrate your return with a feast. You were dead; now you are alive. You were lost; now you are found.

If You Are the Older Brother

If you've lived your whole life trying to earn God's love through spiritual practices, perfect theology, or moral superiority—you've missed the point. The father's love is not earned; it's given. The celebration is not for those who stayed home and behaved; it's for those who return.

Stop counting your good deeds. Stop comparing yourself to "sinners." Join the party. Celebrate that grace is free. Rejoice that love cannot be earned, because that means it also cannot be lost.

The Ultimate Message

Every soul is a prodigal. We have all left the Father's house, descended into matter, squandered our divine inheritance, and found ourselves starving in a far country.

But the story doesn't end in the pigpen. The moment we come to ourselves—the moment gnosis awakens—we can begin the journey home. And we will find that the Father has been watching all along, waiting for the first sign of our return.

He will run to meet us. He will embrace us while we're still far off. He will restore us fully. He will celebrate our return. Because love doesn't hold grudges. Grace doesn't demand payment. The Father simply wants His children home.

And eventually, every prodigal will come home.
