ğŸ™ Our Father in Heaven

Matthew 6:9-13: The model prayer Jesus taught his disciples. Not a magic formula to be recited, but a pattern for all Christian prayer - encompassing God's glory, his kingdom, our needs, forgiveness, and deliverance.

Our Father in heaven,
hallowed be your name,
your kingdom come,
your will be done,
on earth as it is in heaven.
Give us today our daily bread.
And forgive us our debts,
as we also have forgiven our debtors.
And lead us not into temptation,
but deliver us from the evil one.
[For yours is the kingdom and the power and the glory forever. Amen.]

The doxology in brackets is included in some manuscripts and ancient liturgies, though not in the earliest Greek texts.

ğŸ“Š Matthew vs. Luke

The Lord's Prayer appears in two Gospels with slight variations:

Matthew 6:9-13 Luke 11:2-4
"Our Father in heaven" "Father"
"Hallowed be your name" "Hallowed be your name"
"Your kingdom come, your will be done, on earth as in heaven" "Your kingdom come"
"Give us today our daily bread" "Give us each day our daily bread"
"Forgive us our debts, as we also have forgiven our debtors" "Forgive us our sins, for we also forgive everyone who sins against us"
"Lead us not into temptation, but deliver us from the evil one" "Lead us not into temptation"

Note: Matthew's version is longer and more liturgical; Luke's is shorter and simpler. Both are authentic teachings of Jesus given on different occasions. Matthew's version became the standard for Christian worship.

ğŸ›ï¸ Structure: Six Petitions

The Lord's Prayer has two major parts:

Part I: GOD-CENTERED (Petitions 1-3)

  1. Hallowed be your name - God's honor
  2. Your kingdom come - God's rule
  3. Your will be done - God's purposes

First, we pray for God's glory and kingdom. His honor comes before our needs.

Part II: HUMAN-CENTERED (Petitions 4-6)

  1. Give us our daily bread - Provision for today
  2. Forgive us our debts - Pardon for yesterday
  3. Lead us not into temptation - Protection for tomorrow

Then we bring our needs: provision, forgiveness, protection. Past, present, and future are all covered.

8. Doxology: "For Yours Is the Kingdom and the Power and the Glory Forever. Amen"

Note: This doxology appears in later manuscripts and was likely added from early Christian liturgy. It's included in many Protestant traditions but omitted in Catholic recitations.

Meaning:

  • "Yours is the kingdom" - All authority belongs to God
  • "The power" - All strength and might belong to God
  • "The glory" - All honor and praise belong to God
  • "Forever" - God's reign is eternal

Whether original or not, the doxology fittingly concludes the prayer by returning praise to God. We began with "Our Father" and end with "Yours is the kingdom."

1 Chronicles 29:11 - "Yours, LORD, is the greatness and the power and the glory and the majesty and the splendor, for everything in heaven and earth is yours. Yours, LORD, is the kingdom."

ğŸ• Jewish Background: The Kaddish

The Lord's Prayer echoes themes from Jewish prayers, especially the Kaddish, an ancient Aramaic prayer:

"Magnified and sanctified be his great name in the world which he has created according to his will. May he establish his kingdom in your lifetime and in your days and in the lifetime of all the house of Israel, even speedily and at a near time."

Notice the parallels:

  • Kaddish: "Magnified and sanctified be his great name" â†’ Lord's Prayer: "Hallowed be your name"
  • Kaddish: "May he establish his kingdom" â†’ Lord's Prayer: "Your kingdom come"

Jesus was teaching his disciples to pray in a way rooted in Jewish piety, but radically re-centered on the intimate Fatherhood of God.
