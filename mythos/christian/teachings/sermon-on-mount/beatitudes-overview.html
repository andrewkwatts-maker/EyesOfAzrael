Matthew 5:3-12: Eight revolutionary blessings that overturn worldly values and reveal the character of God's kingdom citizens. These are not moral achievements but portraits of those whom God declares blessed - ultimately, a portrait of Jesus himself.

Meaning: Blessed, happy, fortunate, to be envied. More than temporary happiness - this is deep, spiritual well-being and divine favor. The Greek term suggests a state of flourishing under God's smile.

Latin: Beatus

The Latin Vulgate translated makarios as beatus, giving us the name "Beatitudes" (from beatitudo, "blessedness").

Not "Happy" But "Blessed"

Modern translations say "blessed" rather than "happy" because makarios is not dependent on circumstances. The poor in spirit, those who mourn, the persecuted - these don't feel happy by worldly standards, yet God declares them blessed because they belong to his kingdom.

ğŸ”„ The Structure of the Beatitudes

Bookends: Kingdom of Heaven

Notice that the first (poor in spirit) and last (persecuted) beatitudes both promise "theirs is the kingdom of heaven" (present tense). The kingdom brackets the beatitudes - it is the great gift, already present.

Two Groups of Four

  • Beatitudes 1-4: Our need before God (poor, mourning, meek, hungry)
  • Beatitudes 5-8: Our response to others (merciful, pure, peacemaking, persecuted)

Present vs. Future

  • Present tense (1st & 8th): "theirs is the kingdom" - already/now
  • Future tense (2-7): "they will be comforted/inherit/filled/shown mercy/see God/be called" - not yet/future

This reflects the "already but not yet" nature of God's kingdom.

ğŸ“– Old Testament Parallels

The Beatitudes echo wisdom from the Hebrew Scriptures:

  • Psalm 1 - "Blessed is the one who does not walk in step with the wicked..."
  • Psalm 37:11 - "The meek will inherit the land" (quoted in Matt 5:5)
  • Psalm 51:17 - "A broken and contrite heart, O God, you will not despise" (poor in spirit)
  • Isaiah 57:15 - "I live with the one who is contrite and lowly in spirit"
  • Isaiah 61:1-3 - "The LORD has anointed me... to comfort all who mourn" (quoted in Matt 5:4)

âœï¸ The Beatitudes Are a Portrait of Jesus

The ultimate fulfillment of the Beatitudes is not us striving to achieve them, but recognizing that Jesus himself perfectly embodied every beatitude:

  • Poor in spirit - Philippians 2:6-7, Jesus emptied himself
  • Mourn - John 11:35, "Jesus wept"; Isaiah 53:3, "man of sorrows"
  • Meek - Matthew 11:29, "I am gentle and humble in heart"
  • Hunger for righteousness - John 4:34, "My food is to do the will of him who sent me"
  • Merciful - Luke 23:34, "Father, forgive them"
  • Pure in heart - Hebrews 4:15, tempted in every way yet without sin
  • Peacemaker - Ephesians 2:14, "He himself is our peace"
  • Persecuted - 1 Peter 2:23, reviled but did not revile in return

We become blessed not by achieving the beatitudes through moral effort, but by being united to Christ who embodies them perfectly.
