‚õ∞Ô∏è "Be Perfect As Your Father Is Perfect"

Matthew 5:48: The Sermon on the Mount reveals an impossible standard that drives us to Christ. We cannot fulfill it - but Jesus did. The Sermon's purpose is to show us our need for grace and to point us to the one who perfectly embodies its teachings.

üö´ The Impossible Demands

If we're honest, the Sermon on the Mount is impossible for fallen humanity to achieve:

What the Sermon Demands:

  • Never be angry (5:22) - But we all get angry
  • Never lust (5:28) - But we all struggle with impure thoughts
  • Never retaliate (5:39) - But our instinct is self-defense
  • Love enemies (5:44) - But we naturally love those who love us
  • Be perfect as God is perfect (5:48) - But we are sinners
  • Don't be anxious about anything (6:25) - But we all worry
  • Never judge (7:1) - But we constantly evaluate others

The verdict: "Unless your righteousness surpasses that of the Pharisees and the teachers of the law, you will certainly not enter the kingdom of heaven" (Matthew 5:20). No one's righteousness surpasses the Pharisees by their own effort!

ü§î Three Wrong Interpretations

1. Legalism: "Try Harder!"

The error: Read the Sermon as a moral code to achieve through effort. "If I just try hard enough, I can be perfect."

The problem: This leads to despair (because you fail) or pride (because you think you've succeeded). It misses the point that the standard is impossible.

2. Antinomianism: "It's All Grace, Standards Don't Matter"

The error: Dismiss the Sermon as irrelevant. "Jesus fulfilled the law for me, so I don't need to pursue holiness."

The problem: Cheap grace. Jesus calls disciples to radical obedience, not lawlessness (Matthew 7:21-23).

3. Dual-Level Ethics: "This Is Only for Super-Christians"

The error: Say the Sermon is for a spiritual elite (monks, clergy) while ordinary Christians live by lower standards.

The problem: Jesus addressed the sermon to all his disciples, not an elite class. It's meant for every Christian.

‚úÖ The Right Interpretation: Law and Gospel

The Sermon Has Two Functions

1. LAW: Reveals Our Sin

The Sermon exposes our moral inability. It's a mirror that shows us how far we fall short of God's perfection. It drives us to despair of our own righteousness and casts us on God's mercy.

Romans 3:20 - "Through the law we become conscious of our sin."
Galatians 3:24 - "The law was our guardian until Christ came, that we might be justified by faith."

2. GOSPEL: Points to Christ

The Sermon points us to Jesus, who alone fulfilled its impossible standard. Jesus is poor in spirit, meek, merciful, pure in heart. Jesus never sinned, never lusted, loved his enemies, and was perfect. He lived the Sermon for us.

Romans 8:3-4 - "What the law was powerless to do... God did by sending his own Son... in order that the righteous requirement of the law might be fully met in us."

‚úùÔ∏è Jesus: The True Fulfillment

Jesus Perfectly Embodied the Sermon

Poor in spirit - Philippians 2:7, "He made himself nothing"
Mourned - John 11:35, "Jesus wept"
Meek - Matthew 11:29, "I am gentle and humble in heart"
Hungered for righteousness - John 4:34, "My food is to do the will of God"
Merciful - Luke 23:34, "Father, forgive them"
Pure in heart - Hebrews 4:15, "without sin"
Peacemaker - Ephesians 2:14, "He himself is our peace"
Persecuted - 1 Peter 2:23, "When they hurled their insults at him, he did not retaliate"
Perfect - 2 Corinthians 5:21, "God made him who had no sin to be sin for us"

We cannot live the Sermon. Jesus did. And through faith in him, his righteousness becomes ours.

üîÑ The Paradox: Law ‚Üí Gospel ‚Üí Transformation

The Three-Stage Movement

  1. CONVICTION (Law) - The Sermon shows me I cannot save myself. I am not poor in spirit, meek, merciful, or pure in heart. I fall short. I need a Savior.
  2. SALVATION (Gospel) - I trust in Jesus, who fulfilled the Sermon perfectly in my place. His righteousness is credited to me (justification). I am saved by grace alone through faith alone.
  3. TRANSFORMATION (Sanctification) - United to Christ by faith, the Holy Spirit begins to transform me to become like him. I grow in the Sermon's virtues - not to earn salvation, but as a fruit of it. I pursue holiness, empowered by grace.
Philippians 2:12-13 - "Work out your salvation with fear and trembling, for it is God who works in you to will and to act in order to fulfill his good purpose."
2 Corinthians 3:18 - "And we all, who with unveiled faces contemplate the Lord's glory, are being transformed into his image with ever-increasing glory, which comes from the Lord, who is the Spirit."

üéØ How Then Shall We Live?

The Sermon Is Still Relevant for Christians

Even though we're saved by grace, the Sermon on the Mount remains our guide:

1. It Shows Us What Kingdom Life Looks Like

The Sermon describes the character and conduct of citizens of God's kingdom. It's aspirational - the kind of people God is making us to be.

2. It Drives Us to Dependence on Grace

Every time we read the Sermon, we're reminded: "I can't do this on my own. I need Jesus. I need the Spirit's power." It keeps us humble and dependent.

3. It Guides Our Sanctification

The Sermon shows us what Christlikeness looks like. As the Spirit transforms us, we grow in these virtues - not perfectly, but progressively.

4. It Exposes Ongoing Sin

The Sermon is a constant mirror, showing us where we still fall short and driving us back to the cross for forgiveness and renewal.

Romans 8:29 - "For those God foreknew he also predestined to be conformed to the image of his Son."
Galatians 5:16 - "Walk by the Spirit, and you will not gratify the desires of the flesh."

üí¨ Theological Voices

Martin Luther: "The purpose of the Sermon on the Mount is not to make us good but to show us that we are not good, and to drive us to Christ."
John Stott: "The Sermon on the Mount is not a set of rules and regulations for good behavior. It is, rather, a description of the character and conduct of Christians who are being transformed by the grace of God."
D.A. Carson: "The Sermon on the Mount is simultaneously impossible (as a means of earning salvation) and mandatory (as the pattern for kingdom living)."
Augustine: "The righteousness of the Sermon on the Mount is the righteousness of Christ, which he requires of us and which he himself provides for us through the Spirit."

üéØ Summary: The Gospel in the Sermon

The Sermon on the Mount Shows Us:

  1. Our Need - We cannot fulfill God's perfect standard
  2. Our Savior - Jesus fulfilled it for us
  3. Our Identity - In Christ, we are justified (declared righteous)
  4. Our Calling - By the Spirit, we pursue Christlikeness
  5. Our Hope - One day we will be perfected (glorification)

The Sermon is impossible - and that's the point. It drives us to Jesus, who is our righteousness.
