The Twelve Apostles - Chosen Witnesses of the Christ

The Twelve Disciples were ordinary men chosen by Jesus to be His closest followers, witnesses to His ministry, death, and resurrection, and founders of His Church. From fishermen to tax collectors, from zealots to skeptics, they became the foundation stones of Christianity, carrying the Gospel to the ends of the earth and sealing their testimony with their blood.

"And he appointed twelve (whom he also named apostles) so that they might be with him and he might send them out to preach and have authority to cast out demons." ‚Äî Mark 3:14-15 (ESV)

üìú The Biblical Lists of the Twelve

The Twelve Disciples are named in four places in the New Testament, with minor variations in order and naming conventions:

  • Matthew 10:2-4: "The names of the twelve apostles are these: first, Simon, who is called Peter, and Andrew his brother; James the son of Zebedee, and John his brother; Philip and Bartholomew; Thomas and Matthew the tax collector; James the son of Alphaeus, and Thaddaeus; Simon the Zealot, and Judas Iscariot, who betrayed him."
  • Mark 3:16-19: Lists the Twelve with slight variations in order
  • Luke 6:13-16: Calls Thaddaeus "Judas son of James"
  • Acts 1:13: Lists the Eleven after Judas's death, before Matthias's selection

Simon Peter

"The Rock" - First Among the Apostles

Fisherman from Bethsaida, leader of the Twelve, first Pope. Denied Christ three times but was restored to feed His sheep. Crucified upside down in Rome under Nero.

Symbol: Keys, inverted cross, rooster

Andrew

"The First Called" - Bringer of Souls

Peter's brother, disciple of John the Baptist, first to follow Jesus. Brought Peter, the boy with loaves and fishes, and Greeks to Jesus. Martyred on an X-shaped cross.

Symbol: X-shaped cross (St. Andrew's Cross)

"Son of Thunder" - First Apostolic Martyr

Son of Zebedee, brother of John, member of Christ's inner circle. Witnessed Transfiguration and Gethsemane. First apostle martyred, beheaded by Herod Agrippa (44 CE).

Symbol: Sword, scallop shell, pilgrim's staff

"The Beloved" - Apostle of Love

Son of Zebedee, "disciple whom Jesus loved," leaned on Jesus' breast at Last Supper. Gospel author, wrote Revelation. Only apostle not martyred, lived to old age in Ephesus.

"Judas son of James" - Lebbaeus

"The Canaanite" - The Revolutionary

Called "the Zealot" or "Canaanite" (from Aramaic "zealous one"). May have been part of Zealot movement against Rome. Tradition says preached in Egypt, Persia. Martyred by being sawn in half.

Symbol: Saw, fish, oar, book

"The Betrayer" - Son of Perdition

Treasurer of the Twelve, betrayed Jesus for 30 pieces of silver with a kiss in Gethsemane. Overcome by guilt, returned money to priests, hanged himself. Replaced by Matthias.

Symbol: 30 silver coins, money bag, noose

‚ûï The Replacement: Matthias

After Judas's betrayal and death, the remaining eleven apostles gathered in the upper room to choose a replacement. The qualifications were clear: the candidate must have accompanied Jesus from His baptism by John through the Ascension. Two men were proposed: Joseph called Barsabbas (Justus) and Matthias. After prayer, they cast lots, and Matthias was chosen to restore the Twelve.

"So one of the men who have accompanied us during all the time that the Lord Jesus went in and out among us, beginning from the baptism of John until the day when he was taken up from us‚Äîone of these men must become with us a witness to his resurrection." ‚Äî Acts 1:21-22 (ESV)

Matthias: Little is known of his ministry from Scripture. Tradition says he preached in Judea, Ethiopia, or Cappadocia. Various traditions report his martyrdom by stoning, beheading, or crucifixion. His symbol is typically an axe or halberd.

‚≠ê Paul: The Apostle to the Gentiles

While not one of the original Twelve, Paul (formerly Saul of Tarsus) is often counted among the apostles due to his direct calling by the risen Christ and his unparalleled missionary work. A Pharisee who persecuted Christians, he encountered Jesus in a blinding vision on the Damascus Road and became Christianity's greatest missionary and theologian.

"Am I not an apostle? Have I not seen Jesus our Lord? Are not you my workmanship in the Lord? If to others I am not an apostle, at least I am to you, for you are the seal of my apostleship in the Lord." ‚Äî 1 Corinthians 9:1-2 (ESV)

Paul wrote at least 13 New Testament letters, established churches throughout the Roman Empire, and brought the Gospel to the Gentile world. He was martyred in Rome under Nero, traditionally beheaded as a Roman citizen. His feast day is June 29, shared with Peter.

üî• The Calling and Commission

Why Twelve?

The number twelve is deeply symbolic, echoing the twelve tribes of Israel. Jesus was reconstituting Israel around Himself, creating a new covenant people. The Twelve would sit on twelve thrones judging the twelve tribes of Israel (Matthew 19:28). They represented the foundation of the new Israel, the Church.

Ordinary Men, Extraordinary Calling

The disciples were not scholars, priests, or members of the religious elite. They were fishermen, a tax collector, a political revolutionary‚Äîordinary men with ordinary flaws. Peter was impulsive and cowardly. Thomas doubted. James and John were ambitious. Matthew was a collaborator with Rome. Judas betrayed his master.

Yet Christ chose them deliberately. He spent whole nights in prayer before selecting them. He taught them for three years, allowing them to witness His miracles, hear His teachings, and experience His presence. After His resurrection and the coming of the Holy Spirit at Pentecost, these frightened, confused men became bold proclaimers who turned the world upside down.

The Great Commission

"All authority in heaven and on earth has been given to me. Go therefore and make disciples of all nations, baptizing them in the name of the Father and of the Son and of the Holy Spirit, teaching them to observe all that I have commanded you. And behold, I am with you always, to the end of the age." ‚Äî Matthew 28:18-20 (ESV)

ü©∏ Martyrdom: The Blood of the Martyrs

Tradition holds that all of the apostles except John died as martyrs for their faith. They were crucified, stoned, stabbed, sawn in half, flayed alive, and beheaded. They could have saved their lives by denying Christ, but they refused. Their willingness to die for their testimony to the resurrection provides powerful evidence for the truth of their witness.

  • Peter: Crucified upside down in Rome (64-68 CE)
  • Andrew: Crucified on X-shaped cross in Patras, Greece
  • James the Greater: Beheaded in Jerusalem (44 CE) - only martyrdom recorded in Scripture
  • John: Survived boiling in oil, died of natural causes in Ephesus (c. 100 CE)
  • Philip: Crucified in Hierapolis, Phrygia
  • Bartholomew: Flayed alive and beheaded in Armenia
  • Matthew: Martyred in Ethiopia (speared or burned)
  • Thomas: Speared to death in India
  • James the Less: Thrown from Temple, stoned, clubbed in Jerusalem
  • Thaddaeus: Killed with club or axe in Persia
  • Simon the Zealot: Sawn in half in Persia
  • Matthias: Stoned and beheaded in Jerusalem or Ethiopia
  • Paul: Beheaded in Rome (64-68 CE)

"The blood of the martyrs is the seed of the Church." ‚Äî Tertullian

üåç Spreading the Gospel to the Ends of the Earth

After Pentecost, the apostles fanned out across the known world. According to various traditions:

  • Peter: Jerusalem, Antioch, Rome
  • Andrew: Scythia (Ukraine/Russia), Greece, Asia Minor
  • James the Greater: Judea, possibly Spain (Santiago de Compostela tradition)
  • John: Jerusalem, Ephesus, Patmos (exile)
  • Philip: Asia Minor, Phrygia
  • Bartholomew: Armenia, India, Mesopotamia
  • Matthew: Judea, Ethiopia, Persia
  • Thomas: Parthia, India (Mar Thoma Christians trace to him)
  • James the Less: Jerusalem (first bishop)
  • Thaddaeus: Mesopotamia, Armenia, Persia
  • Simon the Zealot: Persia, Egypt
  • Paul: Asia Minor, Greece, Macedonia, Rome, possibly Spain

Through their preaching, miracles, and martyrdom, they planted churches throughout the Roman Empire and beyond‚Äîin Arabia, India, Ethiopia, Armenia, and Persia. The Church they founded continues to this day, spanning every nation and culture on earth.
