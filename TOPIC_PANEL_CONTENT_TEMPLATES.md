# Topic Panel Content Templates

This document outlines content templates for generating rich topic panel content for each asset type.

## Overview

Topic panels add depth and engagement to entity pages through:
- **Background & Origins**: Historical and cultural context
- **Cultural Significance**: Why this entity matters
- **Related Entities**: Connected deities, heroes, creatures, etc.
- **Did You Know?**: Interesting facts and trivia
- **Sources & Further Reading**: Primary texts and references

---

## Template by Asset Type

### 1. DEITIES

#### Background & Origins
- **Birth/Creation Story**: How the deity came into being
- **Etymology**: Meaning and linguistic origins of the name
- **Symbolism**: What symbols represent and why
- **Divine Attributes**: Powers, domains, characteristics

**Example (Athena)**:
> Born fully armed from the head of Zeus after he swallowed her mother Metis, Athena represents pure intellect and strategic thought made manifest. Her birth from Zeus's head symbolizes wisdom emerging directly from divine intellect—thought made manifest without the mediation of physical birth. The name Athena appears to be pre-Greek, adopted by Greek speakers, with attestation in Linear B as 'a-ta-na po-ti-ni-ja' (Lady Athena) c. 1400 BCE.

#### Cultural Significance
- **Worship Centers**: Major temples and sacred sites
- **Festival & Rituals**: How deity was honored
- **Cultural Impact**: Influence on art, literature, philosophy
- **Modern Legacy**: Continued relevance today
- **Archetypal Role**: What archetype they embody

**Example (Athena)**:
> Athena was the most important deity for Athens, which bore her name and identity. The Parthenon, architectural masterpiece of the ancient world, housed Phidias's colossal gold-and-ivory statue. She embodies the **Wisdom Goddess** archetype—divine intellect personified, combining strategic thinking, practical skills, and philosophical depth.

#### Did You Know?
- Alternative names and epithets
- Sacred animals/plants
- First historical attestation
- Unique attributes or powers
- Connections to other mythologies
- Modern cultural references

**Example (Athena)**:
- ✨ Also known as **Pallas Athena**, **Glaukopis (Owl-Eyed)**, and **Athena Promachos (Champion)**
- ✨ Sacred symbols include the **owl** and **olive tree**
- ✨ First attested in written records around **c. 1400 BCE** in Linear B tablets
- ✨ Written in original script as **Ἀθηνᾶ (Athēnâ)**
- ✨ Strongly embodies the **Wisdom Goddess** and **Virgin Goddess** archetypes
- ✨ Associated with **strategic warfare**, **crafts**, and **civilization**

### 2. CREATURES

#### Background & Origins
- **Creation/Birth**: How creature came to be
- **Physical Description**: Appearance and unique features
- **Powers & Abilities**: What makes them formidable
- **Habitat**: Where they dwell
- **Purpose**: Role in mythology

**Example (Hydra)**:
> Born from Typhon and Echidna, the Lernaean Hydra possessed multiple heads (varying accounts give between seven and hundreds), with the central head being immortal. Its most terrifying feature was its regenerative power: when one head was cut off, two would grow back in its place, making conventional combat futile.

#### Cultural Significance
- **Symbolic Meaning**: What the creature represents
- **Story Role**: Part in major myths
- **Modern Usage**: How name/concept is used today
- **Archetypal Pattern**: Universal pattern embodied

**Example (Hydra)**:
> The Hydra symbolizes problems that multiply when attacked conventionally—cutting off one head produces two, representing how direct confrontation can worsen certain challenges. The term 'hydra-headed' describes multifaceted, regenerating problems. In biology, the genus Hydra (regenerating aquatic organisms) was named after this creature.

#### Did You Know?
- Unique abilities
- Famous encounters
- Slayer/defeat method
- Legacy items (weapons, artifacts)
- Scientific/modern namesakes

### 3. HEROES

#### Background & Origins
- **Birth & Lineage**: Parentage and early life
- **Divine Ancestry**: God/mortal heritage
- **Training**: How they prepared for greatness
- **Call to Adventure**: What started their journey

**Example (Heracles)**:
> Son of Zeus and the mortal Alcmene, Heracles was born with divine strength but mortal vulnerability. His name means "Glory of Hera," ironic given Hera's lifelong persecution. Driven mad by Hera, he killed his own family and was condemned to perform twelve impossible labors as penance.

#### Cultural Significance
- **Major Quests**: Primary accomplishments
- **Cultural Impact**: Influence on heroic ideal
- **Worship**: Hero cults and shrines
- **Archetypal Role**: Hero's journey pattern

#### Did You Know?
- Alternative names
- Number of labors/quests
- Weapons and tools
- Divine helpers
- Death/apotheosis

---

### 4. ITEMS (Magical Objects)

#### Background & Origins
- **Creation**: Who made it and how
- **Materials**: What it's made from
- **Purpose**: Why it was created
- **First Wielder**: Original owner

**Example (Mjolnir)**:
> Forged by the dwarven brothers Sindri and Brokkr, Mjolnir was created as part of a wager with Loki. During forging, Loki (as a fly) bit Brokkr, causing him to work the bellows irregularly, resulting in the hammer's characteristically short handle. Despite this "flaw," the hammer became the mightiest weapon in Asgard.

#### Cultural Significance
- **Powers**: What it can do
- **Famous Uses**: Key moments in myths
- **Symbolic Meaning**: What it represents
- **Modern Legacy**: Continued cultural impact

#### Did You Know?
- Special properties
- Requirements to wield
- Times it was stolen/lost
- Modern replicas or references

---

### 5. PLACES (Sacred Sites, Realms)

#### Background & Origins
- **Creation/Formation**: How place came to be
- **Geography**: Physical description
- **Inhabitants**: Who lives there
- **Access**: How to reach it

**Example (Valhalla)**:
> Created by Odin as the hall of the slain, Valhalla stands in Asgard with 540 doors, each wide enough for 800 warriors to march through abreast. Its rafters are spears, its roof is made of shields, and its benches are strewn with armor. The hall is so vast it cannot be measured.

#### Cultural Significance
- **Sacred Function**: Religious importance
- **Historical Sites**: Real-world locations
- **Mythic Events**: What happened there
- **Symbolic Meaning**: What it represents

#### Did You Know?
- Size/capacity
- Famous residents/visitors
- Treasures or features
- Prophecies about its fate

### 6. HERBS & PLANTS

#### Background & Origins
- **Sacred Association**: Deity connection
- **Origin Myth**: How plant came to be
- **Physical Properties**: Appearance and traits
- **Habitat**: Where it grows

**Example (Lotus)**:
> Sacred to both Egyptian and Hindu traditions, the lotus rises pure from muddy waters, blooming untainted. In Egyptian myth, the blue lotus emerged from the primordial waters at creation, from which the sun god Ra was born. Its ability to close at night and reopen with sunrise made it a symbol of death and rebirth.

#### Cultural Significance
- **Ritual Use**: How used in ceremonies
- **Medicinal Properties**: Healing applications
- **Symbolic Meaning**: What it represents
- **Preparation Methods**: How to use it

#### Did You Know?
- Alternative names
- Active compounds (if known)
- Historical uses
- Modern applications
- Taboos or restrictions

### 7. RITUALS & CEREMONIES

#### Background & Origins
- **Establishment**: When/who created ritual
- **Purpose**: What it accomplishes
- **Frequency**: When performed
- **Participants**: Who takes part

**Example (Eleusinian Mysteries)**:
> Established in honor of Demeter and Persephone, the Eleusinian Mysteries were ancient Greece's most famous secret rites. Initiates underwent purification, ritual drama, and mystical revelations promising blessed afterlife. Participation was open to all Greeks, both male and female, free and slave—unusual for ancient religion.

#### Cultural Significance
- **Sacred Function**: Spiritual purpose
- **Social Impact**: Community role
- **Preservation**: How tradition continued
- **Modern Practice**: Contemporary observance

#### Did You Know?
- Secret elements
- Famous initiates
- Prohibitions
- Required offerings
- Duration of ceremony

### 8. TEXTS (Sacred Writings)

#### Background & Origins
- **Authorship**: Who wrote it
- **Date**: When composed
- **Language**: Original script
- **Context**: Historical setting

**Example (Bhagavad Gita)**:
> Composed between 400 BCE and 400 CE as part of the Mahabharata epic, the Gita presents a dialogue between Prince Arjuna and Lord Krishna on the eve of the Kurukshetra War. Written in Sanskrit verse, it addresses the conflict between duty and moral conscience.

#### Cultural Significance
- **Key Teachings**: Main themes
- **Influence**: Impact on tradition
- **Preservation**: How transmitted
- **Interpretation**: Major schools

#### Did You Know?
- Number of verses/chapters
- Famous quotations
- Commentaries
- Translations
- Controversies

### 9. SYMBOLS

#### Background & Origins
- **First Appearance**: Earliest usage
- **Original Meaning**: Initial significance
- **Evolution**: How meaning changed
- **Geographic Spread**: Where used

**Example (Ankh)**:
> Appearing in Egyptian art from the Old Kingdom (c. 2700 BCE), the ankh represents eternal life. Its form combines a cross (earthly existence) with a loop (eternal soul). Gods are shown carrying ankhs, often holding them to mortals' noses to grant the "breath of life."

#### Cultural Significance
- **Sacred Use**: Religious contexts
- **Magic/Power**: Metaphysical properties
- **Modern Adoption**: Contemporary usage
- **Variations**: Different forms

#### Did You Know?
- Alternative names
- Ritual uses
- Protective powers
- Taboos
- Modern revivals

## Content Generation Guidelines

### Quality Standards
1. **Accuracy**: Cite sources for all claims
2. **Depth**: 3-5 substantial paragraphs per section
3. **Engagement**: Write to intrigue and educate
4. **Connections**: Link to related entities
5. **Sources**: Include 3-5 primary source citations

### "Did You Know?" Best Practices
- Use **bold** for key terms
- Keep facts concise (1-2 sentences)
- Mix types: historical, linguistic, symbolic, modern
- Aim for surprising or lesser-known information
- Include at least 5 facts per entity

### Related Entities
- Include 3-5 related entities minimum
- Specify relationship type
- Show entities from different categories
- Prioritize narrative connections over technical ones

### Sources
- Cite primary ancient texts when possible
- Include passage numbers/line references
- Link to corpus search pages
- Prefer primary over secondary sources
- Include modern scholarly works for context

## Implementation Notes

All topic panel data should be:
1. **Dynamically generated** from existing entity fields where possible
2. **Editable** by users for corrections/additions
3. **Versioned** to track changes over time
4. **Sourced** with citations for verification
5. **Accessible** with proper ARIA labels

The TopicPanels component automatically extracts and formats content from the entity JSON schema, generating rich, engaging content for every asset page.
