Yasakani no Magatama

Home → Spiritual Items → Ritual → Yasakani no Magatama

Object Description and Materials

The Yasakani no Magatama stands as one of the three Imperial Regalia of Japan (Sanshu no Jingi), representing benevolence and the immortal soul. Unlike the mirror and sword which are singular objects, the Yasakani no Magatama may refer to a single large magatama or a string of many magatama beads, as the term "yasakani" can mean "large jewel" or "string of jewels." This ambiguity reflects the object's mysterious nature.

Magatama are distinctive comma-shaped or curved beads that appear throughout Japanese prehistory. Their shape resembles a comma, a curved fang, or perhaps a fetal form, with the larger end typically perforated for stringing. This unique form appears to be a Japanese innovation, though similar ornaments exist in Korean archaeology, suggesting cultural exchange across the ancient maritime routes.

Archaeological magatama were crafted from various materials depending on era and status. The earliest examples from the Jomon period (14,000-300 BCE) were made from clay, bone, and stone. During the Yayoi period (300 BCE-300 CE), jade and jasper became prestige materials. By the Kofun period (300-538 CE), when the imperial regalia traditions likely crystallized, the finest magatama were carved from green jadite or nephrite jade, materials associated with spiritual power across East Asian cultures.

The Yasakani no Magatama is traditionally said to be made of brilliant green jade of exceptional quality and size. Some accounts describe it as a single enormous magatama, while others envision a strand of five hundred magatama of descending sizes. The Japanese imperial family maintains the jewel at the Imperial Palace in Tokyo, making it the only one of the three regalia kept at the palace rather than a shrine.

Green jade carried profound spiritual significance in ancient East Asia, associated with immortality, moral virtue, and cosmic vitality. Chinese traditions held that jade preserved the body after death and embodied the five cardinal virtues. Japanese adoption of jade symbolism, modified by indigenous beliefs about soul and spirit, infused the Yasakani no Magatama with layers of meaning connecting life, death, and spiritual continuity.

Origin and Creation Story

The Yasakani no Magatama's mythological origin connects to the same crisis that produced the Yata no Kagami: Amaterasu's withdrawal into the Heavenly Rock Cave. When the sun goddess hid herself, plunging the world into darkness, the eight million gods assembled to devise a way to draw her forth. Among their preparations was the creation of sacred objects to attract her attention.

The deity Tamanooya no Mikoto, whose name means "Master of the Jewel House," fashioned the Yasakani no Magatama from celestial materials. As a divine jeweler, Tamanooya possessed the knowledge and skill to create ornaments of supreme spiritual power. The magatama he created for this cosmic crisis represented his finest work, imbued with the essence of divine virtue.

Along with the Yata no Kagami (mirror), the magatama was hung upon a sakaki tree before the cave entrance as part of the ritual display designed to lure Amaterasu. The jewel's spiritual radiance complemented the mirror's reflective brightness, creating an ensemble of sacred objects whose power and beauty would appeal to the sun goddess's divine nature.

When Amaterasu was successfully drawn from the cave and light was restored to the world, the magatama, along with the mirror and the sword Kusanagi, became the most sacred objects in existence. They had participated in the restoration of cosmic order, demonstrating their power to influence divine will and maintain the harmony of the world.

At the divine descent of Ninigi-no-Mikoto to establish rule over the terrestrial realm, Amaterasu entrusted him with the three regalia. The magatama, representing benevolence, would remind rulers of their duty to govern with compassion and care for their people. This virtue, alongside the mirror's wisdom and the sword's valor, comprised the essential qualities of righteous sovereignty.

Some variants of the mythology describe the magatama as having an even older origin, existing before the cave incident and brought forth from the primordial realm by earlier generations of deities. This suggests the jewel's power is as old as the cosmos itself, predating even the crisis that demonstrated its efficacy.

Ritual Uses and Practices

The Yasakani no Magatama plays crucial roles in imperial succession ceremonies, most importantly the Daijosai, the great thanksgiving ceremony performed by each new emperor. During this ceremony, conducted shortly after accession, the emperor offers newly harvested rice to the kami and partakes of it himself, ritually affirming his connection to the divine order. The jewel's presence, along with the other regalia, legitimizes the succession.

Unlike the mirror at Ise and the sword at Atsuta Shrine, the magatama is kept at the Imperial Palace in Tokyo, in the Kenji-no-ma (Sword and Jewel Room). The decision to keep this regalia item close to the emperor reflects its special significance as the emblem of benevolent rule and spiritual authority. The emperor's proximity to the jewel symbolizes his ongoing responsibility to embody its virtues.

During the Kenji-to-Shokei-no-gi (Ceremony of Inheriting the Sword and Jewel), performed at the moment of succession, the new emperor receives the magatama and sword (or their symbolic representations) from attendants, physically assuming possession of the imperial authority they represent. This ceremony, though modified in modern times, maintains ancient protocols reflecting Shinto traditions.

Shinto ceremonies throughout Japan employ magatama symbolism, even when using replicas or representations rather than the imperial original. Shrine rituals may incorporate magatama as offerings, as decorative elements in sacred regalia, or as symbols in ritual performance. The curved jewel's shape appears in shrine architecture, priest vestments, and religious art.

Personal worship and protection often involve magatama worn as jewelry or kept as amulets. While these everyday magatama make no claim to the Yasakani no Magatama's sacred status, they draw upon the same symbolic tradition, offering protection and spiritual connection to those who wear or carry them. Modern practitioners see them as links to ancient Japanese spirituality.

Kagura performances and Shinto festivals sometimes incorporate magatama in dancers' costumes or ritual props, connecting contemporary ceremony to the mythological events at the Heavenly Rock Cave. These performances maintain living memory of the magatama's role in restoring cosmic order and establishing the foundation for imperial rule.

Symbolism and Meaning

The Yasakani no Magatama primarily symbolizes benevolence (jin), one of the three essential virtues of rulership alongside wisdom (chi, represented by the mirror) and courage (yu, represented by the sword). Benevolence encompasses compassion, kindness, and the ruler's duty to care for the welfare of the people. A sovereign without benevolence becomes a tyrant, no matter how wise or brave.

The magatama's distinctive curved shape has inspired numerous interpretations. Some see it as representing the soul's comma-like form, suggesting the jewel embodies spiritual essence itself. Others interpret the shape as a fetal form, connecting it to birth, renewal, and life's continuity. The curve might also represent the moon in its crescent phase, linking the jewel to lunar cycles and feminine principles.

In Shinto thought, the magatama represents the mitama, the spirit or soul believed to animate living beings and persist after death. The jewel's association with this concept made magatama important grave goods in ancient Japan, placed with the deceased to accompany and protect the soul. The Yasakani no Magatama thus connects to beliefs about spiritual immortality and ancestral continuity.

The jade material itself carries symbolic weight. Jade's toughness despite its appearance suggests hidden strength, while its coolness to touch implies purity and temperance. Green jade particularly evokes spring, growth, and vitality, the renewing forces of nature that benevolent rule should cultivate and protect. The jewel reminds rulers that their primary duty is nurturing life.

Within the trinity of regalia, the magatama occupies a mediating position between the mirror's passive receptivity and the sword's active force. Benevolence synthesizes contemplation and action, using wisdom to guide righteous force. This balanced position makes the magatama essential to the regalia's complete symbolic system.

Contemporary interpretations sometimes connect the magatama to Japanese concepts of harmony (wa), the balanced state where diverse elements coexist productively. The jewel's smooth, enclosed form, without sharp edges or aggressive projections, embodies harmony's ideal of strength through integration rather than conflict.

Associated Deities and Figures

Tamanooya no Mikoto, the divine jeweler, created the Yasakani no Magatama for the ritual to draw Amaterasu from her cave. His role establishes the jewel's celestial origin and the sacred nature of jewel crafting. Shrines honoring Tamanooya celebrate both the deity and the craft traditions he represents, connecting artisanship to divine service.

Amaterasu Omikami, the sun goddess, received the magatama among the offerings presented before her cave and later entrusted it to her grandson Ninigi for his descent to earth. While less directly identified with the jewel than with the mirror (which she called her spirit), Amaterasu's approval and transmission established the magatama's supreme sacred status.

Ninigi-no-Mikoto, Amaterasu's grandson, received the three regalia when he descended from the High Plain of Heaven to establish divine rule over the Japanese islands. The magatama he received symbolized his mandate to rule benevolently, caring for the land and people as Amaterasu cared for all creation. His descendants, the imperial line, inherit this responsibility.

The Japanese Emperor maintains an ongoing spiritual relationship with the magatama as its guardian and the embodiment of the benevolence it represents. Throughout Japanese history, possession of the regalia signified legitimate sovereignty. Even today, when the emperor's role is largely ceremonial, the jewel remains in palace custody as a symbol of the imperial institution.

Empress Jingu (mythological, traditionally dated 3rd century CE) reportedly used magatama in her military campaign to Korea, demonstrating the jewel's protective and authorizing power. Whether historical or legendary, her story connects magatama to successful leadership and divine protection during perilous undertakings.

Various emperors throughout history maintained special relationships with the regalia during their reigns. Emperor Temmu (673-686 CE) emphasized the regalia's significance in establishing his legitimacy after the Jinshin War. Later emperors during succession disputes would emphasize their possession of the true regalia, including the magatama.

Historical Accounts and Records

The Kojiki (712 CE) and Nihon Shoki (720 CE), Japan's oldest chronicles, provide the foundational accounts of the Yasakani no Magatama's mythological origin. These texts, compiled from oral traditions and earlier records, established the canonical narratives connecting the jewel to Amaterasu and the imperial line. Their accounts, though mythological, reflect beliefs already ancient when recorded.

Archaeological evidence demonstrates that magatama production began in the Jomon period (14,000-300 BCE) and intensified during the Yayoi (300 BCE-300 CE) and Kofun (300-538 CE) periods. Excavations of Kofun-period tombs have yielded thousands of magatama, often made of green jade, placed with elite burials. These finds confirm the jewels' importance as symbols of status and spiritual power.

Chinese historical records, including the Wei Zhi (3rd century CE), mention that Japanese emissaries presented magatama as gifts, indicating these objects carried diplomatic significance. Chinese observers noted the Japanese reverence for curved jade objects, providing external documentation of magatama traditions predating the compiled Japanese chronicles.

Throughout Japanese history, the regalia played crucial roles in succession disputes. During the Southern and Northern Courts period (1336-1392), both courts claimed possession of the authentic regalia. Emperor Go-Daigo's Southern Court held what it maintained were the true objects, while the Northern Court governed with what it called authentic regalia. This dispute was only resolved when the Southern Court surrendered its regalia in 1392.

The Yasakani no Magatama reportedly escaped the 1185 naval Battle of Dan-no-ura, when the Kusanagi sword was lost at sea along with the child Emperor Antoku. According to tradition, the magatama was recovered from the waves while the sword sank with the emperor. This dramatic event established different histories for each regalia item.

Modern scholars debate the regalia's actual antiquity and authenticity. Some argue the objects have been replaced or restored multiple times over centuries, while others maintain substantial continuity with ancient originals. The secretive nature of the regalia, hidden from public view, prevents definitive scholarly assessment.

Modern Use and Replicas

The Yasakani no Magatama remains in the Imperial Palace, where it continues to function in succession ceremonies and represent imperial authority. Though postwar constitutional changes redefined the emperor's role, the magatama and other regalia maintain their traditional significance as symbols of the state and continuity of the imperial institution.

The enthronement of Emperor Naruhito in 2019 included traditional ceremonies involving the regalia, demonstrating their continued ceremonial importance. While adapted to modern sensibilities and constitutional requirements, these ceremonies maintained connections to ancient Shinto traditions surrounding the magatama and its sister objects.

Contemporary craftspeople produce magatama for shrine use, personal worship, and jewelry. These modern magatama, made from jade, glass, crystal, or other materials, allow broad participation in traditions associated with the imperial original. Shinto practitioners and spiritual seekers wear magatama for protection, spiritual connection, and cultural identity.

Museums throughout Japan display archaeological magatama from prehistoric and historic periods, providing context for understanding the imperial jewel's significance. These collections trace magatama evolution from simple clay beads to elaborate jade ornaments, illuminating the tradition that produced the Yasakani no Magatama.

The magatama appears frequently in Japanese popular culture, from manga and anime to video games. These representations often emphasize the jewel's mysterious powers and connection to ancient tradition, introducing magatama symbolism to new generations even when taking creative liberties with historical and mythological details.

Academic study of magatama encompasses archaeology, religious studies, and Japanese history. Scholars examine production techniques, distribution patterns, and symbolic evolution, contributing to understanding of ancient Japanese society and spirituality. The imperial magatama, though inaccessible, inspires ongoing research into the traditions it represents.

Related Objects and Practices

The Yata no Kagami (Eight-Span Mirror) forms the second of the Imperial Regalia, representing wisdom. Together, the mirror's wisdom and the magatama's benevolence encompass the contemplative virtues essential to righteous rule. The mirror resides at Ise Grand Shrine, geographically separated from the magatama but eternally linked in symbolic function.

The Kusanagi no Tsurugi (Grass-Cutting Sword) completes the regalia trinity, representing valor. Originally discovered by Susanoo in the body of the serpent Yamata no Orochi, this sword was later lost at sea but subsequently replaced or recovered according to different traditions. It is enshrined at Atsuta Shrine in Nagoya.

Korean magatama (called gogok or kokkok in Korean) demonstrate cultural connections across ancient East Asia. Korean examples, found in similar archaeological contexts, suggest either shared origins or cultural exchange. Studying both traditions illuminates the broader East Asian significance of curved jade ornaments.

Chinese bi discs and cong tubes represent related jade traditions in ancient China, where jade held comparable spiritual significance. While differently shaped, these Chinese jade objects served analogous functions as status symbols, grave goods, and spiritual implements. Cross-cultural jade traditions informed Japanese developments.

Tamadare, strings of magatama worn as necklaces or attached to ceremonial objects, extend magatama symbolism into ritual costume and regalia. Priests performing Shinto ceremonies may wear tamadare as part of their ceremonial dress, connecting their ritual role to the ancient traditions of jewel symbolism.

Contemporary jade and crystal jewelry drawing on magatama traditions offers practitioners opportunities for personal spiritual connection. These modern interpretations, while distinct from ancient ritual objects, maintain the association between curved jewels and spiritual protection, making accessible a tradition otherwise restricted to imperial and shrine contexts.
