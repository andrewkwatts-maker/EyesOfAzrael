Yata no Kagami

Home → Spiritual Items → Ritual → Yata no Kagami

Object Description and Materials

The Yata no Kagami, literally the "Eight-Span Mirror" or "Eight Hand-Widths Mirror," stands as one of the three Imperial Regalia of Japan (Sanshu no Jingi), sacred objects that have symbolized the legitimacy and divine authority of the Japanese imperial line since mythological times. As with the other regalia, the mirror's actual appearance remains unknown to the general public, as it has been hidden from view for centuries and is housed in the Inner Shrine (Naiku) of Ise Grand Shrine.

Historical and archaeological evidence from similar mirrors of the Yayoi and Kofun periods suggests that the original Yata no Kagami was likely a bronze mirror of Chinese or Korean influence, or a Japanese adaptation of continental mirror-making techniques. These ancient mirrors were typically circular with a polished reflective surface on one side and decorative patterns on the reverse. The "eight-span" measurement suggests a diameter of approximately 46 centimeters (18 inches), making it significantly larger than typical ritual mirrors.

The mirror's name contains numerical significance beyond simple measurement. Eight is a sacred number in Japanese tradition, associated with abundance and expansion, as the character for eight (å…«) widens as it descends, suggesting growth and prosperity. The mirror's size thus carries symbolic weight, representing not merely physical dimensions but expansive wisdom and cosmic scope.

Traditional bronze mirrors from this era were made using high-tin bronze alloys that could be polished to remarkable reflectivity. The casting process involved pouring molten bronze into stone or clay molds, with the decorative back cast in relief while the front surface was ground and polished smooth. Such mirrors required regular repolishing to maintain their reflective quality, making their care itself a ritual act.

The mirror is said to be enshrined within multiple protective cases and containers, wrapped in silk and housed in the Naiku's innermost sanctuary. The shrine buildings themselves are rebuilt every twenty years in the Shikinen Sengu ceremony, with the mirror ceremonially transferred to the new structure. No photographs or detailed descriptions of the mirror exist from modern times, and even the priests who handle it during transfers do so with the object wrapped and concealed.

Origin and Creation Story

The Yata no Kagami's mythological origin is inextricably linked to one of Japanese mythology's most important narratives: the withdrawal of Amaterasu Omikami, the sun goddess, into the Ama-no-Iwato (Heavenly Rock Cave). According to the Kojiki and Nihon Shoki, Japan's oldest historical chronicles, Amaterasu hid in the cave after her brother Susanoo's violent and destructive behavior in her realm, plunging the world into darkness.

Desperate to restore light to the world, the eight million gods gathered in council. The god of wisdom, Omoikane, devised a plan to lure Amaterasu from the cave. The smith god Ishikoridome no Mikoto forged the Yata no Kagami from materials gathered from heaven itself, making a mirror of divine quality capable of reflecting the light of the sun goddess. This celestial craftsmanship ensured the mirror's eternal purity and power.

The gods hung the mirror on a sakaki tree (Cleyera japonica), along with the Yasakani no Magatama (jewel) and cloth offerings, positioning them before the cave's entrance. The goddess Ame-no-Uzume performed an ecstatic, provocative dance that made the gathered gods laugh uproariously. Curious about the commotion, Amaterasu asked what was happening, and was told that a goddess more radiant than herself had appeared.

Intrigued, Amaterasu opened the cave slightly to look. At that moment, she saw her own reflection in the Yata no Kagami, brilliant and divine, and was drawn out further by her own radiance. The strong god Ame-no-Tajikarao then pulled her fully from the cave, and a shimenawa (sacred rope) was placed across the entrance to prevent her return. Light was restored to the world, and the mirror that had reflected her glory became one of the most sacred objects in existence.

When Amaterasu's grandson Ninigi-no-Mikoto descended to earth to establish divine rule over Japan, Amaterasu entrusted him with the three regalia, including the Yata no Kagami. She instructed him to revere the mirror as her own spirit, saying, "When you look upon this mirror, let it be as looking upon me." This instruction established the mirror as not merely a sacred object but a living embodiment of the sun goddess herself.

Ritual Uses and Practices

The Yata no Kagami serves as the central sacred object of Ise Grand Shrine, the holiest site in Shinto. The mirror's presence consecrates the Inner Shrine (Naiku), dedicated to Amaterasu, making it the spiritual center of Japan. Priests conduct daily offerings and rituals before the mirror, though they never directly view it, performing rites before its multiple layers of protective encasements.

The Shikinen Sengu, the ceremony of rebuilding Ise Shrine every twenty years, represents the most significant ritual involving the mirror. This practice, continuing for over 1,300 years, involves constructing entirely new shrine buildings on an adjacent plot, then ceremonially transferring the sacred objects, including the mirror, to the new structures in the dead of night. This cycle of renewal embodies Shinto concepts of purity, renewal, and the eternal present.

Imperial accession ceremonies (Daijosai) have traditionally involved rituals acknowledging the mirror's authority. While the mirror itself remains at Ise, the emperor's legitimacy derives in part from his connection to it. Modern emperors still perform secret rituals during accession that involve the regalia, though the specific nature of these ceremonies remains undisclosed.

In broader Shinto practice, mirrors (kagami) serve as shintai, vessels or "god-bodies" that enshrine kami (divine spirits) in shrines throughout Japan. This practice directly derives from the Yata no Kagami's role as the embodiment of Amaterasu. Worshippers making offerings before shrine mirrors address the kami believed to reside within or be represented by the mirror.

The mirror's symbolic presence extends to household worship. Many Japanese homes maintain kamidana (household shrines) that include small mirrors representing Amaterasu's presence. Morning offerings of rice, water, and salt before these mirrors connect ordinary domestic life to the central myth and the imperial divine heritage.

Annual festivals at Ise and associated shrines commemorate events from the Amaterasu mythology, including ceremonies honoring the restoration of light to the world. These celebrations acknowledge the mirror's role in drawing forth the sun goddess and maintaining cosmic order. Kagura dances performed at these festivals often reenact elements of the original cave narrative.

Symbolism and Meaning

The Yata no Kagami primarily symbolizes truth and wisdom. Its reflective surface shows things as they truly are, without deception or distortion. In the foundational myth, it revealed Amaterasu's own divine nature to herself, suggesting that the mirror's truth includes self-knowledge and recognition of one's authentic nature. Wisdom, in this context, means seeing clearly, both outward reality and inner truth.

The mirror represents Amaterasu herself, her light, warmth, and life-giving power. As Amaterasu instructed, looking upon the mirror is equivalent to looking upon her. This identification makes the mirror more than a symbol; it is a real presence, a point where the divine touches the material world. The mirror thus embodies the Shinto concept of kami residing in physical objects.

Among the three regalia, the mirror traditionally represents wisdom, while the sword (Kusanagi no Tsurugi) represents valor and the jewel (Yasakani no Magatama) represents benevolence. These three virtues together comprise the ideal qualities of rulership, with wisdom given primacy through the mirror's identification with the supreme deity. A ruler who sees clearly can govern justly.

The mirror symbolizes purity, one of Shinto's central concerns. Its polished surface, properly maintained, remains spotless and clear. Ritual purification practices in Shinto aim to achieve a similar clarity of spirit, removing the pollutions and obscurations that cloud perception and disrupt harmony with kami. The mirror serves as an ideal toward which practitioners aspire.

In psychological interpretation, the mirror represents self-reflection and consciousness. Amaterasu, hidden in darkness and separation, emerged when she saw herself reflected. The mirror draws out the divine light that exists within, suggesting that spiritual awakening involves recognizing one's own true nature. This interpretation resonates with Buddhist influences on Japanese spirituality.

The mirror also symbolizes the bond between heaven and earth, between kami and humanity. As a divine object brought from heaven and enshrined on earth, it serves as a connecting point between realms. The emperor's relationship to the mirror represents humanity's relationship to the divine: respectful, receptive, and responsive.

Associated Deities and Figures

Amaterasu Omikami, the sun goddess and supreme deity of Shinto, stands as the mirror's primary divine association. As the mirror was created to draw her from hiding and later given as her spirit embodiment, Amaterasu and the mirror are essentially identified. Her qualities of light, life, purity, and cosmic order are the mirror's qualities. Worship of the mirror is worship of Amaterasu.

Ishikoridome no Mikoto, the divine smith, created the Yata no Kagami using celestial materials and techniques. This deity's craftsmanship established the tradition of sacred mirror-making that continues in Shinto practice. Ishikoridome is honored in the mirror-making tradition and in shrines dedicated to craftwork and creation.

Ame-no-Uzume, the goddess whose dance drew Amaterasu toward the cave entrance where the mirror waited, plays a crucial role in the mirror's mythology. Her performance represents the power of art, joy, and sacred performance to restore cosmic order. The kagura dances performed at Shinto festivals descend from her original dance.

Ninigi-no-Mikoto, Amaterasu's grandson, received the mirror along with the other regalia when he descended to establish divine rule on earth. His descent represents the origin of the imperial line and the transfer of divine authority from heaven to the terrestrial realm. The mirror he received continues to legitimize his descendants.

The Japanese Emperor, as direct descendant of Ninigi and thus of Amaterasu, maintains an ongoing relationship with the mirror. Though postwar constitutional changes removed the emperor's divine status, the imperial family continues to observe Shinto ceremonies involving the regalia. The emperor alone has formal authority over the mirror.

Susanoo, whose destructive behavior drove Amaterasu into hiding, serves as a necessary antagonist in the mirror's origin story. Without his transgressions creating the crisis, there would have been no need for the mirror's creation. His role illustrates how chaos and conflict can paradoxically give rise to sacred objects and renewed order.

Historical Accounts and Records

The Kojiki (Records of Ancient Matters, 712 CE) provides the earliest complete account of the Yata no Kagami's mythological origin. This text, compiled by O no Yasumaro from oral traditions, established the canonical version of the Amaterasu cave story and the mirror's creation. The Kojiki's account remains foundational for understanding the mirror's significance.

The Nihon Shoki (Chronicles of Japan, 720 CE) offers parallel accounts with some variations, reflecting different oral traditions compiled into the official history. This text provided additional details about the mirror's transmission through the imperial line and its placement at Ise Shrine. Discrepancies between the texts sparked centuries of scholarly commentary.

Historical records trace the mirror's movement from the imperial palace to its current location at Ise Shrine during the reign of Emperor Suinin (traditionally dated 29 BCE-70 CE, though likely later). Princess Yamatohime-no-mikoto, the emperor's daughter, is credited with establishing the shrine at Ise after the goddess Amaterasu indicated this as her desired dwelling place.

The mirror was reportedly damaged in a palace fire in 1040 CE, leading to complex theological debates about whether the damaged object retained its sacred power. Some traditions hold that the current mirror is a replica created after this incident, while others maintain it is the restored original. This uncertainty has never been officially resolved.

Throughout Japanese history, possession or control of the regalia signified legitimate imperial authority. During periods of conflict, competing courts sometimes claimed to possess the authentic regalia. The Southern Court during the Nanboku-cho period (1336-1392) held what it claimed were the true regalia, including the mirror, while the Northern Court governed from Kyoto.

Modern academic scholarship has attempted to reconstruct the regalia's history using available textual and archaeological evidence. While the myths clearly postdate the historical practices they describe, mirrors played important roles in Yayoi and Kofun period religion (300 BCE-538 CE), suggesting the traditions have ancient roots even if the specific narratives were formulated later.

Modern Use and Replicas

The Yata no Kagami remains enshrined at Ise Grand Shrine, where it continues to receive daily offerings and veneration. While the general public cannot view the mirror, they can visit Ise and worship at the shrine, connecting to Amaterasu through the mirror's presence. Millions of Japanese visit Ise annually, making it the most visited shrine in Japan.

A replica of the mirror is maintained at the Imperial Palace in Tokyo for use in accession and other imperial ceremonies. This practice of maintaining palace copies of the regalia began in antiquity, ensuring that emperors could perform necessary rituals even when the originals remained at their shrine locations. The palace replica carries ceremonial significance even if not the original's full sacred weight.

Shinto shrines throughout Japan enshrine mirrors as shintai, following the model established by the Yata no Kagami. These mirrors serve as focal points for worship and as vessels for kami presence. Visitors to virtually any Shinto shrine will see mirrors in the worship halls, maintaining the connection between local practice and the central Amaterasu tradition.

Contemporary craftspeople continue to make ritual mirrors in traditional styles for shrine use and household worship. These mirrors, though not claiming the original's power, maintain the technical and aesthetic traditions of sacred mirror-making. Some artisans specialize in reproducing ancient mirror styles for museums, researchers, and practitioners.

The Yata no Kagami appears extensively in Japanese popular culture, from manga and anime to video games and literature. These representations often take creative liberties with the object's appearance and powers, imagining what the hidden mirror might look like and attributing various magical abilities to it. This cultural engagement keeps the mythology alive for new generations.

Academic study of the mirror continues in religious studies, Japanese history, and archaeology. Scholars examine the mirror's mythological functions, its role in legitimating political authority, and its connections to broader East Asian mirror traditions. Archaeological discoveries of ancient mirrors provide comparative material for understanding what the original might have looked like.

Related Objects and Practices

The Yasakani no Magatama (curved jewel) forms another third of the Imperial Regalia, traditionally representing benevolence. This comma-shaped jewel, also given to Ninigi during his descent, complements the mirror's wisdom with the virtue of compassion. Together they represent two essential qualities of divine and imperial authority.

The Kusanagi no Tsurugi (Grass-Cutting Sword), the third of the regalia, represents valor. Originally named Ama-no-Murakumo-no-Tsurugi (Sword of the Gathering Clouds of Heaven), it was discovered by Susanoo in the body of the eight-headed serpent Yamata no Orochi. The three regalia together embody the complete virtues required for righteous rule.

Bronze mirrors from the Yayoi and Kofun periods provide archaeological context for understanding the Yata no Kagami's likely appearance. Many of these mirrors were Chinese imports or Japanese copies of Chinese designs, featuring intricate geometric and animal patterns on their backs. Museums throughout Japan display examples of these ancient mirrors.

Miko, shrine maidens who assist in Shinto ceremonies, perform kagura dances that descend from Ame-no-Uzume's original performance before the Heavenly Rock Cave. These dances, performed at shrines throughout Japan, maintain the ritual tradition that originally accompanied the mirror's revelation to Amaterasu.

The shimenawa, sacred rope marking boundaries between ordinary and sacred space, derives from the rope placed across the cave to prevent Amaterasu's return to hiding. These ropes appear at shrines, sacred trees, and other locations throughout Japan, each invoking the original victory over darkness.

Household kamidana (god-shelves) throughout Japan often include small mirrors representing Amaterasu's presence. This practice extends the logic of the Yata no Kagami into domestic worship, allowing ordinary households to maintain their own connection to the sun goddess through mirror symbolism.
